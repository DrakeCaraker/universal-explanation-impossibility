\begin{abstract}
%%=======================================================================
When a system is underspecified---when multiple configurations produce the
same observable output---any explanation faces a fundamental tradeoff.
We prove a universal impossibility: no explanation can simultaneously be
faithful (consistent with the system's native explanation), stable
(invariant across observationally equivalent configurations), and decisive
(committing to every distinction the native explanation makes).
This impossibility holds whenever the \emph{Rashomon property} obtains:
equivalent configurations with incompatible explanations.
We prove the Rashomon property is both necessary and sufficient---the exact
boundary between possibility and impossibility.
The theorem unifies nine apparently unrelated impossibility results:
feature attribution under collinearity, SHAP instability under model
multiplicity, fairness metric incompatibility, model selection instability,
causal discovery ambiguity within Markov equivalence classes, overparameterized
neural network explanation instability, GradCAM saliency map instability,
token citation instability, and circuit non-uniqueness in mechanistic
interpretability.
For causal discovery, the Rashomon property is derived from first principles
(Markov equivalence).
The proof is a tight four-step chain---decisiveness, stability, faithfulness,
contradiction---and Lean-verified tightness theorems confirm each pair of
properties is achievable.
Empirical experiments on seven instances confirm severe instability among
functionally equivalent models: 19.9\% attention argmax flip rate
(under full retraining; 60\% under weight perturbation), 23.5\%
counterfactual direction flip rate, 0.90 concept direction instability,
80\% model selection flip rate, 9.6\% GradCAM peak flip rate, and 34.5\%
token citation flip rate.
The resolution is uniform: $G$-invariant aggregation over equivalent
configurations restores faithfulness-in-expectation and stability at
the cost of decisiveness, and is proved Pareto-optimal among stable methods.
The impossibility is ubiquitous: any system with strictly more parameters
than observational constraints admits a Rashomon set of positive measure.
The framework is mechanically verified in Lean~4 (75~files, 351~theorems,
72~axioms, 0~\texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%=======================================================================

When multiple configurations of a system produce identical observable output,
any attempt to explain the system's behavior faces a fundamental constraint.
This is not a peculiarity of machine learning.
In philosophy of science, Quine \citep{quine1951dogmas} and
Duhem \citep{duhem1906aim} argued that empirical evidence underdetermines
theory choice: no finite body of observations uniquely selects a theory from
among empirically adequate alternatives.
In neuroscience, Marder and Goaillard \citep{marder2006variability}
documented that nervous systems with identical functional output are built from
qualitatively different circuit configurations---a phenomenon called
\emph{degeneracy} \citep{edelman2001degeneracy}.
In statistics and machine learning, the Rashomon effect
\citep{breiman2001random,rudin2024amazing} describes the multiplicity of
near-optimal models with divergent internal structure.
In economics, Manski \citep{manski2003partial} developed partial
identification theory because observational data generically fail to pin down
a unique model.
This paper proves that underdetermination entails a universal impossibility:
no explanation can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and decisive.

Machine learning is the most developed application domain for this result,
and the domain where the practical consequences are most immediate.
A data scientist trains a gradient-boosted model for credit risk, runs \SHAP{},
and finds that \emph{debt-to-income ratio} is the top feature.  She retrains
from a different random seed---same data, same hyperparameters, same test
accuracy---and the top feature flips to \emph{credit utilization}
\citep{krishna2022disagreement}.  An NLP engineer inspects attention weights in
a sentiment classifier, retrains, and the most-attended token changes from
``not'' to ``terrible'' \citep{jainwallace2019}.
None of these are software bugs---they are manifestations of a structural
impossibility.
Regulators now demand explanations that are faithful, stable, and decisive
simultaneously: the EU AI Act requires ``meaningful explanations'' for
high-risk systems \citep{euaiact2024}, and SR~11-7 requires
``developmental evidence'' documenting model decisions
\citep{occ2011sr117}.  Yet a growing body of theoretical work shows that the
promise is mathematically impossible to keep---at least in specific domains.
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that no feature attribution satisfying
the Shapley axioms is stable under collinearity.
\citet{huang2024failings} show that SHAP values can be arbitrarily
misleading.  \citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent}
independently prove that common fairness metrics cannot simultaneously be
satisfied.  \citet{verma1991equivalence} establish that observationally
equivalent causal structures are indistinguishable from data alone.

Each of these impossibilities was proved independently, in domain-specific
language, under domain-specific assumptions.  The attribution impossibility
assumes collinearity among features; Chouldechova's impossibility assumes
unequal base rates across groups; Markov equivalence assumes faithfulness of
the directed acyclic graph.  No one has asked: is there \emph{one} structural
cause?  Is the impossibility intrinsic to underspecification itself, rather
than to any particular domain?

In this paper we answer affirmatively.  We prove a \emph{Universal Explanation
Impossibility}: no explanation of an underspecified system can simultaneously
be faithful, stable, and decisive.  The theorem requires only the
\emph{Rashomon property}---the existence of equivalent hypotheses that
honestly report incompatible explanations---and the proof is four lines.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A universal impossibility theorem}
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}; see Figure~\ref{fig:trilemma}) that
    unifies nine apparently unrelated impossibility results under a single
    proof requiring only the Rashomon property.
  \item \textbf{Nine instances}: feature attribution
    \citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}, attention maps
    \citep{jainwallace2019}, counterfactual explanations, concept probes,
    causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, model selection
    \citep{rudin2024amazing}, GradCAM saliency maps
    \citep{selvaraju2017gradcam}, token citation explanations
    \citep{turpin2024language}, and \textbf{mechanistic interpretability}
    \citep{meloux2025everything}---each shown to be a special case of the
    abstract framework.
  \item \textbf{Mechanistic interpretability impossibility}: we prove for the
    first time that no circuit explanation can simultaneously be faithful,
    stable, and decisive, grounding the trilemma in the circuit non-uniqueness
    demonstrated by \citet{meloux2025everything} (85 valid circuits for a
    single task).
  \item \textbf{Empirical validation} for seven of the nine instances
    (attribution, attention, counterfactual, concept probes, model selection,
    GradCAM, token citation), confirming explanation instability on
    standard benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{A uniform resolution}: $G$-invariant aggregation restores
    faithfulness and stability at the cost of decisiveness.  \DASH{} for
    feature attribution, CPDAGs for causal discovery, and ensemble probes
    for concepts are all instances of this resolution.
  \item \textbf{Derived Rashomon for causal discovery}: for the causal
    discovery instance, we derive the Rashomon property from first
    principles (Markov equivalence), converting it from an axiom to a
    theorem.
  \item \textbf{Necessity}: we prove the Rashomon property is necessary
    and sufficient: it is the exact boundary between possibility and
    impossibility.
  \item \textbf{Ubiquity}: the impossibility is generic whenever the
    parameter dimension exceeds the observable dimension, covering
    virtually all modern overparameterized models.
  \item \textbf{Mechanical verification} in Lean~4
    \citep{demoura2021lean4}: 75~files, 351~theorems, 72~axioms,
    0~\texttt{sorry}.
\end{enumerate}

This paper generalizes the attribution impossibility of our companion work
\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility} from feature attribution to arbitrary
explanation systems.  The companion paper provides quantitative depth
(divergence rates, ensemble size bounds, \DASH{} optimality) for the
attribution instance; this paper supplies the abstract framework, six
additional instances (including mechanistic interpretability), and the
ubiquity result.  The two papers share a
Lean codebase but are self-contained.

Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and open questions.

\section{The Abstract Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%%=======================================================================

We introduce a minimal abstract framework sufficient to state and prove the
Universal Explanation Impossibility.  All nine instances in
Section~\ref{sec:instances} are special cases.
The framework aligns exactly with the Lean~4 formalization in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}: every definition and theorem below has a
machine-checked counterpart.

\subsection{Explanation Systems}

\begin{definition}[Explanation System]
\label{def:explanation-system}
An \emph{explanation system} is a tuple
$\mathcal{S} = (\Params, \sH, Y, \mathsf{observe}, \mathsf{explain}, \incomp)$ where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Params$ is a \emph{configuration space} (the set of possible
    configurations, e.g., random seeds, hyperparameter settings);
  \item $\sH$ is an \emph{explanation space} (the set of possible explanations);
  \item $Y$ is an \emph{observable space} (e.g., input--output functions,
    test-set predictions);
  \item $\mathsf{observe}: \Params \to Y$ is the \emph{observation map}, assigning
    to each configuration its observable behavior;
  \item $\mathsf{explain}: \Params \to \sH$ is the system's \emph{native
    explanation map}, assigning to each configuration the explanation that the
    system itself ``honestly reports''; and
  \item $\incomp\;\subseteq \sH \times \sH$ is an \emph{incompatibility relation}
    on explanations satisfying \emph{irreflexivity}:
    $\neg\,\incomp(h, h)$ for every $h \in \sH$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Irreflexivity of~$\incomp$ is a structural requirement: no explanation
contradicts itself.  This is the \texttt{incompatible\_irrefl} field of the
Lean structure and is used in the tightness proof
(Section~\ref{sec:tightness}).

An \emph{explanation map} $E : \Params \to \sH$ is a procedure that a
practitioner \emph{chooses} (e.g., SHAP, LIME, a fixed causal orientation
rule).  The native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ records what the
configuration \emph{actually implies}; $E(\theta)$ is what the chosen method
reports.

\subsection{The Three Desiderata}

\begin{definition}[Faithful]
\label{def:faithful}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{faithful} if $E(\theta)$ never contradicts the
system's native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\quad
  \neg\,\incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta)\bigr).
\]
That is, $E(\theta)$ may be less specific than $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ but
never disagrees with it.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Stable]
\label{def:stable}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{stable} if equivalent configurations receive
the same explanation:
\[
  \forall\,\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params,\quad
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Decisive]
\label{def:decisive}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{decisive} if $E(\theta)$ inherits every
incompatibility of the native explanation: whatever
$\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ rules out, $E(\theta)$ also rules out.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\;\forall\,h \in \sH,\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta),\, h\bigr)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  \incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\, h\bigr).
\]
$E$ is at least as committal as $\mathsf{explain}$.
\end{definition}

In the feature attribution instance (Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}),
faithfulness means the chosen method never contradicts the model's own SHAP
values, stability means consistency across retraining seeds, and decisiveness
means that every distinction made by the native attribution is preserved.

\subsection{The Rashomon Property}

The key structural condition linking underspecification to explanation
impossibility is the following.

\begin{definition}[Rashomon Property]
\label{def:rashomon}
An explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the \emph{Rashomon property} if there
exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params$ with
\[
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta_2)\bigr).
\]
That is, two configurations that are observationally equivalent honestly report
incompatible explanations.
\end{definition}

The Rashomon property is the formal statement of underspecification at the
explanation level: the data do not uniquely determine the explanation, even in
principle.  It generalizes the Rashomon effect of
\citet{rudin2024amazing,fisher2019models} from model selection to arbitrary
explanation targets.

\subsection{Main Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Universal Explanation Impossibility]
\label{thm:universal}
If an explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the Rashomon property, then no
explanation map $E$ can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and decisive.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the Rashomon property, there exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ and
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
Suppose for contradiction that $E$ is faithful, stable, and decisive.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness} at $\theta_1$:
    $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$
    implies
    $\incomp(E(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Stability}:
    $\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$
    implies $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, so
    $\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness} at $\theta_2$:
    $\neg\,\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item Steps 2 and 3 contradict.  \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof uses all three properties in a tight chain:
decisive $\to$ stable $\to$ faithful $\to$ contradiction.
This mirrors the Lean proof exactly (\texttt{explanation\_impossibility} in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The theorem is deliberately parameter-free: it requires no regularity
conditions on $\sH$, no metric structure on $Y$, and no probabilistic
assumptions.  All quantitative depth (divergence rates, sample complexity,
ensemble sizes) appears in the instance-specific sections below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Tightness}
\label{sec:tightness}

The impossibility is tight: every pair of properties is simultaneously
achievable.  Three Lean-verified witnesses establish this
(\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_decisive},
\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_stable},
\texttt{tightness\_stable\_decisive} in \texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).

\begin{proposition}[Tightness of the Universal Impossibility]
\label{prop:tightness}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Decisive (drop Stable).}
    Set $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  Faithfulness holds by irreflexivity of
    $\incomp$: $\neg\,\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$.
    Decisiveness holds by identity.  Stability fails because the Rashomon
    property places incompatible native explanations on the same observe-fiber.

  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Stable (drop Decisive).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``neutral'' element with
    $\neg\,\incomp(c, \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ for all~$\theta$, and set
    $E(\theta) = c$.  Faithfulness and stability hold, but $E$ abstains on
    every distinction the native explanation makes.

  \item \textbf{Stable $+$ Decisive (drop Faithful).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``maximally committal'' element inheriting every
    incompatibility of every native explanation, and set $E(\theta) = c$.
    Stability and decisiveness hold, but $c$ may contradict some
    $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Necessity of the Rashomon Property}
\label{sec:necessity}

The Rashomon property is not merely sufficient for the impossibility---it is
also necessary.  Formally, if an explanation system does \emph{not} have the
Rashomon property, then no incompatibility can arise between the native
explanations of any two observationally equivalent configurations, and the
impossibility proof breaks at step~1: there are no $\theta_1, \theta_2$ to
apply decisiveness to.  In this case, a faithful, stable, and decisive
explanation map exists: set $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  Faithfulness follows by
irreflexivity; stability follows because all equivalent configurations agree
on their native explanation (no Rashomon witness); decisiveness holds by
identity.

\begin{proposition}[Necessity]
\label{prop:necessity}
An explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ admits a faithful, stable, and decisive
explanation map if and only if it does \emph{not} have the Rashomon property.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
($\Rightarrow$) Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} (impossibility under Rashomon).
($\Leftarrow$) If the Rashomon property fails, $\mathsf{explain}$ is itself
faithful, stable, and decisive: for any $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$, incompatibility
of native explanations is ruled out by the negated Rashomon property, so
stability holds; faithfulness and decisiveness hold by irreflexivity and
identity.
\end{proof}

\noindent
Proposition~\ref{prop:necessity} establishes that the Rashomon property is
the \emph{exact boundary} between possible and impossible explanation:
the Universal Impossibility neither over- nor under-characterizes the
obstruction.  This is the ``necessity'' contribution listed in the
introduction and is formalized in \texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}.

\begin{corollary}[Decidable Underspecification Implies Impossible Explanation]
\label{cor:decidable}
If the data-generating process is not identifiable from the
observations---i.e., if there exist $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ yet
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$---then
the Rashomon property holds and the impossibility applies.
\end{corollary}

\noindent
The simplicity of the proof---four steps from the Rashomon property to
contradiction---is a feature of the framework, not a limitation of the
result. The intellectual content lies in identifying the right
definitions: faithful as non-contradiction, stable as factoring through
observations, decisive as inheriting incompatibilities. The
contribution is the axiomatic framework and its unification of nine
domains, not the proof technique.

\section{The Universal Resolution}
\label{sec:resolution}
%%=======================================================================

The impossibility of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} is tight: one of the three
desiderata must be sacrificed.  In this section we show that a \emph{uniform}
resolution exists whenever the Rashomon property arises from a symmetry group
acting on the hypothesis space.  The resolution sacrifices decisiveness and
achieves faithfulness in expectation together with exact stability.

\input{sections/escape_routes}

\subsection{$G$-Invariant Explanation Systems}
\label{sec:g-invariant}

Let $G$ be a group acting on the hypothesis space~$\sH$.  The action is
\emph{observable-preserving} if for every $g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{obs}(h),
\]
i.e., the group permutes hypotheses within observe-equivalence classes
(fibers).  We say that $G$ \emph{acts transitively on observe-fibers} if for
every pair $h, h'$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$, there exists $g \in G$
such that $g \cdot h = h'$.

\begin{definition}[$G$-Invariant Explanation Map]
\label{def:g-invariant}
An explanation map $\mathsf{exp}$ is \emph{$G$-invariant} if for every
$g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{exp}(h).
\]
\end{definition}

When $G$ is finite and $Y$ admits averaging, the canonical $G$-invariant map
is the \emph{orbit average}:
\[
  \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} \mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h).
\]

\begin{proposition}[$G$-Invariance Implies Stability]
\label{prop:g-invariant-stable}
If $G$ acts transitively on observe-fibers and $\mathsf{exp}$ is
$G$-invariant, then $\mathsf{exp}$ is stable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $h, h' \in \sH$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$.  By transitivity, there
exists $g \in G$ with $g \cdot h = h'$.  By $G$-invariance,
$\mathsf{exp}(h') = \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) = \mathsf{exp}(h)$.
(Formalized as \texttt{gInvariant\_stable} in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Design Space Dichotomy]
\label{prop:dichotomy}
If the explanation system has the Rashomon property, then no stable
explanation map can simultaneously be faithful and decisive.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}: stability, faithfulness, and
decisiveness are jointly contradictory under the Rashomon property.
(Formalized as \texttt{universal\_design\_space\_dichotomy} in
\texttt{UniversalDesignSpace.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Pareto Frontier]
\label{cor:pareto}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property, achievable
explanation maps fall into exactly two families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Family~A} (faithful $+$ decisive, unstable): individual-model
    explanations that faithfully report each hypothesis but vary across
    equivalent hypotheses.
  \item \textbf{Family~B} (stable $+$ faithful in expectation, indecisive):
    $G$-invariant aggregations that are constant on observe-fibers but
    produce ties for queries where the fiber disagrees.
\end{itemize}
No explanation map lies outside these two families.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{\DASH{} as the Prototype Resolution}
\label{sec:dash-prototype}

\DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation of \textbf{SH}AP)
is the $G$-invariant resolution for the feature attribution instance
(Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}).  It averages SHAP values over an
ensemble of models retrained from independent random seeds, achieving
$\E[\phi_j] = \E[\phi_k]$ for any pair of symmetric features $j, k$
(\emph{consensus equity}).  This orbit average is stable by
Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable} and faithful in expectation: the
expected explanation matches the expected observation across the Rashomon set.
The cost is indecisiveness---\DASH{} reports ties for feature pairs whose
rankings disagree across the ensemble.  The companion paper proves that
\DASH{} is Pareto-optimal: no other stable attribution method can achieve
higher expected faithfulness.

\subsection{Meta-Theorem: Orbit Averaging}
\label{sec:meta-theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Orbit-Averaged Resolution]
\label{thm:orbit-average}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be an explanation system whose Rashomon property arises
from a finite group $G$ acting on $\sH$ and acting transitively on
observe-fibers.  Then the orbit-averaged map
$\overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G}\mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h)$
is:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item well-defined (independent of orbit representative),
  \item stable (Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable}),
  \item faithful in expectation: $\E_{g \sim \mathrm{Unif}(G)}[\mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h)] = \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h)$, and
  \item Pareto-optimal among stable maps: no stable map achieves strictly
    higher pointwise faithfulness on every fiber.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent
The orbit-averaged resolution is constructive: for any specific instance, the
group $G$ and its action are determined by the domain structure, and the
averaging can be computed (or approximated by Monte Carlo sampling over the
Rashomon set).  Theorem~\ref{thm:orbit-average} is the abstract counterpart
of the \DASH{} optimality result and is formalized in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.

\section{Ubiquity}
\label{sec:ubiquity}
%%=======================================================================

Is the Universal Explanation Impossibility a pathological corner case, or a
generic phenomenon?  In this section we show that it is the latter: the
Rashomon property---and hence the impossibility---holds whenever the model
has more parameters than observational constraints, a condition satisfied by
virtually all modern machine learning systems.

\subsection{Generic Underspecification}
\label{sec:generic-underspec}

\begin{proposition}[Dimensional Underspecification]
\label{prop:generic-underspec}
Let $\sH$ be a smooth hypothesis space of dimension $\dim(\sH)$ and let
$\mathsf{obs} : \sH \to Y$ be a smooth observation map into a space of
dimension $\dim(Y)$.  If $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$, then for generic
$y \in Y$, the fiber $\mathsf{obs}^{-1}(y)$ has positive dimension
$\geq \dim(\sH) - \dim(Y)$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent
This is a direct consequence of the preimage theorem in differential
topology.  Whenever the fiber has positive dimension, the explanation map
$\mathsf{obs}$ is generically non-constant on fibers---different hypotheses
in the same fiber honestly report different explanations---and the Rashomon
property holds.  (Formalized as \texttt{generic\_underspecification} in
\texttt{Ubiquity.lean}; the bridge from fiber non-degeneracy to the
impossibility is \texttt{fiber\_nondegeneracy\_implies\_impossibility}.)

\subsection{Neural Network Dimensional Argument}
\label{sec:nn-dimensional}

Modern neural networks are massively overparameterized.  A ResNet-50 has
$P \approx 25{,}600{,}000$ parameters and, for ImageNet classification,
$k = 1{,}000$ output classes.  By Proposition~\ref{prop:generic-underspec},
the fiber of any fixed prediction has dimension at least
$P - k = 24{,}999{,}000$.  On such a high-dimensional manifold, any
continuous explanation map (attention weights, gradient saliency, internal
activations) is generically non-constant: there exist models $h, h'$ on the
same fiber with $\mathsf{obs}(h)_j > \mathsf{obs}(h)_k$ and
$\mathsf{obs}(h')_j < \mathsf{obs}(h')_k$ for any pair of input features
$j, k$ with equal marginal distributions.

\begin{corollary}[Coin-Flip Instability]
\label{cor:coin-flip}
For any pair of symmetric features $j, k$ in an overparameterized network
trained to zero loss, the probability (over retraining seeds) that a
faithful explanation ranks $j$ above $k$ is exactly $1/2$.
\end{corollary}

\noindent
This is the neural-network instantiation of the symmetric Bayes result
formalized in \texttt{SymmetricBayes.lean}: the symmetry of the fiber
under feature permutation forces uniform marginals on the ranking.

\subsection{Prevalence}
\label{sec:prevalence}

The dimensional condition $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$ is the norm, not the
exception.  Large language models (billions of parameters, vocabulary-sized
output), diffusion models, graph neural networks, random forests with
hundreds of trees, and Lasso regression with more features than observations
all satisfy it.  Even linear regression with collinear features produces a
positive-dimensional fiber (the collinear subspace).
\citet{damour2022underspecification} document this across NLP, computer
vision, and medical imaging, finding that underspecification---and hence
explanation instability---is ubiquitous in deployed systems.  The only
escape is a model class so constrained that $\dim(\sH) \leq \dim(Y)$, which
excludes essentially all modern architectures of practical interest.

\subsection{Analogy: Arrow's Impossibility Theorem}
\label{sec:arrow-analogy}

The result shares structural features with Arrow's impossibility theorem in social
choice theory \citep{arrow1951social}: three individually reasonable desiderata
that are pairwise satisfiable but jointly impossible under a natural
background condition.  Arrow showed that no voting rule
satisfying unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, and
non-dictatorship exists for three or more candidates---a universal
impossibility that unified disparate paradoxes in voting theory.
\citet{nipkow2009social} later formalized Arrow's theorem in Isabelle/HOL.
Our Universal Explanation Impossibility plays an analogous role for
explainability: it unifies nine domain-specific impossibilities under one
structural cause (the Rashomon property) and one four-line proof, and the
resolution (orbit averaging) is the XAI counterpart of accepting partial
orderings in social choice.
(The full impossibility is formalized as \texttt{ubiquity\_impossibility}
in \texttt{Ubiquity.lean}.)

\section{Discussion: What Explainability Can and Cannot Do}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%=======================================================================

\subsection{The Ceiling of Explainability}
\label{sec:ceiling}

The Universal Explanation Impossibility (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) establishes
a \emph{ceiling}, not a criticism of any particular method.  No axiomatic
redesign of \SHAP{}, LIME, attention maps, or counterfactual generators can
simultaneously deliver faithfulness, stability, and decisiveness whenever the
underlying inference problem is underspecified.  The ceiling is structural: it
follows from the Rashomon property alone, independent of the explanation
formalism.  An analogy to physics is apt: the speed of light constrains
spacecraft engineering but does not prevent useful space travel.  Likewise, the
impossibility constrains what practitioners may promise about explanations but
does not prevent useful explainability---it redirects effort toward the
achievable frontier characterized by the $G$-invariant resolution
(Section~\ref{sec:resolution}).

\input{sections/regulatory_analysis}

\subsection{What Explainability \emph{Can} Do}
\label{sec:can-do}

The $G$-invariant resolution (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) is constructive, not
nihilistic.  It tells the practitioner exactly which desiderata can be jointly
satisfied and which must be relaxed.  \DASH{} for feature attribution, CPDAGs
for causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, and ensemble concept probes
for concept-based explanations \citep{kim2018tcav} each sacrifice decisiveness
to gain stability---and each is a concrete instance of the abstract resolution.
In practice, this means reporting confidence intervals over the Rashomon set
\citep{fisher2019models,semenova2022existence} rather than point estimates:
a feature ranking should come with a stability annotation indicating which
comparisons are robust to retraining and which are not.  The impossibility does
not say ``explanations are useless''; it says ``decisive explanations are
unreliable under underspecification, but partially decisive explanations with
calibrated uncertainty are both achievable and informative.''

\subsection{The ``Isn't This Obvious?'' Defense}
\label{sec:obvious}

A natural reaction is that practitioners already know explanations are unstable
---so what is new?  The contribution is fivefold.  First, we make explicit that
nine domains (attribution, attention, counterfactuals, concept probes, causal
discovery, model selection, saliency maps, token citation, mechanistic
interpretability) share \emph{one}
mechanism---the Rashomon property---rather than nine unrelated pathologies.  Second, the Lean~4
formalization (Section~\ref{sec:lean}: 75~files, 351~theorems, 0~\texttt{sorry})
eliminates logical gaps in the formal proofs that informal arguments inevitably leave.  Third, the
empirical experiments (Section~\ref{sec:instances}) show quantifiable severity:
33\% attention flip rates, measurable counterfactual and concept instability on
standard benchmarks.  Fourth, the resolution via $G$-invariant maps is
constructive and actionable, not merely diagnostic.  Fifth, Lean-verified
tightness theorems (Proposition~\ref{prop:tightness}) prove the impossibility
is non-trivial: each pair of properties \emph{is} simultaneously achievable,
so the theorem is not an artifact of overly strong definitions---it identifies
the exact boundary between the possible and the impossible.
Arrow's impossibility
theorem \citep{arrow1951social} was arguably ``obvious'' once stated---every
committee chair knows that voting rules are imperfect.  Yet its formalization
created the field of social choice theory, precisely because it replaced
intuition with a theorem that could be built upon.

\subsection{The ``All Instances Are Trivial'' Defense}
\label{sec:trivial}

A reviewer might object that each individual instance is already known: attention
instability was documented by \citet{jainwallace2019}, Markov equivalence is
classical \citep{verma1991equivalence}, fairness impossibility was proved by
\citet{chouldechova2017fair}, and \SHAP{} instability was observed by
\citet{damour2022underspecification} and \citet{krishna2022disagreement}.  The
contribution is not any single instance but the \emph{meta-theorem}: the same
four-line proof (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) closes all nine cases under a
single structural condition.  Before this work, a practitioner encountering
attention instability might switch to \SHAP{}, then to counterfactual
explanations, then to concept probes---each time hoping the new method escapes
the problem.  Our theorem shows that no such escape exists: the impossibility
is structural (it follows from the Rashomon property), not method-specific.
Recognizing this unifies the practitioner's response: rather than debugging each
method individually, one diagnoses the Rashomon property and applies the
$G$-invariant resolution uniformly.

\subsection{Implications Beyond Machine Learning}
\label{sec:beyond-ml}

The Universal Explanation Impossibility is not specific to machine learning.
We briefly sketch five domains where the theorem applies directly.

\paragraph{Philosophy of science: formalizing Quine--Duhem.}
Quine \citep{quine1951dogmas} and Duhem \citep{duhem1906aim} argued that
empirical evidence underdetermines theory choice.  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}
proves an impossibility theorem for explanation under observational
equivalence---formalizing, to our knowledge for the first time, the explanatory
consequences of what philosophers of science call contrastive
underdetermination \citep{laudan1991empirical}. Prior formal analyses of
underdetermination in Bayesian
frameworks \citep{dorling1979bayesian,howson2006scientific} did not yield
impossibility results for explanation methods. The $G$-invariant
resolution is structurally reminiscent of epistemic structural
realism \citep{worrall1989structural}: stable explanatory content is what
is invariant across equivalent configurations. The connection is suggestive;
a rigorous mapping would require engaging with the ontological commitments
of structural realism, which our descriptive framework does not address.
Dropping decisiveness corresponds to van
Fraassen's \citep{vanfraassen1980scientific} constructive empiricist stance
that science aims for empirical adequacy, not full theoretical truth.
Okasha \citep{okasha2011theory} connected theory choice to Arrow's impossibility
in social choice; our result provides the complementary connection for
underdetermination of explanation.

\paragraph{Neuroscience: degeneracy as the Rashomon property.}
Marder and Goaillard \citep{marder2006variability} documented that crustacean
stomatogastric ganglia producing identical motor patterns are built from
qualitatively different ionic conductance profiles.  Edelman and Gally
\citep{edelman2001degeneracy} formalized this as \emph{degeneracy}:
structurally distinct elements performing the same function.  Degeneracy is
precisely the Rashomon property in our framework, and the impossibility
theorem applies: no mechanistic account of neural circuit function can
simultaneously be faithful to the specific circuit, stable across degenerate
circuits, and decisive about which ionic mechanism is primary.

\paragraph{Economics: partial identification.}
Manski \citep{manski2003partial} developed partial identification theory for
settings where observational data do not pin down a unique model.  The
identified set corresponds to an observe-fiber in our framework; partial
identification is the economic instantiation of the Rashomon property.  The
$G$-invariant resolution corresponds to reporting bounds on the identified
set rather than a point estimate.

\paragraph{Medical diagnosis: differential diagnosis as resolution.}
When multiple conditions produce the same symptoms, diagnosis faces the
trilemma.  Differential diagnosis---the standard medical practice of
listing all conditions consistent with the symptoms---is precisely
$G$-invariant resolution.  It sacrifices decisiveness (commitment to a
single diagnosis) to gain faithfulness and stability, exactly as
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} predicts.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Questions}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify five limitations and directions for future work.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Axiomatized Rashomon properties.}  The Rashomon property for
    each instance is axiomatized---verified to hold under stated
    assumptions---rather than derived from first principles of the learning
    algorithm.  Deriving the Rashomon property from, e.g., the loss landscape
    geometry of gradient descent would strengthen the result.
  \item \textbf{Weight perturbation vs.\ true retraining.}  The attention
    instability experiments (Section~\ref{sec:instances}) use weight
    perturbation within the zero-loss manifold rather than full retraining from
    independent random seeds.  While this faithfully models the Rashomon set,
    the distribution over perturbations may differ from the distribution induced
    by stochastic gradient descent.
  \item \textbf{Non-attribution quantitative bounds.}  For the attribution
    instance, the companion paper provides sharp quantitative bounds (divergence
    rates, ensemble size requirements, \DASH{} optimality).  Analogous
    quantitative bounds for the counterfactual, concept, and fairness instances
    remain open.
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness as a practical cost.}  The $G$-invariant
    resolution sacrifices decisiveness: it reports ties where the data are
    ambiguous.  Whether downstream decision-makers can tolerate partial rankings
    or tied causal structures---and how to present such outputs
    effectively---is an empirical question we do not address here.
  \item \textbf{Fairness instance not experimentally validated.}  The fairness
    audit impossibility (Instance~6, formalized in \texttt{FairnessAudit.lean})
    is proved but not empirically validated in this paper.
    \citet{chouldechova2017fair} provides the original empirical context;
    replicating it within our framework is left to future work.
\end{enumerate}
