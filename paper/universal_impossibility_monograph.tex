\begin{abstract}
%%% ==========================================================================
We prove a universal impossibility: no explanation system can simultaneously be
faithful (never contradicting the system's native explanation), stable
(consistent across observationally equivalent configurations), and decisive
(inheriting every incompatibility of the native explanation) when
the underlying inference problem is underspecified---that is, when
observationally equivalent configurations produce incompatible explanations
(the Rashomon property).
This single abstract theorem, which we call the \emph{Explanation
Impossibility}, is a meta-theorem whose reach extends far beyond machine
learning.  We derive the impossibility as a zero-axiom consequence in
eight scientific domains: the degeneracy of the genetic code (biology),
gauge freedom in electromagnetism (physics), microstate degeneracy in
statistical mechanics (physics), syntactic ambiguity in natural
language (linguistics), the phase problem in crystallography,
underdetermined linear systems (mathematics), the database view update
problem (computer science), and Markov equivalence in causal inference
(statistics).  We further instantiate it across nine ML explanation types:
feature attribution under collinearity
\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}, attention map instability, counterfactual
explanation instability, concept probe (TCAV) instability, causal discovery
ambiguity \citep{verma1991equivalence}, model selection instability under the
Rashomon effect \citep{rudin2024amazing,fisher2019models}, saliency map
(GradCAM) instability, token citation explanation instability, and
mechanistic interpretability non-uniqueness.
The proof is a tight four-step chain---decisiveness, stability, faithfulness,
contradiction---and Lean-verified tightness theorems show that each pair of
properties is achievable, confirming that the impossibility is non-trivial.
Cross-domain experiments empirically validate the impossibility in seven of
the eight scientific domains (Table~\ref{tab:cross-domain}), including a
perfect dose-response between codon degeneracy and usage entropy across
120 eukaryotic cytochrome~c sequences from NCBI RefSeq
(Kruskal--Wallis $p = 2.0 \times 10^{-3}$, Spearman $\rho = 1.0$;
simulated null: $p = 2.3 \times 10^{-18}$), significant parser disagreement on ambiguous sentences
($p = 9 \times 10^{-4}$), and causal discovery convergence only at infinite
sample size.
Empirical experiments on four ML instances confirm severe instability among
functionally equivalent models: 19.9\% attention argmax flip rate
(under full retraining; 60\% under weight perturbation), 23.5\%
counterfactual direction flip rate, 0.90 concept direction instability,
and 80\% model selection flip rate.
The resolution is uniform: $G$-invariant explanation systems that aggregate
over equivalent hypotheses restore faithfulness-in-expectation and stability at
the cost of decisiveness, and \DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation of
\textbf{SH}AP) is a concrete instance of this resolution for feature attribution.
The impossibility is ubiquitous: any learning problem with strictly more
parameters than constraints admits a Rashomon set of positive measure, and
neural networks with hidden dimension exceeding the data dimension always fall
into this regime.
The framework is mechanically verified in Lean~4 (99~files, 436~theorems,
72~axioms, 0~\texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%% ==========================================================================

A data scientist trains a gradient-boosted model for credit risk, runs \SHAP{},
and finds that \emph{debt-to-income ratio} is the top feature.  She retrains
from a different random seed---same data, same hyperparameters, same test
accuracy---and the top feature flips to \emph{credit utilization}
\citep{krishna2022disagreement}.  An NLP engineer inspects attention weights in
a sentiment classifier, retrains, and the most-attended token changes from
``not'' to ``terrible'' \citep{jainwallace2019}.  A colleague runs LIME on the
same credit model and obtains yet a third ranking
\citep{molnar2022general}.  None of these are software bugs.

The explainable AI (XAI) research program promises explanations that are
\emph{faithful} (they reflect what the model actually computes),
\emph{stable} (they are reproducible across equivalent models), and
\emph{decisive} (they resolve every explanation query with a definite answer).
Regulators now demand all three: the EU AI Act requires ``meaningful
explanations'' for high-risk systems \citep{euaiact2024}, and SR~11-7 requires
``developmental evidence'' documenting model decisions
\citep{occ2011sr117}.  Yet a growing body of theoretical work shows that the
promise is mathematically impossible to keep---at least in specific domains.
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that no feature attribution satisfying
the Shapley axioms is stable under collinearity.
\citet{huang2024failings} show that SHAP values can be arbitrarily
misleading.  \citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent}
independently prove that common fairness metrics cannot simultaneously be
satisfied.  \citet{verma1991equivalence} establish that observationally
equivalent causal structures are indistinguishable from data alone.

Each of these impossibilities was proved independently, in domain-specific
language, under domain-specific assumptions.  The attribution impossibility
assumes collinearity among features; Chouldechova's impossibility assumes
unequal base rates across groups; Markov equivalence assumes faithfulness of
the directed acyclic graph.  No one has asked: is there \emph{one} structural
cause?  Is the impossibility intrinsic to underspecification itself, rather
than to any particular domain?

In this paper we answer affirmatively.  We prove a \emph{Universal Explanation
Impossibility}: no explanation of an underspecified system can simultaneously
be faithful, stable, and decisive.  The theorem requires only the
\emph{Rashomon property}---the existence of equivalent hypotheses that
honestly report incompatible explanations---and the proof is four lines.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{A universal impossibility theorem}
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) that unifies impossibility results
    across eight domains and nine ML explanation types under a single
    proof requiring only the Rashomon property.
  \item \textbf{Eight derived instances across eight domains}
    (Section~\ref{sec:derived-instances}): underdetermined linear systems
    (mathematics), genetic code degeneracy (biology), gauge freedom
    (physics), microstate degeneracy in statistical mechanics (physics),
    syntactic ambiguity (linguistics), the phase problem
    (crystallography), the view update problem (computer science), and
    Markov equivalence (statistics)---each derived with zero axioms from
    domain-specific first principles.
  \item \textbf{Nine ML instances}
    (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}): feature attribution
    \citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}, attention maps
    \citep{jainwallace2019}, counterfactual explanations, concept probes,
    causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, model selection
    \citep{rudin2024amazing}, saliency maps (GradCAM), token citation explanations,
    and mechanistic interpretability (circuit
    discovery)---each shown to be a special case of the abstract framework.
  \item \textbf{Empirical illustration} for seven of the nine ML instances
    (attribution, attention, counterfactual, concept probes, model selection, saliency maps, and token citation),
    confirming explanation instability on standard benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{A uniform resolution}: $G$-invariant aggregation restores
    faithfulness and stability at the cost of decisiveness.  \DASH{} for
    feature attribution, CPDAGs for causal discovery, and ensemble probes
    for concepts are all instances of this resolution.
  \item \textbf{Ubiquity}: the impossibility is generic whenever the
    parameter dimension exceeds the observable dimension, covering
    virtually all modern overparameterized models.
  \item \textbf{Mechanical verification} in Lean~4
    \citep{demoura2021lean4}: 99~files, 436~theorems, 72~axioms,
    0~\texttt{sorry}.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent In short: faithful, stable, decisive---pick two.

This paper generalizes the attribution impossibility of our companion work
\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility} from feature attribution to arbitrary
explanation systems.  The companion paper provides quantitative depth
(divergence rates, ensemble size bounds, \DASH{} optimality) for the
attribution instance; this paper supplies the abstract framework, eight
additional instances, and the ubiquity result.  The two papers share a
Lean codebase but are self-contained.

\section{The Abstract Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%%% ==========================================================================

We introduce a minimal abstract framework sufficient to state and prove the
Explanation Impossibility.  All instances in
Sections~\ref{sec:derived-instances} and~\ref{sec:ml-instances} are special cases.
The framework aligns exactly with the Lean~4 formalization in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}: every definition and theorem below has a
machine-checked counterpart.

\subsection{Explanation Systems}

\begin{definition}[Explanation System]
\label{def:explanation-system}
An \emph{explanation system} is a tuple
\[
  \mathcal{S} = (\Params, \sH, Y, \mathsf{observe}, \mathsf{explain}, \incomp)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Params$ is a \emph{configuration space} (the set of possible
    configurations, e.g., random seeds, hyperparameter settings);
  \item $\sH$ is an \emph{explanation space} (the set of possible explanations);
  \item $Y$ is an \emph{observable space} (e.g., input--output functions,
    test-set predictions);
  \item $\mathsf{observe}: \Params \to Y$ is the \emph{observation map}, assigning
    to each configuration its observable behavior;
  \item $\mathsf{explain}: \Params \to \sH$ is the system's \emph{native
    explanation map}, assigning to each configuration the explanation that the
    system itself ``honestly reports''; and
  \item $\incomp\;\subseteq \sH \times \sH$ is an \emph{incompatibility relation}
    on explanations satisfying \emph{irreflexivity}:
    $\neg\,\incomp(h, h)$ for every $h \in \sH$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Irreflexivity of~$\incomp$ is a structural requirement: no explanation
contradicts itself.  This is the \texttt{incompatible\_irrefl} field of the
Lean structure and is used in the tightness proof
(Section~\ref{sec:tightness}).

An \emph{explanation map} $E : \Params \to \sH$ is a procedure that a
practitioner \emph{chooses} (e.g., SHAP, LIME, a fixed causal orientation
rule).  The native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ records what the
configuration \emph{actually implies}; $E(\theta)$ is what the chosen method
reports.

\subsection{The Three Desiderata}

\begin{definition}[Faithful]
\label{def:faithful}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{faithful} if $E(\theta)$ never contradicts the
system's native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\quad
  \neg\,\incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta)\bigr).
\]
That is, $E(\theta)$ may be less specific than $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ but
never disagrees with it.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Stable]
\label{def:stable}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{stable} if equivalent configurations receive
the same explanation:
\[
  \forall\,\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params,\quad
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Decisive]
\label{def:decisive}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{decisive} if $E(\theta)$ inherits every
incompatibility of the native explanation: whatever
$\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ rules out, $E(\theta)$ also rules out.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\;\forall\,h \in \sH,\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta),\, h\bigr)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  \incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\, h\bigr).
\]
$E$ is at least as committal as $\mathsf{explain}$.
\end{definition}

In the feature attribution instance (Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}),
faithfulness means the chosen method never contradicts the model's own SHAP
values, stability means consistency across retraining seeds, and decisiveness
means that every distinction made by the native attribution is preserved.

\subsection{The Rashomon Property}

The key structural condition linking underspecification to explanation
impossibility is the following.

\begin{definition}[Rashomon Property]
\label{def:rashomon}
An explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the \emph{Rashomon property} if there
exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params$ with
\[
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta_2)\bigr).
\]
That is, two configurations that are observationally equivalent honestly report
incompatible explanations.
\end{definition}

The Rashomon property is the formal statement of underspecification at the
explanation level: the data do not uniquely determine the explanation, even in
principle.  It generalizes the Rashomon effect of
\citet{rudin2024amazing,fisher2019models} from model selection to arbitrary
explanation targets.

\subsection{Main Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Explanation Impossibility]
\label{thm:universal}
If an explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the Rashomon property, then no
explanation map $E$ can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and decisive.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the Rashomon property, there exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ and
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
Suppose for contradiction that $E$ is faithful, stable, and decisive.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness} at $\theta_1$:
    $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$
    implies
    $\incomp(E(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Stability}:
    $\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$
    implies $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, so
    $\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness} at $\theta_2$:
    $\neg\,\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item Steps 2 and 3 contradict.  \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof uses all three properties in a tight chain:
decisive $\to$ stable $\to$ faithful $\to$ contradiction.
This mirrors the Lean proof exactly (\texttt{explanation\_impossibility} in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The simplicity of the proof---four steps from the Rashomon
property to contradiction---is a feature of the framework, not a
limitation of the result.  The intellectual content lies in identifying the
right definitions: faithful as non-contradiction, stable as factoring
through observations, decisive as inheriting incompatibilities.  The
contribution is the axiomatic framework and its unification of nine
domains, not the proof technique.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The theorem is deliberately parameter-free: it requires no regularity
conditions on $\sH$, no metric structure on $Y$, and no probabilistic
assumptions.  All quantitative depth (divergence rates, sample complexity,
ensemble sizes) appears in the instance-specific sections below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Tightness}
\label{sec:tightness}

The impossibility is tight: every pair of properties is simultaneously
achievable.  Three Lean-verified witnesses establish this
(\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_decisive},
\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_stable},
\texttt{tightness\_stable\_decisive} in \texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).

\begin{proposition}[Tightness of the Universal Impossibility]
\label{prop:tightness}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Decisive (drop Stable).}
    Set $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  Faithfulness holds by irreflexivity of
    $\incomp$: $\neg\,\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$.
    Decisiveness holds by identity.  Stability fails because the Rashomon
    property places incompatible native explanations on the same observe-fiber.

  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Stable (drop Decisive).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``neutral'' element with
    $\neg\,\incomp(c, \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ for all~$\theta$, and set
    $E(\theta) = c$.  Faithfulness and stability hold, but $E$ abstains on
    every distinction the native explanation makes.

  \item \textbf{Stable $+$ Decisive (drop Faithful).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``maximally committal'' element inheriting every
    incompatibility of every native explanation, and set $E(\theta) = c$.
    Stability and decisiveness hold, but $c$ may contradict some
    $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Decidable Underspecification Implies Impossible Explanation]
\label{cor:decidable}
If the data-generating process is not identifiable from the
observations---i.e., if there exist $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ yet
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$---then
the Rashomon property holds and the impossibility applies.
\end{corollary}

The converse also holds for fully specified systems: when each observation
uniquely determines its configuration, $E = \mathsf{explain}$ satisfies all
three properties simultaneously (Lean: \texttt{fully\_specified\_possibility}
in \texttt{Necessity.lean}).

\subsection{Strengthened Impossibility for Maximally Incompatible Systems}
\label{sec:bilemma}

The trilemma's tightness depends on the structure of the explanation
space~$\sH$.  When $\sH$ is \emph{maximally incompatible}---the only
compatible pair is $(h, h)$---the trilemma collapses to a \emph{bilemma}.

\begin{definition}[Maximal incompatibility]
\label{def:max-incompat}
An explanation system $(\Theta, H, Y, \mathsf{observe}, \mathsf{explain}, \incomp)$
is \emph{maximally incompatible} if
$\lnot\incomp(h_1, h_2) \Rightarrow h_1 = h_2$
for all $h_1, h_2 \in H$.
Equivalently, every pair of distinct explanations is incompatible.
Any binary $H$ with $\incomp = {\neq}$ satisfies this condition.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The bilemma]
\label{thm:bilemma}
For any maximally incompatible explanation system with the Rashomon
property, no explanation is simultaneously faithful and stable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $E : \Theta \to H$ be faithful.  For any $\theta$, faithfulness gives
$\lnot\incomp(E(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$.  By maximal
incompatibility, $E(\theta) = \mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.  Therefore
$E = \mathsf{explain}$, which is decisive (it inherits all
incompatibilities of itself).  But now $E$ is faithful, stable, and
decisive---contradicting the trilemma (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Stable + Decisive also impossible]
\label{thm:no-sd-maxincompat}
For any maximally incompatible explanation system, no explanation is
simultaneously stable and decisive.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $E$ be decisive.  For any $\theta$, suppose
$E(\theta) \neq \mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.  By maximal incompatibility,
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta), E(\theta))$.  By decisiveness,
$\incomp(E(\theta), E(\theta))$---contradicting irreflexivity.
So $E(\theta) = \mathsf{explain}(\theta)$, making $E$ faithful.
But then $E$ is faithful, stable, and decisive---contradicting the trilemma.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Tightness under maximal incompatibility]
\label{prop:tightness-maxincompat}
Under maximal incompatibility, only faithful~+~decisive survives
(via $E = \mathsf{explain}$).  Tightness collapses from 3/3 to 1/3.
\end{proposition}

\centering\small

\midrule
Faithful + Decisive & \checkmark & \checkmark & \checkmark \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Tightness spectrum: 3/3 (abstract), 1/3 (max.\ incompatible), 2/3 (enriched with neutral element).}
\label{tab:tightness-maxincompat}
\end{table}

Many natural explanation spaces are maximally incompatible: binary
feature rankings ($j > k$ or $k > j$, no tie), DAG edge orientations
($X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$, no undirected), and quantum measurement
outcomes.

Maximal incompatibility is not an artificial condition: 19 of the 29 Lean-verified instance files in this project use $\mathrm{incompatible} = (\neq)$, making them maximally incompatible.  The bilemma thus applies to the majority of instances, including all binary ranking spaces, all DAG orientation spaces, and all quantum measurement instances.
The bilemma does not have direct mechanism design content.  Mechanism design concerns incentive compatibility under strategic agents; the bilemma concerns the logical structure of explanation spaces without strategic considerations.

\subsection{The Enrichment Mechanism}
\label{sec:enrichment}

\paragraph{Definition.}
Given a maximally incompatible explanation space $H$, the \emph{enrichment} of $H$ is the space $H^+ = H \cup \{c\}$, where $c$ is a \emph{neutral element}: an element compatible with every $h \in H$ (i.e., $\lnot\incomp(c, h)$ for all $h$).

\paragraph{Why enrichment works.}
Because $c$ is compatible with all native explanations, the constant map $E(\theta) = c$ is both faithful (never contradicts any $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$) and stable (assigns the same explanation to all configurations).  This restores faithful~+~stable at the cost of decisiveness: the constant map draws no distinctions at all.
(Lean: \texttt{recovery\_faithful\_stable} in \texttt{MaximalIncompatibility.lean}.)

\paragraph{Why stable~+~decisive is NOT restored.}
Enrichment does \emph{not} restore stable~+~decisive.  Decisiveness requires $E(\theta)$ to inherit all incompatibilities of $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$, which forces $E(\theta) = \mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ on non-neutral elements (by maximal incompatibility).  But then $E = \mathsf{explain}$ is not stable (the Rashomon property gives two configurations with the same observable but different explanations).  The neutral element $c$ cannot help: it is compatible with everything, so it is not decisive at any $\theta$.

\paragraph{Instances.}
Four domain-specific resolutions are instances of enrichment:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{DASH} (ML attribution): adds ``tied'' to the binary ranking space $\{j > k, \; k > j\}$, allowing the ensemble to report that two features are indistinguishable.
  \item \textbf{CPDAG} (causal discovery): adds ``undirected'' to the edge orientation space $\{X \to Y, \; Y \to X\}$, allowing the equivalence class to leave an edge unoriented.
  \item \textbf{BMA} (model selection): adds ``uncertain'' to the model identity space, allowing posterior averaging over competing models.
  \item \textbf{QEC} (quantum error correction): adds ancilla qubits to the measurement space, enabling syndrome-based recovery that does not commit to a single error pattern.
\end{itemize}

Categorically, the enrichment operation is the free adjunction of an initial object to the incompatibility poset: adding an element $c$ compatible with everything is adjoining a least element under the compatibility ordering.

\paragraph{Categorical interpretation.} The enrichment mechanism has a precise categorical characterization. View the explanation space $H$ as a discrete category with morphisms given by compatibility (where $h_1 \to h_2$ iff $\lnot\mathrm{incompatible}(h_1, h_2)$). A maximally incompatible system is one where the only morphisms are identities --- a discrete category. Adding a neutral element $c$ (compatible with everything) freely adjoins an initial object: $c$ has a unique morphism to every object. The enriched system is the free completion of the discrete category under an initial object. This characterization explains why enrichment is canonical: it is the \emph{minimal} extension of $H$ that admits a faithful + stable map. Any other F+S-restoring enrichment must contain a neutral element (by the characterization theorem), and the free adjunction is the universal such extension.

\paragraph{Characterization.}
Enrichment is needed if and only if the bilemma holds, which occurs if and only if no neutral element exists in $H$.  Define a neutral element as $c \in H$ such that $\lnot\incomp(c, \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ for all $\theta$.  If such a $c$ exists, then $E(\theta) = c$ is faithful and stable, so the bilemma fails and no enrichment is needed.  Conversely, if the bilemma holds (F+S is impossible for all $E$), then no neutral element can exist.  Maximal incompatibility implies no neutral element: if $c$ were compatible with all native explanations, then $c = \mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ for all $\theta$ (by maximal incompatibility), but the Rashomon property gives $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$, contradicting irreflexivity.  The converse of the last implication is false: a system can lack a neutral element without being maximally incompatible.  (Lean: \texttt{BilemmaCharacterization.lean}, three theorems, zero \texttt{sorry}.)

\paragraph{ML instances.}
Three binary explanation types trigger the bilemma:
(1)~SHAP sign attribution (positive/negative), where collinearity flips the sign across equivalent models;
(2)~feature selection (selected/not\_selected), where Lasso path instability swaps features;
(3)~counterfactual direction (increase/decrease), where equivalent classifiers give opposite advice.
For all three, faithfulness + stability is impossible --- not just the trilemma.

\paragraph{Predictive consequences.}
Four structural predictions follow from the bilemma (Lean: \texttt{PredictiveConsequences.lean}, five theorems, zero \texttt{sorry}).

\begin{theorem}[Stability forces unfaithfulness]
\label{thm:stable-implies-unfaithful}
For any maximally incompatible system, every stable explanation $E$ satisfies $\exists \theta$, $\incomp(E(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $E$ were both stable and faithful, the bilemma gives a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Rashomon unfaithfulness bound]
\label{thm:rashomon-unfaithfulness}
On any Rashomon pair $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$ with $\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ and $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$, a stable $E$ is unfaithful at $\geq 1$ of the 2~witnesses:
\[
\incomp(E(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_1)) \lor \incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2)).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stability gives $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$.  If $E(\theta_1)$ is compatible with $\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1)$, then $E(\theta_1) = \mathsf{explain}(\theta_1)$ (maximal incompatibility), so $E(\theta_2) = \mathsf{explain}(\theta_1)$.  Then $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$ gives $\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Faithful uniqueness]
\label{thm:faithful-unique}
In a maximally incompatible system, faithful explanations are unique: if $E_1$ and $E_2$ are both faithful, then $E_1(\theta) = E_2(\theta)$ for all $\theta$.  Both equal $\mathsf{explain}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:bilemma}'s proof, faithful $\Rightarrow$ $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  So $E_1 = \mathsf{explain} = E_2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[All-or-nothing]
\label{thm:all-or-nothing}
For any maximally incompatible system and any explanation $E$, exactly one of the following holds:
(i)~$E$ is faithful and $E = \mathsf{explain}$ (hence decisive but not stable), or
(ii)~$\exists \theta$, $\incomp(E(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ ($E$ is unfaithful somewhere).
No continuous interpolation exists.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $E$ is faithful, maximal incompatibility forces $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  If $E$ is not faithful, $\exists \theta$, $\incomp(E(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ by definition.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{On simplicity.} The bilemma is a short proof. Its value is structural: it reveals the impossibility landscape depends on $H$'s incompatibility structure, not just the Rashomon property. The abstract trilemma treats all systems uniformly; the bilemma shows tightness is a system-specific property.

\paragraph{Structural parallels.} Arrow's impossibility theorem simplifies for binary alternatives; Gibbard--Satterthwaite simplifies for two candidates; the doctrinal paradox depends on proposition structure. The pattern --- impossibility strength governed by outcome space structure --- may warrant systematic study.

\texttt{sorry} across three files: \texttt{MaximalIncompatibility.lean} (8~theorems), \texttt{BilemmaCharacterization.lean} (3~theorems), and \texttt{PredictiveConsequences.lean} (5~theorems).

\section{The Universal Resolution}
\label{sec:resolution}
%%% ==========================================================================

The impossibility of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} is tight: one of the three
desiderata must be sacrificed.  In this section we show that a \emph{uniform}
resolution exists whenever the Rashomon property arises from a symmetry group
acting on the hypothesis space.  The resolution sacrifices decisiveness and
achieves faithfulness in expectation together with exact stability.

\subsection[G-Invariant Explanation Systems]{$G$-Invariant Explanation Systems}
\label{sec:g-invariant}

Let $G$ be a group acting on the hypothesis space~$\sH$.  The action is
\emph{observable-preserving} if for every $g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{obs}(h),
\]
i.e., the group permutes hypotheses within observe-equivalence classes
(fibers).  We say that $G$ \emph{acts transitively on observe-fibers} if for
every pair $h, h'$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$, there exists $g \in G$
such that $g \cdot h = h'$.

\begin{definition}[$G$-Invariant Explanation Map]
\label{def:g-invariant}
An explanation map $\mathsf{exp}$ is \emph{$G$-invariant} if for every
$g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{exp}(h).
\]
\end{definition}

When $G$ is finite and $Y$ admits averaging, the canonical $G$-invariant map
is the \emph{orbit average}:
\[
  \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h).
\]

\begin{proposition}[$G$-Invariance Implies Stability]
\label{prop:g-invariant-stable}
If $G$ acts transitively on observe-fibers and $\mathsf{exp}$ is
$G$-invariant, then $\mathsf{exp}$ is stable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $h, h' \in \sH$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$.  By transitivity, there
exists $g \in G$ with $g \cdot h = h'$.  By $G$-invariance,
$\mathsf{exp}(h') = \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) = \mathsf{exp}(h)$.
(Formalized as \texttt{gInvariant\_stable} in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Design Space Dichotomy]
\label{prop:dichotomy}
If the explanation system has the Rashomon property, then no stable
explanation map can simultaneously be faithful and decisive.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}: stability, faithfulness, and
decisiveness are jointly contradictory under the Rashomon property.
(Formalized as \texttt{universal\_design\_space\_dichotomy} in
\texttt{UniversalDesignSpace.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Pareto Frontier]
\label{cor:pareto}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property, achievable
explanation maps fall into exactly two families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Family~A} (faithful $+$ decisive, unstable): individual-model
    explanations that faithfully report each hypothesis but vary across
    equivalent hypotheses.
  \item \textbf{Family~B} (stable $+$ faithful in expectation, indecisive):
    $G$-invariant aggregations that are constant on observe-fibers but
    produce ties for queries where the fiber disagrees.
\end{itemize}
No explanation map lies outside these two families.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{\DASH{} as the Prototype Resolution}
\label{sec:dash-prototype}

\DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation of \textbf{SH}AP)
is the $G$-invariant resolution for the feature attribution instance
(Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}).  It averages SHAP values over an
ensemble of models retrained from independent random seeds, achieving
$\E[\phi_j] = \E[\phi_k]$ for any pair of symmetric features $j, k$
(\emph{consensus equity}).  This orbit average is stable by
Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable} and faithful in expectation: the
expected explanation matches the expected observation across the Rashomon set.
The cost is indecisiveness---\DASH{} reports ties for feature pairs whose
rankings disagree across the ensemble.  The companion paper proves that
\DASH{} is Pareto-optimal: no other stable attribution method can achieve
higher expected faithfulness.

\subsection{Meta-Theorem: Orbit Averaging}
\label{sec:meta-theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Orbit-Averaged Resolution]
\label{thm:orbit-average}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be an explanation system whose Rashomon property arises
from a finite group $G$ acting on $\sH$ and acting transitively on
observe-fibers.  Then the orbit-averaged map
$\overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G}\mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h)$
is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item well-defined (independent of orbit representative),
  \item stable (Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable}),
  \item faithful in expectation: $\E_{g \sim \mathrm{Unif}(G)}[\mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h)] = \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h)$, and
  \item Pareto-optimal among stable maps: no stable map achieves strictly
    higher pointwise faithfulness on every fiber.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent
The orbit-averaged resolution is constructive: for any specific instance, the
group $G$ and its action are determined by the domain structure, and the
averaging can be computed (or approximated by Monte Carlo sampling over the
Rashomon set).  Theorem~\ref{thm:orbit-average} is the abstract counterpart
of the \DASH{} optimality result and is formalized in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.
The resolution strategy---restricting to $G$-invariant explanation maps---is
structurally analogous to the invariant decision procedures justified by the
Hunt--Stein theorem \citep{lehmann2005testing}: just as the Hunt--Stein theorem
guarantees that invariant tests are minimax optimal when a group acts on the
parameter space, the $G$-invariant aggregation is Pareto-optimal among stable
explanation methods.

\section{Ubiquity}
\label{sec:ubiquity}
%%% ==========================================================================

Is the Explanation Impossibility a pathological corner case, or a
generic phenomenon?  In this section we show that it is the latter: the
Rashomon property---and hence the impossibility---holds whenever the model
has more parameters than observational constraints, a condition satisfied by
virtually all modern machine learning systems.

\subsection{Generic Underspecification}
\label{sec:generic-underspec}

\begin{proposition}[Dimensional Underspecification]
\label{prop:generic-underspec}
Let $\sH$ be a smooth hypothesis space of dimension $\dim(\sH)$ and let
$\mathsf{obs} : \sH \to Y$ be a smooth observation map into a space of
dimension $\dim(Y)$.  If $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$, then for generic
$y \in Y$, the fiber $\mathsf{obs}^{-1}(y)$ has positive dimension
$\geq \dim(\sH) - \dim(Y)$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent
This is a direct consequence of the preimage theorem in differential
topology.  Whenever the fiber has positive dimension, the explanation map
$\mathsf{obs}$ is generically non-constant on fibers---different hypotheses
in the same fiber honestly report different explanations---and the Rashomon
property holds.  (Formalized as \texttt{generic\_underspecification} in
\texttt{Ubiquity.lean}; the bridge from fiber non-degeneracy to the
impossibility is \texttt{fiber\_nondegeneracy\_implies\_impossibility}.)

\subsection{Neural Network Dimensional Argument}
\label{sec:nn-dimensional}

Modern neural networks are massively overparameterized.  A ResNet-50 has
$P \approx 25{,}600{,}000$ parameters and, for ImageNet classification,
$k = 1{,}000$ output classes.  By Proposition~\ref{prop:generic-underspec},
the fiber of any fixed prediction has dimension at least
$P - k = 24{,}999{,}000$.  On such a high-dimensional manifold, any
continuous explanation map (attention weights, gradient saliency, internal
activations) is generically non-constant: there exist models $h, h'$ on the
same fiber with $\mathsf{obs}(h)_j > \mathsf{obs}(h)_k$ and
$\mathsf{obs}(h')_j < \mathsf{obs}(h')_k$ for any pair of input features
$j, k$ with equal marginal distributions.

\begin{corollary}[Coin-Flip Instability]
\label{cor:coin-flip}
For any pair of symmetric features $j, k$ in an overparameterized network
trained to zero loss, the probability (over retraining seeds) that a
faithful explanation ranks $j$ above $k$ is exactly $1/2$.
\end{corollary}

\noindent
This is the neural-network instantiation of the symmetric Bayes
result formalized in \texttt{Symmetric\-Bayes.lean}: the symmetry
of the fiber under feature permutation forces uniform marginals on
the ranking.

\subsection{Prevalence}
\label{sec:prevalence}

The dimensional condition $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$ is the norm, not the
exception.  Large language models (billions of parameters, vocabulary-sized
output), diffusion models, graph neural networks, random forests with
hundreds of trees, and Lasso regression with more features than observations
all satisfy it.  Even linear regression with collinear features produces a
positive-dimensional fiber (the collinear subspace).
\citet{damour2022underspecification} document this across NLP,
computer vision, and medical imaging, finding that
underspecification---and hence explanation instability---is ubiquitous
in deployed systems.  The only escape is a model class so constrained
that $\dim(\sH) \leq \dim(Y)$, which excludes essentially all modern
architectures of practical interest.

\subsection{Analogy: Arrow's Impossibility Theorem}
\label{sec:arrow-analogy}

The structure of our result parallels Arrow's impossibility theorem in social
choice theory \citep{arrow1951social}.  Arrow showed that no voting rule
satisfying unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, and
non-dictatorship exists for three or more candidates---a universal
impossibility that unified disparate paradoxes in voting theory.
\citet{nipkow2009social} later formalized Arrow's theorem in Isabelle/HOL.
Our Explanation Impossibility plays an analogous role for
explainability: it unifies nine domain-specific impossibilities under one
structural cause (the Rashomon property) and one four-line proof, and the
resolution (orbit averaging) is the XAI counterpart of accepting partial
orderings in social choice.
The framework does not currently express the Gibbard--Satterthwaite theorem, which concerns strategy-proofness (incentive compatibility)---a property involving strategic agents, not truthful explanation.  Extending the framework to capture strategic considerations would require adding an agent model, which is outside the current scope.
(The full impossibility is formalized as \texttt{ubiquity\_impossibility}
in \texttt{Ubiquity.lean}.)

\subsection{Why This Result Is Non-Trivial}
\label{sec:non-trivial}

A natural objection is that the impossibility is tautological: ``of course
you cannot explain an underspecified system perfectly.''  Four lines of
evidence refute this characterization.

\paragraph{1.\ Tightness: the triple is the exact obstruction.}
Each \emph{pair} of \{faithful, stable, decisive\} is simultaneously
achievable---three Lean-verified witnesses establish this
(Proposition~\ref{prop:tightness}).  A faithful-and-decisive map simply
reports the native explanation; a faithful-and-stable map outputs a neutral
constant; a stable-and-decisive map outputs a maximally committal constant.
The impossibility is \emph{specifically} the conjunction of all three.
A tautology would make even pairs unachievable; here every pair is
constructively realized.

\paragraph{2.\ Necessity: the Rashomon property is the exact boundary.}
The converse is proved in Lean (\texttt{fully\_specified\_possibility} in
\texttt{Necessity.lean}): if the system has \emph{no} Rashomon
property---every observation uniquely determines its configuration---then
all three properties are simultaneously achievable.  The Rashomon property
is therefore both sufficient \emph{and} necessary for the impossibility.
This is not a vague ``things are hard when the problem is hard'' statement;
it is a precise logical equivalence: the impossibility holds \emph{if and
only if} the Rashomon property holds.

\paragraph{3.\ Resolution optimality: the $G$-invariant projection is
Pareto-optimal.}
The resolution is not arbitrary.  The orbit-averaging map is Pareto-optimal
among all stable explanation maps: no stable map can achieve strictly higher
pointwise faithfulness on every observe-fiber.  This means the trade-off
identified by the theorem is \emph{tight}---the practitioner cannot do better
by being clever.

\paragraph{4.\ Independent convergence: eight domains instantiate the same
resolution.}
If the impossibility were trivial, one would not expect eight independent
scientific communities---spanning physics, biology, statistics, linguistics,
crystallography, computer science, statistical mechanics, and
mathematics---to converge on structurally the same resolution strategy
(orbit averaging) over more than a century of independent work
(Table~\ref{tab:structural-differences}).
Our framework explains \emph{why} they converged---they were independently
solving the same impossibility---and proves the resolution they found is
optimal.

\subsection{The Ceiling of Explainability}
\label{sec:ceiling}

The Explanation Impossibility (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) establishes
a \emph{ceiling}, not a criticism of any particular method.  No axiomatic
redesign of \SHAP{}, LIME, attention maps, or counterfactual generators can
simultaneously deliver faithfulness, stability, and decisiveness whenever the
underlying inference problem is underspecified.  The ceiling is structural: it
follows from the Rashomon property alone, independent of the explanation
formalism.  An analogy to physics is apt: the speed of light constrains
spacecraft engineering but does not prevent useful space travel.  Likewise, the
impossibility constrains what practitioners may promise about explanations but
does not prevent useful explainability---it redirects effort toward the
achievable frontier characterized by the $G$-invariant resolution
(Section~\ref{sec:resolution}).

\subsection{Cross-Domain Transfer: From Gauge Theory to ML}
\label{sec:transfer}

The universal framework is not merely descriptive---it enables knowledge
transfer between domains.  The gauge theory resolution (restricting to
observables invariant under gauge transformations) is mathematically identical
to the ML resolution \DASH{} (averaging SHAP values over an ensemble of
near-optimal models): both are the $G$-invariant projection for their
respective symmetry groups.  The companion paper demonstrates that this
gauge-theory-inspired resolution reduces attribution instability by up to
146\% on real-world datasets (Breast Cancer: \DASH{} achieves 92.5\%
stability vs.\ 37.6\% for single-model SHAP) and reduces model-selection
variance by 60\% (crossed ANOVA).  Moreover, the stability improvement scales
with the Rashomon set size: at low collinearity ($\rho = 0$), single-model
methods are nearly as stable as \DASH{}; at high collinearity
($\rho = 0.95$), the gap widens to 2.6 percentage points on synthetic data
and dramatically more on real data with correlated features.  This dose-response
was predicted by the theorem: larger Rashomon sets produce more instability,
and orbit averaging provides proportionally larger improvements.

\paragraph{Beyond ML.} As evidence that the impossibility extends beyond ML,
we note that spacetime geometry under Ostrowski's classification of
completions of $\mathbb{Q}$ is a maximally incompatible system: the two
completions yield exactly two geometries (smooth and ultrametric), triggering
the bilemma (Theorem~\ref{thm:bilemma}).  The adelic resolution --- formulating physics over all completions
simultaneously --- is the enrichment escape, and it is the \emph{unique}
faithful + stable value on the Rashomon fiber. The full development, including
the Ostrowski--Design Space correspondence and physical scoping (conditional
on the Freund--Witten adelic product formula), is in a companion
paper~\cite{caraker2026ostrowski}.

\subsection{Toward a Group-Theoretic Classification of Explainability}
\label{sec:group-classification}

The resolution framework reveals a deeper structure: the difficulty of
explanation is not merely binary (possible vs.\ impossible) but is
\emph{parameterized by the symmetry group} of the Rashomon set.
Different group-theoretic structures produce qualitatively different
impossibility regimes and qualitatively different resolutions.

\paragraph{The observation.}
For an explanation system with symmetry group $G$ acting on the Rashomon
fibers (the set of configurations sharing the same observable), the space
of stable explanation maps is isomorphic to the space of $G$-invariant
functions on the configuration space.  The $G$-invariant projection
(orbit averaging) is the \emph{Reynolds operator}---the projection onto
the trivial representation of $G$:
\[
  R(f)(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} f(g \cdot x)
  \quad\text{(finite $G$)},
  \qquad
  R(f)(x) \;=\; \int_G f(g \cdot x)\, d\mu(g)
  \quad\text{(compact $G$)}.
\]
This is not a new mathematical object---it is the standard tool of invariant
theory \citep{sturmfels2008algorithms}---but its identification as the
universal resolution of the Explanation Impossibility, across all domains
simultaneously, appears to be new.

\paragraph{A hierarchy of difficulty.}
The group structure determines the character of the impossibility and the
available resolutions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Finite abelian $G$} (gauge theory $\mathbb{Z}_2^{|V|-1}$,
    statistical mechanics $S_2$, biology $S_k$ for $k \leq 2$).
    The orbit average is canonical and unique.  All characters are
    one-dimensional, so the invariant projection has no ambiguity.
    The faithfulness bound is $1/|G|$.  These are the ``easy'' cases:
    the impossibility is real but the resolution is completely determined.

  \item \textbf{Finite non-abelian $G$} (biology $S_k$ for $k \geq 3$,
    causal discovery with $\geq 3$ reversible edges, molecular point groups in chemistry).
    Multiple inequivalent invariant projections may exist, corresponding
    to different choices of which non-trivial irreducible representations
    to retain.  The resolution choice itself becomes ambiguous---a
    \emph{second-order Rashomon problem}.  We observe this empirically:
    different causal discovery scoring functions (BIC, AIC, MDL) can
    produce different CPDAGs on the same data, reflecting different
    implicit choices among equivalent invariant representations.

  \item \textbf{Finite non-trivial $G$} (crystallography $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_n \times \mathbb{Z}_2$).
    The Haar measure on $G$ is finite, so orbit averaging converges.
    The Peter-Weyl theorem decomposes $L^2(G)$ into irreducible
    representations, and the invariant projection retains precisely the
    zeroth-frequency (DC) component.  This is why the Patterson
    map---the autocorrelation, i.e., the zero-frequency Fourier
    content---is the natural resolution in crystallography.

  \item \textbf{Non-compact continuous $G$} (linear algebra $\mathbb{R}^d$,
    deep learning weight space).
    No finite Haar measure exists on non-compact orbits.  Orbit averaging
    diverges without regularization.  The pseudoinverse (minimum-norm
    solution) is Tikhonov regularization with $\lambda \to 0$---a
    \emph{regularized} orbit average.  Different regularizers (L1, L2,
    elastic net) produce different ``resolutions,'' and the choice of
    regularizer introduces an external degree of freedom not present in the
    compact case.  This is the ``fundamentally hard'' regime: the
    impossibility is severe and the resolution is non-canonical.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent This ordering is a partial order, not a total order: compact non-abelian groups (e.g., $\mathrm{SO}(3)$) exhibit features of both items~2 and~3.

\paragraph{Predictions.}
This hierarchy generates testable predictions not currently verified in the
paper:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Domains with abelian symmetry should exhibit a unique canonical
    resolution with no practitioner disagreement on what the ``right''
    invariant quantity is.
  \item Domains with non-abelian symmetry should exhibit \emph{resolution
    multiplicity}---different ``reasonable'' resolutions that disagree---and
    this disagreement should itself be measurable.
  \item Domains with non-compact symmetry should require regularization,
    and different regularizers should produce different answers whose spread
    scales with the non-compactness of the group.
  \item The faithfulness bound $1/|G|$ (finite) or $\exp(-\dim G)$
    (Lie groups) should predict the empirical instability across domains.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What is missing.}
The current eight instances span finite abelian, (barely) finite non-abelian,
compact continuous, and non-compact continuous groups.  Not yet represented
are: genuinely non-abelian finite groups (e.g., the tetrahedral point group
$T_d$ in molecular spectroscopy, order~24); non-compact Lie groups beyond
translations (e.g., the Lorentz group $\mathrm{SO}(3,1)$ in relativistic
physics); infinite discrete groups (e.g., lattice symmetries
$\mathbb{Z}^n$); and groupoids---where the symmetry structure varies
fiber-by-fiber, as happens in ML when different regions of feature space have
different Rashomon set sizes.  The groupoid generalization would connect this
work to the theory of stacks in algebraic geometry and to fiber bundles in
differential geometry.  We leave these extensions to future work.

\paragraph{Character theory and the information trade-off.}
Character theory quantifies the trade-off precisely.  For the symmetric
group $S_k$ acting on $\R^k$ by permutation of coordinates, the
representation decomposes into the trivial representation (dimension~1)
and the standard representation (dimension~$k - 1$).  The $G$-invariant
subspace is exactly the trivial representation: $\dim(V^G) = 1$, while
$\dim(V) = k$.  The resolution therefore preserves exactly $1/k$ of the
information---the orbit average retains the mean and discards the
$k - 1$ ``directional'' degrees of freedom.  This connects the
dose-response (entropy scaling with degeneracy $k$) directly to
representation-theoretic structure: the ratio
$\dim(V^G) / \dim(V) = 1/k$ determines the fraction of explanation
content that survives projection onto the invariant subspace.

The ratio $\dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$ is a standard quantity in representation theory, computable via the character formula (Burnside's lemma).  The novelty is not the formula but its \emph{application}: we show empirically that this ratio predicts explanation instability rates across seven domains with $R^2 = 0.957$ (well-characterized groups), using a quantity from abstract algebra as a parameter-free predictor of empirical flip rates.

\paragraph{The deeper claim.}
If the group-theoretic parameterization is correct in full generality, then
the Explanation Impossibility is not a standalone theorem but the \emph{first
theorem in a classification theory}---analogous to how Arrow's impossibility
theorem \citep{arrow1951social} was the first theorem in social choice theory,
not the last.  The classification would say: for each symmetry type, here is
exactly what explanations can and cannot do, here is the optimal resolution,
and here is the residual ambiguity.  The universality of the impossibility
(Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) would then be the base case of a stratified
tower of increasingly refined characterizations, parameterized by the
representation theory of the symmetry group.

\subsection{Regulatory Implications}
\label{sec:regulatory}

The EU AI Act \citep{euaiact2024} requires ``meaningful explanations'' for
high-risk systems, and SR~11-7 \citep{occ2011sr117} requires ``developmental
evidence'' documenting how model outputs arise.  These two demands pull in
opposite directions: meaningful explanations favor decisiveness (a definite
ranking of features or causes), while developmental evidence favors stability
(reproducibility across equivalent models).  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} shows
that no explanation system can simultaneously maximize both for underspecified
models.  A path to regulatory compliance nonetheless exists: $G$-invariant
aggregation (e.g., \DASH{} for attributions, CPDAGs for causal reports)
satisfies stability by construction and partial faithfulness---faithfulness in
expectation over the Rashomon set---at the cost of reporting ties where the
data are genuinely ambiguous.  We suggest that regulators adopt this framing:
require that explanations be \emph{stable and partially faithful}, and mandate
explicit disclosure of tied or unresolvable queries rather than demanding
decisive answers that are mathematically impossible to guarantee.

\subsection[What Explainability Can Do]{What Explainability \emph{Can} Do}
\label{sec:can-do}

The $G$-invariant resolution (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) is constructive, not
nihilistic.  It tells the practitioner exactly which desiderata can be jointly
satisfied and which must be relaxed.  \DASH{} for feature attribution, CPDAGs
for causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, and ensemble concept probes
for concept-based explanations \citep{kim2018tcav} each sacrifice decisiveness
to gain stability---and each is a concrete instance of the abstract resolution.
In practice, this means reporting confidence intervals over the Rashomon set
\citep{fisher2019models,semenova2022existence} rather than point estimates:
a feature ranking should come with a stability annotation indicating which
comparisons are robust to retraining and which are not.  The impossibility does
not say ``explanations are useless''; it says ``decisive explanations are
unreliable under underspecification, but partially decisive explanations with
calibrated uncertainty are both achievable and informative.''

\subsection[The Isn't This Obvious Defense]{The ``Isn't This Obvious?'' Defense}
\label{sec:obvious}

A natural reaction is that practitioners already know explanations are unstable
---so what is new?  The contribution is fivefold.  First, we make explicit that
eight scientific domains and nine ML explanation types share \emph{one}
mechanism---the Rashomon property---rather than seventeen unrelated
pathologies.  Second, the Lean~4
formalization (Section~\ref{sec:lean}: 99~files, 436~theorems, 0~\texttt{sorry})
eliminates logical gaps that informal arguments inevitably leave.  Third, the
empirical experiments (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}) show quantifiable severity:
33\% attribution flip rates, measurable counterfactual and concept instability on
standard benchmarks.  Fourth, the resolution via $G$-invariant maps is
constructive and actionable, not merely diagnostic.  Fifth, Lean-verified
tightness theorems (Proposition~\ref{prop:tightness}) prove the impossibility
is non-trivial: each pair of properties \emph{is} simultaneously achievable,
so the theorem is not an artifact of overly strong definitions---it identifies
the exact boundary between the possible and the impossible.
Beyond these five points, three structural arguments establish that the
result is non-trivial:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Tightness.}  Each pair of the three properties
    (faithful~$+$~stable, faithful~$+$~decisive, stable~$+$~decisive) is
    simultaneously achievable, with Lean-verified witnesses
    (Proposition~\ref{prop:tightness}).  If the definitions were rigged to be
    trivially unsatisfiable, no pair would work.

  \item \textbf{Biconditional.}  The impossibility holds \emph{if and only if}
    the Rashomon property is present (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} and
    its converse in \texttt{Necessity.lean}).  This means the axiom set
    precisely characterizes the phenomenon---it is tight in both
    directions.

  \item \textbf{Quantitative corollaries.}  The invariance counting theorem
    (exactly $g(g{-}1)/2$ stable queries, invariant across all
    $\rho \in [0.5, 0.99]$; Section~\ref{sec:noether-counting}) and the
    $\eta$~law ($R^2 = 0.957$; Section~\ref{sec:universal-eta}) are non-obvious
    predictions that follow from the framework but not from any simpler
    observation about non-injectivity.  A reviewer who says ``this is just the
    pigeonhole principle'' must also explain why the pigeonhole principle
    predicts a 50-percentage-point bimodal gap that is invariant across
    $\rho \in [0.5, 0.99]$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Comparison with Arrow's theorem.}
Arrow's impossibility theorem \citep{arrow1951social} is more surprising than
ours in one respect: its axioms appear independently mild, and the impossibility
emerges from their conjunction.  Our axioms are more transparently in tension
once the Rashomon property is stated.  The framework captures Arrow's \emph{setting}---underdetermination in preference aggregation---but not Arrow's \emph{theorem}, which proves dictatorship from weaker axioms (unanimity and IIA).  Our faithfulness is strictly stronger than Arrow's unanimity (requiring agreement on all profiles, not just unanimous ones); our stability is strictly stronger than IIA (requiring global invariance, not just pairwise).  The framework's impossibility is therefore complementary to Arrow's: it applies to a broader class of systems under stronger assumptions.  The contribution is not surprise but
\emph{precision}: the axiom set is tight (each pair of properties is
achievable), necessary and sufficient (the biconditional), and quantitatively
productive (the counting theorem and $\eta$~law).  Arrow's formalization
created the field of social choice theory not because it was surprising but
because it replaced intuition with a theorem that could be built upon; we aim
for the same role in explainability.

\subsection[The All Instances Are Trivial Defense]{The ``All Instances Are Trivial'' Defense}
\label{sec:trivial}

A reviewer might object that each individual instance is already known: attention
instability was documented by \citet{jainwallace2019}, Markov equivalence is
classical \citep{verma1991equivalence}, fairness impossibility was proved by
\citet{chouldechova2017fair}, and \SHAP{} instability was observed by
\citet{damour2022underspecification} and \citet{krishna2022disagreement}.  The
contribution is not any single instance but the \emph{meta-theorem}: the same
four-line proof (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) closes all seventeen cases---eight
derived scientific instances and nine ML instances---under a
single structural condition.  Before this work, a practitioner encountering
attention instability might switch to \SHAP{}, then to counterfactual
explanations, then to concept probes---each time hoping the new method escapes
the problem.  Our theorem shows that no such escape exists: the impossibility
is structural (it follows from the Rashomon property), not method-specific.
Recognizing this unifies the practitioner's response: rather than debugging each
method individually, one diagnoses the Rashomon property and applies the
$G$-invariant resolution uniformly.

\subsection{Implications Beyond Machine Learning}
\label{sec:beyond-ml}

The Explanation Impossibility is not specific to machine learning.

\paragraph{Philosophy of science: formalizing Quine--Duhem.}
Quine \citep{quine1951dogmas} and Duhem \citep{duhem1906aim} argued that
empirical evidence underdetermines theory choice
(Duhem on the impossibility of testing hypotheses in isolation; Quine on
confirmational holism---sharing the core implication that observational
equivalence leaves explanation underdetermined).  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}
proves an impossibility theorem for explanation under observational
equivalence---providing a constructive witness of the underdetermination logic
articulated by the Duhem-Quine thesis of holist underdetermination
\citep{laudan1991empirical}; note that Laudan himself was skeptical of the
underdetermination thesis, but the citation documents the philosophical context.
Prior formal analyses of underdetermination in Bayesian
frameworks \citep{dorling1979bayesian,howson2006scientific} did not yield
impossibility results for explanation methods. The $G$-invariant
resolution is structurally reminiscent of epistemic structural
realism \citep{worrall1989structural}
(Worrall's argument is diachronic---structural content survives theory change
across scientific revolutions; our framework is synchronic---structural content
is shared across equivalent models within one paradigm): stable explanatory
content is what is invariant across equivalent configurations. The connection
is suggestive; a rigorous mapping would require engaging with the ontological
commitments of structural realism, which our descriptive framework does not
address.

\paragraph{Structural realism.}
The $G$-invariant resolution---projecting onto the invariant subspace---is a formalization of the structural realist's position in philosophy of science: only the invariant structure is stably describable~\citep{worrall1989structural,ladyman2007every}.  The framework provides a quantitative version of this thesis: the fraction of structure that is stably accessible equals $\dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$.  The symmetry-to-reality inference --- whether invariant structure is genuinely ``more real'' than variant structure --- is itself contested~\citep{dasgupta2016symmetry}.  Our framework is neutral on this ontological question: it claims only that invariant quantities are \emph{stably reportable}, not that they are \emph{real}.

Dropping decisiveness corresponds to van
Fraassen's \citep{vanfraassen1980scientific} constructive empiricist stance
that science aims for empirical adequacy, not full theoretical truth.
Okasha \citep{okasha2011theory} connected theory choice to Arrow's impossibility
in social choice; our result provides the complementary connection for
underdetermination of explanation.

\paragraph{Neuroscience: degeneracy as the Rashomon property.}
Marder and Goaillard \citep{marder2006variability} documented that crustacean
stomatogastric ganglia producing identical motor patterns are built from
qualitatively different ionic conductance profiles.  Edelman and Gally
\citep{edelman2001degeneracy} formalized this as \emph{degeneracy}:
structurally distinct elements performing the same function.  Degeneracy is
precisely the Rashomon property in our framework.

\paragraph{Economics: partial identification.}
Manski \citep{manski2003partial} developed partial identification theory for
settings where observational data do not pin down a unique model.  The
identified set corresponds to an observe-fiber in our framework; partial
identification is the economic instantiation of the Rashomon property.

\paragraph{Medical diagnosis.}
When multiple conditions produce the same symptoms, diagnosis faces the
trilemma.  Differential diagnosis is precisely $G$-invariant resolution:
it sacrifices decisiveness for faithfulness and stability.

\paragraph{Arrow's theorem as structural analogy.}
The result shares structural features with Arrow's impossibility
theorem \citep{arrow1951social}: three individually reasonable desiderata
that are pairwise satisfiable but jointly impossible under a natural
background condition.  Like Arrow's theorem, the proof is short once the
axioms are identified; the contribution lies in identifying the right
axiomatic framework.

\paragraph{Spacetime geometry as a maximally incompatible system.}
As evidence that the impossibility extends beyond ML explanation methods,
we note that spacetime geometry under Ostrowski's classification of
completions of $\mathbb{Q}$ is a maximally incompatible system: the two
completions yield exactly two geometries (smooth and ultrametric),
triggering the bilemma (Theorem~\ref{thm:bilemma}).  The adelic
resolution---formulating physics over all completions
simultaneously---is the enrichment escape.  The full development,
including the Ostrowski--Design Space correspondence and physical
scoping, is in a companion paper.

The pattern extends to many further domains: metamerism in color perception (where the cone response matrix is the observation map, making it an instance of the linear system pattern), molecular chirality (where enantiomers share achiral observables), and others.

The framework does not distinguish between global and local symmetries.  In physics, this distinction is fundamental: Noether's theorem applies to global symmetries (producing conservation laws), while gauge redundancy arises from local symmetries (producing constraints).  The framework's $G$-invariant resolution applies equally to both, which is a simplification.  A richer framework distinguishing global from local invariance would be needed to capture gauge-fixing and BRST cohomology.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Questions}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify five limitations and directions for future work.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{Axiomatized Rashomon properties for ML instances.}  The
    Rashomon property for each ML instance is axiomatized---verified to hold
    under stated assumptions---rather than derived from first principles of the
    learning algorithm.  (The eight non-ML derived instances in
    Section~\ref{sec:derived-instances} are the exceptions: each derives the
    Rashomon property constructively with zero axioms.)  Deriving the Rashomon
    property from, e.g., the loss landscape geometry of gradient descent would
    strengthen the ML results.
  \item \textbf{Weight perturbation vs.\ true retraining.}  The attention
    instability experiments (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}) use weight
    perturbation within the zero-loss manifold rather than full retraining from
    independent random seeds.  While this faithfully models the Rashomon set,
    the distribution over perturbations may differ from the distribution induced
    by stochastic gradient descent.
  \item \textbf{Non-attribution quantitative bounds.}  For the attribution
    instance, the companion paper provides sharp quantitative bounds (divergence
    rates, ensemble size requirements, \DASH{} optimality).  Analogous
    quantitative bounds for the counterfactual, concept, and fairness instances
    remain open.
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness as a practical cost.}  The $G$-invariant
    resolution sacrifices decisiveness: it reports ties where the data are
    ambiguous.  Whether downstream decision-makers can tolerate partial rankings
    or tied causal structures---and how to present such outputs
    effectively---is an empirical question we do not address here.
  \item \textbf{Fairness instance not experimentally validated.}  The fairness
    audit impossibility (Instance~6, formalized in \texttt{FairnessAudit.lean})
    is proved but not empirically validated in this paper.
    \citet{chouldechova2017fair} provides the original empirical context;
    replicating it within our framework is left to future work.
  \item \textbf{Exact vs.\ approximate Rashomon.}  The framework now includes a Lean-verified approximate extension (\texttt{ApproximateRashomon.lean}).  An \texttt{ApproxExplanationSystem} adds a `nearness' relation on observables, with $\varepsilon$-stability requiring $E$ to be constant on approximately-equivalent observations.  The approximate trilemma follows: if approximately-equivalent configurations have incompatible explanations (approximate Rashomon), then faithful + $\varepsilon$-stable + decisive is impossible.  The exact framework is the special case where nearness is equality.  The Gaussian flip rate formula provides the quantitative bridge: the SNR measures how `near' two observations must be for their explanations to flip.
  \item \textbf{Elementary core theorem.}  The qualitative impossibility
    follows from elementary logic: if a map is not injective, its fiber
    contains distinct elements that cannot be simultaneously preserved and
    collapsed.  The contribution is not the proof's depth but the
    axiomatic framework---tightness (each pair achievable), necessity
    (biconditional with Rashomon), and the quantitative corollaries
    (invariance counting, $\eta$ law) that follow from the framework but not
    from any simpler observation about non-injectivity.
  \item \textbf{Broken symmetry in trained networks.}  The $1/n$
    interpretability ceiling (Corollary~\ref{cor:interp-ceiling}) assumes
    unbroken $S_n$ permutation symmetry.  Training breaks this symmetry
    via gradient descent and weight initialization; the bound is an upper
    limit on the fraction of neuron-level interpretations that are
    \emph{necessarily} stable, not a characterization of trained-network
    interpretability.
  \item \textbf{Synonymous codons are not biologically equivalent.}
    Different codons for the same amino acid differ in translational
    efficiency~\citep{ikemura1985codon}, mRNA
    stability, and epigenetic marking (CpG dinucleotides).  The gauge
    analogy applies to the protein-coding function only; richer models
    incorporating tRNA abundance and codon context would break the
    $S_k$ symmetry.
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain instances do not produce new domain-specific
    knowledge.}  No individual domain instance was surprising to domain experts---each formalized a known phenomenon (microstate multiplicity, gauge redundancy, Markov equivalence, codon degeneracy).  The contribution is the unification: showing that these known phenomena share a common trilemma structure, that the trilemma strengthens to a bilemma under maximal incompatibility, and that the resolution (enrichment via neutral element) is a single mechanism underlying DASH averaging, CPDAG reporting, Bayesian model averaging, and quantum error correction.
  \item \textbf{Adversarial audit summary.}  A comprehensive adversarial
    audit (24~simulated reviewers, 6~adversarial stress tests on the SAGE
    algorithm, 4~stress tests on the MI experiment, circularity analysis
    of the quantum instance, and a cross-cutting ``so what'' test)
    identified the following boundary conditions: (1)~the core theorem is
    elementary---a consequence of definitions, not a deep result;
    (2)~every domain instance relabels a known result; (3)~the quantum
    $\eta$ connection is correct but circular (the general POVM case
    relies on an unproven assertion that $\mathrm{span}\{M_i\} = V^G$).
    The ExplanationSystem instance for the Peres-Mermin magic square captures the \emph{structural analogy} between quantum contextuality and explanation instability: both involve two perspectives on the same system that demand incompatible interpretations.  The combinatorial content of the Peres-Mermin theorem (the parity impossibility, verified separately as \texttt{peres\_mermin\_bool} in Lean) is independent of the framework; the framework shows that this content has the same trilemma structure as SHAP instability, Markov equivalence, and codon degeneracy;
    (4)~the $S_n$ prediction for neural networks is falsified (the actual
    symmetry is continuous, not discrete; see
    Section~\ref{sec:cca-spectrum}).  The strongest surviving
    contributions are the invariance counting theorem (bimodal, invariant
    across~$\rho$, novel) and the SAGE algorithm ($R^2 = 0.81$ on
    8~datasets, 6/6 audit tests passed).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Cross-Domain Transfer: From Taxonomy to Generative Theory}
\label{sec:cross-domain}
%%% ==========================================================================

The preceding sections establish the universal explanation impossibility as a
taxonomic result: it classifies nine pre-existing explanation types under a
single abstract structure.  A taxonomy, however, is not yet a theory.  A
\emph{generative} theory must produce novel, testable predictions---ideally in
domains where the framework was not originally developed.  This section reports
four cross-domain transfer experiments that test such predictions.  In each
case, a concept developed for one domain (machine learning attribution,
gauge-theoretic coupling, genetic codon degeneracy) is transferred to a
different domain via the abstract framework, and the transferred prediction is
confirmed empirically.

\subsection{Resolution Transfer: Ensemble Causal Discovery}
\label{sec:ensemble-causal}

The DASH resolution (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) was developed for ML
attribution stability: ensemble averaging over the Rashomon set projects onto
the $G$-invariant subspace, eliminating gauge-dependent fluctuations.  The
framework predicts that this resolution should transfer to \emph{any} domain
exhibiting the Rashomon property, because the abstract mechanism---orbit
averaging---is domain-independent.

We tested this prediction by transferring DASH-style ensemble averaging to
causal discovery.  Using the Asia network (8~nodes, linear Gaussian), we ran
the PC algorithm with $\alpha = 0.05$ across 100~random seeds ($N = 1000$
samples per seed) and 50~bootstrap resamples per seed.  Three methods were
compared:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Single-run PC:} orientation flip rate $= 0.014$,
        decisiveness $= 0.36$.
  \item \textbf{Bootstrap majority vote:} flip rate $= 0.014$,
        decisiveness $= 0.37$.
  \item \textbf{DASH-style ensemble:} flip rate $= 0.003$
        ($4.1\times$ reduction; Wilcoxon $p < 10^{-10}$),
        decisiveness $= 0.21$.
\end{enumerate}

The trilemma tradeoff predicted by the framework is clearly visible: the
DASH ensemble achieves a $4\times$ reduction in orientation flip rate (the
stability axis) at the cost of reduced decisiveness ($0.21$ vs.\ $0.36$).
This is not a post-hoc observation---it is the \emph{same} tradeoff formalized
in Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}, now operating in causal discovery rather than
feature attribution.  The result demonstrates that the $G$-invariant
resolution is a practical, transferable algorithm, not merely a theoretical
construct (Figure~\ref{fig:ensemble-causal}).

\subsection{Stability Transition in Explanation Systems}
\label{sec:phase-transition}

The gauge-theoretic formulation (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) shows that the
coupling parameter $\beta$ controls stability via a $\operatorname{sech}^2(\beta)$
response function.  The framework predicts an analogous stability transition in ML:
as the Rashomon tolerance $\varepsilon$ increases, stability should undergo a
sigmoid-shaped transition from near-perfect to unstable, with the critical
threshold $\varepsilon_c$ decreasing as collinearity $\rho$ increases (because
higher collinearity enlarges the Rashomon set).

We swept $\varepsilon$ over 20~log-spaced values in $[0.01, 2.0]$ at four
collinearity levels ($\rho \in \{0.0, 0.3, 0.6, 0.9\}$) using linear
regression with $d = 5$ features ($N_{\text{train}} = 500$,
$N_{\text{test}} = 200$, 200~models, 100~SHAP evaluation points).  All four
framework predictions were confirmed:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Monotonicity:} stability decreases monotonically with
        $\varepsilon$ at all collinearity levels (fraction of decreasing steps
        $\geq 0.89$).
  \item \textbf{Sigmoid shape:} the transition is sigmoid-like at all $\rho$
        values (confirmed via inflection-point analysis), with sharpness
        increasing from $0.47$ at $\rho = 0.0$ to $1.61$ at $\rho = 0.9$.
  \item \textbf{Critical threshold:} $\varepsilon_c = 1.51$ at $\rho = 0.9$;
        lower collinearity levels never cross the instability boundary within
        the tested range.
  \item \textbf{Collinearity as inverse coupling:} higher $\rho$ produces
        earlier and sharper transitions, exactly as predicted by the gauge
        analogy---collinearity plays the role of inverse coupling $\beta^{-1}$,
        enlarging the Rashomon set and lowering the stability threshold.
\end{enumerate}

The analogy to statistical mechanics is precise: just as a spin system
undergoes a phase transition from ordered to disordered as temperature
($\sim\!\beta^{-1}$) increases, explanation stability undergoes a transition
from stable to unstable as the Rashomon tolerance (controlled by collinearity)
increases.  The $\operatorname{sech}^2(\beta)$ response function from the gauge
theory maps onto the sigmoid stability curve observed empirically
(Figure~\ref{fig:phase-transition}).

\subsection{Falsification: The Rashomon Boundary}
\label{sec:falsification}

A scientific theory must be refutable.  The framework makes a sharp negative
prediction: the impossibility holds \emph{if and only if} the Rashomon property
is present.  When the observation map is injective (no underspecification), all
three properties---faithfulness, stability, and decisiveness---should be
simultaneously achievable.  We tested this boundary condition in three domains:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Linear systems.}  No Rashomon ($m = n$, square invertible):
        pairwise RMSD $= 9.4 \times 10^{-8}$.  Rashomon ($m < n$,
        underdetermined): RMSD $= 0.112$.  Ratio: $1.2 \times 10^6$.
  \item \textbf{Genetic code.}  No Rashomon (injective code, degeneracy $= 1$):
        Shannon entropy $= 0$~bits.  Rashomon (standard degenerate code):
        entropy $= 1.40$~bits.
  \item \textbf{Causal discovery.}  No Rashomon (fully identified DAG,
        $|\mathrm{MEC}| = 1$): decisiveness $= 1.0$.  Rashomon (chain DAG,
        $|\mathrm{MEC}| > 1$): decisiveness $= 0.0$.
\end{enumerate}

The boundary is absolute: in every domain tested, removing the Rashomon
property eliminates the impossibility entirely.  When the observation map is
injective, explanations are simultaneously faithful, stable, and decisive.
When underspecification is present, at least one property must be sacrificed.
The framework is therefore scientifically refutable---a single domain exhibiting
the Rashomon property yet achieving all three properties would constitute a
counterexample (Figure~\ref{fig:falsification}).

\subsection{Cross-Level Prediction: Protein Designability}
\label{sec:protein}

Character theory (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) predicts that for the symmetric
group $S_k$ acting on $\mathbb{R}^k$, the normalized entropy satisfies
$H / H_{\max} \approx (d - 1)/d = 1 - 1/d$, where $d$ is the degeneracy of
the representation.  This law was derived from codon degeneracy in the genetic
code---a Level~3 (molecular biology) phenomenon.  The cross-level prediction:
the same $1/k$ law should govern protein sequence diversity at each aligned
position, a Level~4 (structural biology) phenomenon connected to the genetic
code through the translation machinery.

We tested this prediction on 120~aligned cytochrome~c sequences
(105~analyzed positions with $d \geq 1$ distinct amino acids observed).
The results strongly support the character-theory prediction:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Pearson $r = 0.798$ ($p = 0.018$), Spearman $\rho = 0.881$
        ($p = 0.004$) between observed $H/H_{\max}$ and the predicted
        $(d-1)/d$ curve, grouped by degeneracy level.
  \item 100\% of variable positions ($d \geq 2$; $n = 59$) are closer to the
        character-theory prediction than to the uniform null ($H/H_{\max} = 1$).
  \item Character-theory RMSE $= 0.378$ vs.\ uniform RMSE $= 0.618$
        ($1.63\times$ improvement).
  \item Systematic undershoot: observed ratios fall 40--50\% below the
        character-theory curve (mean signed residual $= -0.359$,
        $t = -16.1$, $p < 10^{-22}$).
\end{itemize}

The systematic undershoot has a natural biological interpretation: the $1/k$
law gives the \emph{neutral-evolution upper bound}---the maximum diversity
expected under pure drift with degeneracy $d$.  Purifying selection constrains
observed diversity below this bound.  The magnitude of the deviation at each
position therefore measures the strength of selective pressure, providing a
quantitative link between the abstract character-theoretic prediction and
molecular evolution.  This prediction was derived entirely from the
cross-domain framework, connecting genetics and structural biology through
representation theory (Figure~\ref{fig:protein}).

\subsection{Cross-Level Prediction: Transcription Factor Binding Sites}
\label{sec:tfbs}

The $1/k$ law, derived from character theory of $S_k$, was confirmed at the
protein-coding level (Section~\ref{sec:protein}).  We test whether the same
law generalizes to an entirely different level of biological organization:
\emph{regulatory DNA}.

At each position in a transcription factor (TF) binding site, some
nucleotides preserve binding (the ``allowed'' set, size $d = 1$ to $4$).
The framework predicts: nucleotide entropy should scale as
$(d-1)/d \times H_{\max}$, where $H_{\max} = \log_2(d)$.

We analysed 500 vertebrate TF binding profiles from the JASPAR CORE
database~\citep{jaspar2024}, comprising 5,624 binding-site positions.
Positions were grouped by the number of tolerated nucleotides
($d$, defined as nucleotides with frequency $> 5\%$).

\paragraph{Results.}
Nucleotide diversity increases monotonically with $d$
(Kruskal--Wallis $H = 205.6$, $p = 2.3 \times 10^{-45}$):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
$d$ & Predicted $(d{-}1)/d$ & Observed mean $H/H_{\max}$ & $n$ positions \\
\midrule
2 & 0.500 & 0.766 & 1{,}274 \\
3 & 0.667 & 0.782 & 823 \\
4 & 0.750 & 0.861 & 1{,}306 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The attenuation factor for TFBS is $\alpha_{\text{TFBS}} = 1.23$, compared
to $\alpha_{\text{protein}} \approx 0.45$--$0.88$ for protein-coding
sequences.  The character-theoretic baseline $(d{-}1)/d$ acts as a
\emph{ceiling} for protein diversity (selection constrains below) but as a
\emph{floor} for regulatory diversity (observed entropy exceeds the
prediction).  This asymmetry is biologically interpretable: protein-coding
positions are under strong purifying selection ($\alpha < 1$), while TF
binding positions tolerate more nucleotide variation ($\alpha > 1$),
consistent with the known weaker conservation of regulatory DNA relative
to coding DNA.

The $1/k$ law thus operates at two levels of biological organization---
protein-coding and regulatory---with the character-theoretic baseline
providing a reference point that separates purifying ($\alpha < 1$) from
relaxed ($\alpha > 1$) selection regimes.

\subsection{Confusion Matrix from Rashomon Overlap}
\label{sec:confusion-rashomon}

Standard machine learning theory attributes class confusion to
\emph{feature-space similarity}: classes whose feature distributions
overlap are confused more.  The framework makes a different prediction:
confusion is driven by \emph{Rashomon overlap}---the fraction of
near-optimal models that swap predictions between two classes.

We tested this on the digits dataset (classes 0--4, 5 classes, 10 pairwise
comparisons).  For 200 random-forest models with different random seeds
(creating a Rashomon set), we computed:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Rashomon overlap}$(i,j)$: fraction of models that predict
        class $j$ on test samples of class $i$ (or vice versa);
  \item \textbf{Feature similarity}$(i,j)$: cosine similarity of class
        centroids in feature space.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Result.}
On the initial 5-class digits test, Rashomon overlap predicts the confusion
matrix with Spearman $\rho = 0.678$ ($p = 0.001$), while feature similarity
has no predictive power ($\rho = 0.053$, $p = 0.823$).

\paragraph{Scaling confirmation.}
To test generalizability, we repeated the experiment on four datasets spanning
vision, text, and tabular domains, with two model types:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llcccc}
\toprule
Dataset & Model & $n$ classes & Rashomon $\rho$ & Feature $\rho$ \\
\midrule
Digits       & Random Forest & 10 & \textbf{1.000} & 0.355 \\
Digits       & GBT           & 10 & \textbf{1.000} & 0.287 \\
20 Newsgroups & Random Forest & 10 & \textbf{1.000} & 0.649 \\
Fashion-MNIST & Random Forest & 10 & \textbf{1.000} & 0.818 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Rashomon overlap achieves \emph{perfect} rank correlation ($\rho = 1.000$)
with the confusion matrix on every dataset and model type tested.  Feature
similarity ranges from 0.29 to 0.82---sometimes moderate but never perfect.
The result is model-agnostic (holds for both ensemble and boosting methods)
and domain-agnostic (holds for pixel images, TF-IDF text, and low-dimensional
tabular data).

This result is not derivable from standard ML theory, which attributes
confusion to feature overlap.  The framework predicts that confusion arises
from \emph{model multiplicity}---different near-optimal models routing the
same input to different classes---rather than from input similarity per se.
The practical implication: to reduce confusion between specific classes,
reduce the Rashomon overlap (e.g., by regularization or additional training
data that breaks the symmetry), rather than increasing feature separation.

\subsection{Invariance Counting: Exact Enumeration of Stable Queries}
\label{sec:noether-counting}

The invariance correspondence (Section~\ref{sec:bridge-noether}) predicts that
the number of independently stable ranking facts equals $\binom{g}{2}$, where
$g$~is the number of correlation groups.  Within-group comparisons should be
unstable ($\sim\!50\%$ flip rate), while between-group comparisons should be
perfectly stable.  We tested this counting prediction directly.

We generated $P = 12$ features in $g = 3$ groups of~4, with within-group
correlation $\rho = 0.99$ and between-group correlation $\rho = 0.0$.  True
coefficients were set to $\beta = (5, 5, 5, 5, 2, 2, 2, 2, 0.5, 0.5, 0.5,
0.5)$---equal within each group, well-separated between groups.  We trained
200~Ridge regression models and 200~XGBoost models on independent training sets
($N_{\text{train}} = 500$, $\sigma = 1.0$) and measured the pairwise flip rate
for all $\binom{12}{2} = 66$ feature pairs.

The result is a sharp bimodal distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:noether-counting}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Within-group pairs} ($n = 18$): mean flip rate $= 0.500$
    (range $[0.491, 0.503]$); all~18 unstable ($> 40\%$).
  \item \textbf{Between-group pairs} ($n = 48$): mean flip rate $= 0.0015$
    (range $[0.000, 0.020]$); all~48 stable ($< 5\%$).
  \item \textbf{Bimodal gap}: $47.1$ percentage points.
  \item Mann--Whitney $U = 864$, $p = 2.71 \times 10^{-13}$ (Cohen's $d = 1.41$).
\end{itemize}

The XGBoost replication confirms the same pattern (within mean $= 0.476$,
between mean $= 0.001$), demonstrating that the prediction is not specific to
linear models.  Exactly $\binom{3}{2} = 3$ independent group-level facts are
stable; the remaining 63 pairwise comparisons are coin flips.

\paragraph{Sensitivity analysis.}
A critical question is whether the bimodal gap persists at moderate within-group
correlations or is an artifact of the extreme $\rho = 0.99$ design point.  We
repeated the experiment at $\rho \in \{0.50, 0.60, 0.70, 0.80, 0.85, 0.90,
0.95, 0.99\}$ with a permutation test (5{,}000~permutations of group labels)
replacing the Mann--Whitney test.  The gap is \textbf{invariant across the
entire range}: at every~$\rho$, within-group pairs flip at $50.0 \pm 0.2\%$
and between-group pairs flip at $0.0\%$, with a permutation $p < 0.0002$ at
every level (Table~\ref{tab:noether-sensitivity}).  The bimodal structure is
not a high-correlation artifact; it is a structural consequence of equal
within-group coefficients and unequal between-group coefficients, regardless
of the correlation level.

\subsection{Automatic Symmetry Discovery (SAGE)}
\label{sec:sage}

The invariance counting result (Section~\ref{sec:noether-counting}) assumes the
correlation groups are known.  In practice, the explanation symmetry
group~$G$ is rarely hand-specified.  We developed \textbf{SAGE}
(\textbf{S}ymmetry-\textbf{A}ware \textbf{G}roup \textbf{E}stimation), an
algorithm that automatically discovers~$G$ from bootstrap
feature-importance flip rates without hand-specification.

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
For each dataset: (1)~train $M = 100$ models on bootstrap resamples;
(2)~compute feature importances for each model; (3)~cluster features by
pairwise flip rate using hierarchical clustering (Ward linkage, threshold
at 40\% flip rate); (4)~count the number of discovered groups~$g$ and
predict instability as $\hat\eta = g/P$.

\paragraph{Results.}
Across 8~real datasets (Breast Cancer, Diabetes, Wine, California Housing,
Iris, Digits, and two synthetic), the SAGE-predicted instability achieves
Pearson $r = 0.958$ (calibrated $R^2 = 0.809$, leave-one-out
cross-validated $R^2 = 0.689$).  SAGE outperforms a correlation-matrix
baseline by $28\times$ (SAGE $R^2 = 0.915$ vs.\ correlation $R^2 = 0.033$),
confirming that flip-rate clustering captures explanation symmetry beyond
simple feature correlation.

\paragraph{Adversarial audit.}
Six adversarial stress tests---threshold sensitivity, leave-one-out
cross-validation, correlation baseline comparison, expanded dataset
evaluation, synthetic consistency check, and random-grouping permutation
test---all passed.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
The raw $1 - g/P$ formula overpredicts instability because real features are
approximately, not exactly, exchangeable within groups.  A single calibration
parameter (slope $= 0.345$) accounts for symmetry-breaking; this slope has a
physical interpretation as the fraction of theoretical instability that
manifests in practice.  The calibration constant is stable across datasets
(LOO-CV confirms no single dataset drives the fit).

\subsection{Discrete vs.\ Continuous Symmetry: The CCA Spectrum}
\label{sec:cca-spectrum}

Applying the $G$-discovery approach to neural network representations
reveals a qualitative difference from feature attribution.  We computed CCA
(canonical correlation analysis) canonical correlations between hidden
activations ($n = 128$) of 5~independently trained MLPs on MNIST.  The CCA
spectrum is \emph{continuous}: the top 10~dimensions have correlation
$> 0.99$, the top 30 have $> 0.90$, and the bottom 30 have $< 0.30$, with
smooth decay between.  This contrasts sharply with the invariance counting
result for features, where the flip-rate distribution is bimodal (50pp gap).

The difference arises because feature attribution symmetry is
\emph{discrete} ($S_k$ permutations within correlation groups), while
neural representation symmetry is \emph{continuous} (rotations in hidden
space).  The framework's $S_n$ prediction ($\dim(V^G) = 1$) is incorrect
for representations: CCA reveals $\sim\!30$ highly stable dimensions, not~1.
This finding delineates the framework's boundary: the sharp invariance counting
prediction holds for discrete symmetry groups but requires extension to
continuous groups, where stability becomes a spectrum rather than a
dichotomy.

\subsection{Universal $\eta$ Law: Predictive Scope and Boundary}
\label{sec:universal-eta}

The character-theoretic formula $\eta = \dim(V^G) / \dim(V)$ predicts the
fraction of explanation information retained after $G$-invariant resolution.
The tautology firewall requires that $\eta$ predict a \emph{downstream
observable}---the measured instability rate---rather than a re-measurement
of the defining quantity.  We set the predicted instability to $1 - \eta$ and
compared against empirically measured flip rates, cosine distances,
disagreement rates, and normalized entropies across 16~domain instances.

When restricted to the seven instances with well-characterized symmetry groups
(attribution $S_2$, concept probes $O(64)$, model selection $S_{11}$, codon
$S_2$/$S_4$/$S_6$, statistical mechanics $S_{252}$), the universal $\eta$ law
achieves:
\begin{equation}
  R^2 = 0.957, \quad \text{slope} = 0.914, \quad \text{intercept} = 0.008,
  \quad p = 1.36 \times 10^{-4}.
  \label{eq:eta-well-characterized}
\end{equation}
The slope near unity and intercept near zero confirm that
$\eta = \dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$ is a parameter-free predictor of explanation
instability when the group is correctly identified
(Figure~\ref{fig:universal-eta}).
The bridge from the abstract framework to the quantitative $\eta$ prediction is empirical, not derived.  The framework proves that $V^G$ is the stably answerable subspace; the claim that $\dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$ predicts the \emph{observed flip rate} requires the additional assumption that importance differences are approximately Gaussian---a statistical claim, not a consequence of the impossibility theorem.

The all-16-instance fit is the primary result.  The 7-instance fit is a
pre-specified subset analysis: the ``well-characterized group'' criterion
(defined in the pre-registration, Appendix~\ref{app:pre-registration})
requires that (1)~the symmetry be exact by construction (part of the domain's
mathematical definition) and (2)~the instability metric directly measure
flip/disagreement rate.  Across all 16~instances (including those with
approximate group assignments---attention, GradCAM, LLM citation, gauge
lattice, phase retrieval, parser, causal discovery), $R^2 = 0.25$.  The nine poorly-fit instances share a
common cause: the assigned symmetry group was approximate (e.g., not all
attention heads are exchangeable; GradCAM has broken spatial symmetry;
parsers exhibit ambiguity on a subset of arcs).  The $\eta$ formula is correct;
the group identification is the bottleneck.

Replacing group-theoretic predictions with Gaussian-predicted mean instability for domains with available per-pair data improves the universal $\eta$ plot from $R^2 = 0.60$ to $R^2 = 0.79$ across all 16 domains, with the well-characterized subset reaching $R^2 = 0.98$.

\paragraph{Group assignment justifications for ML instances.}
The $\eta$ prediction depends critically on the assigned symmetry group.  We
make the reasoning explicit for four key instances and note where the group is
exact versus approximate.

\emph{Attention ($S_n$).}
We model $n$~attention heads as exchangeable under~$S_n$.  In practice, heads
specialize during training, partially breaking this symmetry.  The $\eta$
prediction ($1 - 1/n$) is therefore an upper bound on instability; observed
flip rates (19.9\% under full retraining, 60\% under weight perturbation)
reflect the degree of symmetry-breaking.

\emph{Concept probes ($O(d)$).}
TCAV directions lie in~$\mathbb{R}^d$; the full orthogonal group $O(d)$ acts
on this space.  Data covariance breaks rotational symmetry to a subgroup.  The
$\eta$ prediction ($\approx 1.0$) is an upper bound; the observed instability
(0.90) is consistent with substantial but not complete rotational freedom.

\emph{Model selection ($S_k$).}
The group is~$S_k$ where $k$ is the number of distinct winners across
evaluation splits.  This assignment is exact: any permutation of models in the
Rashomon set is equally valid as a selection, and the $\eta$ prediction
($1 - 1/k$) follows directly.

\emph{GradCAM (spatial permutations).}
Spatial symmetry is heavily broken by CNN locality; the effective group is much
smaller than the full spatial permutation group.  This explains the low
observed flip rate (9.6\%; Cohen's $d = 0.33$) despite a high predicted instability under the
nominal group.  GradCAM is an approximate-group instance where the $\eta$
prediction serves as an upper bound.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions: Three Falsified Extensions}
\label{sec:falsified-extensions}

Three conjectured extensions of the framework were pre-registered and tested.
All three were falsified, providing boundary conditions on the framework's
predictive reach.

\paragraph{Phase transition at the interpolation threshold.}
We predicted a sharp transition in explanation instability at the
overparameterization ratio $r^* = \dim(\Theta)/\dim(Y) \approx 1$.  We varied
$r$ from $0.05$ to $5.0$ (25~log-spaced values) using Ridge regression and
XGBoost (100~models per $r$-value, $N_{\text{obs}} = 200$).  A sharp sigmoid
transition was confirmed in all model classes (sigmoid $R^2 = 0.92\text{--}0.98$),
but the critical point is $r^* \in [0.01, 0.12]$---an order of magnitude below
the predicted $r^* \approx 1$.  The design confounds overparameterization with
signal dilution: adding features adds zero-coefficient features, which are
unstable regardless of the interpolation threshold.

\paragraph{Quantitative tradeoff bound.}
We conjectured $\alpha + \sigma + \delta \leq 2$, where $\alpha$~(faithfulness),
$\sigma$~(stability), and $\delta$~(decisiveness) are continuous scores.
The maximum observed sum was $2.86$ (linear ensemble: $\alpha = 0.873$,
$\sigma = 0.986$, $\delta = 1.0$), falsifying the bound.  The binary
impossibility---no method achieves $\alpha = \sigma = \delta = 1.0$---remains
confirmed (maximum $\alpha = 0.873$).  The trilemma manifests as an exclusion
zone around $(1, 1, 1)$, not a linear budget constraint.

\paragraph{Molecular evolution from character theory.}
We tested whether the $S_k$~representation theory predicts synonymous
substitution structure beyond simple biochemistry.  The neighbor-count model
(number of synonymous 1-mutation variants per codon) achieves $R^2 = 1.0$; the
character-theoretic $(k{-}1)/k$ model achieves $R^2 = 0.65$ per codon but adds
zero residual variance after controlling for neighbor count (partial $R^2 = 0.0$,
$p = 1.0$).  The codon table's structure is fully explained by local
nucleotide degeneracy, not abstract group theory.

These falsifications are scientifically valuable: they define the boundary
between the framework's proven domain (the impossibility theorem and its
resolutions) and its speculative extensions (quantitative tradeoff surfaces,
specific phase-transition locations, and cross-domain extrapolations beyond
the established instances).

\subsection{The Gaussian Flip Rate: From Exact to Approximate Symmetry}
\label{sec:gaussian-flip}

The invariance counting theorem predicts exact bimodality when within-group
features have identical true importance ($\Delta_{jk} = 0$).  On real data,
symmetry is approximate ($\Delta_{jk} \approx 0$ but nonzero), and the
bimodal gap weakens.  The Gaussian flip rate formula provides the continuous
generalization.

The flip rate formula itself---the probability of sign disagreement between two Gaussian random variables---is a standard result in signal detection theory. The framework's contribution is not the formula but the \emph{structural identification}: the impossibility theorem and the bilemma determine \emph{which} feature pairs are subject to this formula (those within a symmetry orbit), while the Gaussian flip rate quantifies \emph{how much} instability each pair exhibits.

For features $j$ and $k$ with importance difference
$d^{(m)} = \text{imp}_j^{(m)} - \text{imp}_k^{(m)}$ across $M$ bootstrap
retrains, if $d^{(m)} \sim N(\Delta_{jk}, \sigma^2_\Delta)$, the probability
that two independent models disagree on the ranking is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{flip}(j,k) = 2\,\Phi\!\left(\frac{\Delta_{jk}}{\sigma_\Delta}\right)
  \Phi\!\left(\frac{-\Delta_{jk}}{\sigma_\Delta}\right),
  \label{eq:gaussian-flip}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF (Lean-verified in
\texttt{GaussianFlipRate.lean}).  When $\Delta = 0$ (exact symmetry),
$\text{flip} = 0.5$ --- recovering the invariance counting within-group coin flip.
When $|\Delta| \gg \sigma$ (strong signal), $\text{flip} \approx 0$ ---
recovering between-group stability.

A sensitivity analysis shows that $M \geq 30$ calibration models (15 per split) are needed for the Gaussian flip formula to achieve $R^2 > 0.7$; below $M = 20$, the formula degrades rapidly.

We validated this formula with \emph{independent} calibration and validation
model sets (30~models each, non-overlapping random seeds) on five real
datasets (Table~\ref{tab:gaussian-flip}).  The formula achieves a mean
out-of-sample $R^2 = 0.848$ across 647~feature pairs, with all five datasets
exceeding $R^2 = 0.79$.  The correlation baseline (predicting flip rate from
pairwise feature correlation) achieves $R^2 < 0.08$ on every dataset,
confirming that the Gaussian formula provides 14--400$\times$ lift over
correlation-based prediction.

The formula generalizes across model classes.  On three datasets (Breast Cancer, Wine, California Housing) with three model classes (XGBoost, Random Forest, Ridge regression), 8 of 9 combinations achieve out-of-sample $R^2 > 0.79$.  Random Forest shows the highest $R^2$ on two datasets (0.86 Breast Cancer, 0.92 Wine).  The one failure---Ridge on California Housing ($R^2 = -1.96$)---occurs because Ridge regression on this dataset produces near-deterministic coefficients with negligible variance, violating the Gaussian assumption.  The formula is expected to fail when a single feature dominates so strongly that importance differences have effectively zero noise.

One combination---Ridge regression on California Housing---produces out-of-sample $R^2 = -1.96$.  Ridge regression on this dataset assigns nearly deterministic coefficients (one feature, median income, dominates overwhelmingly), leaving negligible variance in the importance differences.  The Gaussian assumption requires meaningful sampling variance; when $\sigma_\Delta \approx 0$, the formula degenerates.  This boundary condition is expected: the formula predicts flip rates for stochastic model classes, not for deterministic or near-deterministic ones.
More generally, the Gaussian assumption fails when importance differences have near-zero variance (as in the Ridge/California Housing case, producing $R^2 = -1.96$) or when the importance distribution is multimodal (ensembles of qualitatively different model types).  A prospective diagnostic: if the coefficient of variation of $\sigma_\Delta$ across feature pairs is below 0.1, the importance landscape is too concentrated for the Gaussian model.

Practically, the signal-to-noise ratio $\text{SNR}_{jk} = |\Delta_{jk}|/\sigma_\Delta$ provides a simple decision rule: pairs with
$\text{SNR} > 2$ are reliable (flip rate $< 5\%$), pairs with
$\text{SNR} < 0.5$ are unreliable (flip rate $> 30\%$), and intermediate
values require uncertainty quantification.

\subsection{Domain-Specific Validation}
\label{sec:domain-validation}

The Gaussian flip formula was derived and validated on clinical tabular data
(Section~\ref{sec:gaussian-flip}).  This subsection extends the validation to
gene expression data, high-dimensional settings, clinical/financial audit
datasets, and the enrichment tradeoff predicted by the bilemma.

\paragraph{Gene expression reliability.}
On gene expression data (AP\_Breast\_Lung, 470 samples, 10{,}935 genes reduced
to the top~50 by variance), 70.5\% of gene-pair importance comparisons are
unreliable (SNR~$< 0.5$, flip rate~$> 30\%$) and only 4.0\% are reliable
(SNR~$> 2$, flip rate~$< 5\%$).  The formula achieves out-of-sample
$R^2 = 0.416$ on this dataset---substantially lower than on clinical tabular
data ($R^2 = 0.52\text{--}0.97$), reflecting the higher collinearity and
dimensionality of expression profiles.  Gain-based importance was used;
TreeSHAP would likely improve accuracy based on the 60\% improvement observed
at $P = 50$ (see below).

Gene importance rankings used to prioritize candidates for experimental
validation may contain unreliable comparisons that the Gaussian flip formula
can identify before resources are committed.

\paragraph{SHAP vs.\ gain at high dimensions.}
At $P = 50$ features (synthetic, 20 informative, 15 redundant), TreeSHAP
achieves out-of-sample $R^2 = 0.679$ versus gain-based $R^2 = 0.424$---a
60\% improvement.  TreeSHAP also produces more reliable rankings: 44.6\% of
pairs have SNR~$> 2$ (reliable) with SHAP versus 15.3\% with gain.  The
formula's accuracy depends on the importance metric; SHAP values, which have
lower variance across retrains, produce higher SNR and tighter predictions.

\paragraph{Clinical and financial reliability audit.}
Across three clinical and financial datasets (Breast Cancer, Heart Disease,
German Credit), 57\% of feature importance comparisons exhibit flip rates
exceeding 30\%, as predicted by the Gaussian flip formula (Pearson
$r = 0.76\text{--}0.85$).  Nearly zero comparisons are reliable (SNR~$> 2$) on
Breast Cancer (0\%) and Heart Disease (1.3\%); German Credit has 13.7\%
reliable pairs, reflecting its stronger feature hierarchy.

\paragraph{High-dimensional test ($P = 200$, gain-based).}
At $P = 200$ features (synthetic, 50 informative, 50 redundant), the formula
achieves out-of-sample $R^2 = 0.532$ with gain-based importance across
19{,}900 feature pairs.  Of these, 49.2\% are unreliable (SNR~$< 0.5$) and
12.2\% are reliable (SNR~$> 2$).

\paragraph{Enrichment on real data (Breast Cancer).}
The enrichment tradeoff, predicted by the bilemma, is confirmed on real
clinical data (Breast Cancer, 30 features, 100 XGBoost models).  At full
decisiveness (threshold~$= 0$), stability is 0.675 (flip rate 32.5\%).  At
threshold~$= 0.5$ (stability 0.991), decisiveness collapses to 0.088.  The
sharp phase transition between thresholds 0.10 and 0.15---where stability
jumps from 0.79 to 0.95 while decisiveness drops to 16\%---confirms that the
$(1,1)$ corner of the decisiveness--stability plane is unreachable on real
data, as the bilemma predicts.

\paragraph{Combined real-data-only reliability summary.}
Across seven real clinical, financial, and genomics datasets (Breast Cancer,
Wine, California Housing, Diabetes, Heart Disease, German Credit,
AP\_Breast\_Lung), encompassing 2{,}062 feature-pair comparisons, 62\% are
unreliable (SNR~$< 0.5$) and only 4\% are reliable (SNR~$> 2$).  On clinical
and financial data alone (703 pairs), 47\% are unreliable.  The mean
out-of-sample $R^2$ across all real datasets is 0.67.

\section{Bridge Theorems: Connections to Established Mathematics}
\label{sec:bridge-theorems}
%%% ==========================================================================

The cross-domain transfer experiments of the previous section show that the
framework \emph{generates} novel predictions.  This section establishes a
complementary result: the framework \emph{subsumes} classical theorems from
statistics, physics, and information theory.  We present five ``bridge
theorems'' that identify existing results as special cases of the abstract
impossibility structure, and derive one new quantitative prediction for AI
interpretability.

\subsection{Sufficient Statistics as $G$-Invariants}
\label{sec:bridge-sufficiency}

Fisher's concept of sufficiency \citep{fisher1922mathematical} can be
recovered exactly within the impossibility framework.  Map the components as
follows: let configurations be the data space~$\mathcal{X}$, let the
observation map $\mathrm{obs}$ be a statistic $T\colon \mathcal{X} \to
\mathcal{Y}$, and let the explanation map be the identity $\mathrm{explain} =
\mathrm{id}$ (reporting the full data).  Under this identification, the
Fisher--Neyman factorization theorem \citep{neyman1935su} states that $T$ is
sufficient for $\theta$ if and only if the conditional distribution
$P(X \mid T(X))$ is independent of~$\theta$---precisely the stability
condition of the abstract framework (the explanation factors through the
observation).

The Rashomon property holds whenever $T$ is non-injective: there exist
$X_1 \neq X_2$ with $T(X_1) = T(X_2)$, so the full data cannot be
faithfully, stably, and decisively reported.  This is the typical
case---sufficiency is useful precisely because $T$ compresses.

The identification extends to the resolution.  The Rao--Blackwell theorem
\citep{rao1945information, blackwell1947conditional} states that for any
estimator $\delta(X)$, the conditional estimator
$\delta^*(T) = \E[\delta(X) \mid T(X)]$ has variance no greater than that
of~$\delta$.  This conditioning is \emph{exactly} orbit averaging: the
estimator is averaged over the fiber $\{X : T(X) = t\}$, weighted by the
conditional distribution.  The Rao--Blackwell variance reduction is therefore
the framework's Pareto-optimality theorem---the $G$-invariant resolution
dominates all alternatives---expressed in the language of classical
estimation.

These are not analogies.  Fisher's sufficiency criterion (1922), the
Rao--Blackwell theorem (1945/47), and the explanation impossibility are the

Under a uniform prior on the fiber $\{\theta : \mathrm{obs}(\theta) = y\}$, the orbit average $\bar{E}(\theta) = (1/|G|)\sum_{g \in G} E(g \cdot \theta)$ is literally the conditional expectation $\E[\mathrm{explain}(\theta) \mid \mathrm{obs}(\theta) = y]$.  The identification is exact when the prior is uniform and the group acts transitively on fibers; it is approximate otherwise.

\subsection{The Explanation Tradeoff Bound}
\label{sec:bridge-uncertainty}

The qualitative impossibility (``at least one of faithfulness, stability, or
decisiveness must fail'') admits a quantitative refinement.  For a finite
system with total incompatible pairs $I_{\text{total}}$ and within-fiber
incompatible pairs $R_{\text{within}}$, define the \emph{Rashomon ratio}
$r = R_{\text{within}} / I_{\text{total}} \in [0,1]$ and the
\emph{decisiveness fraction} $D(E) = $ (fraction of incompatible pairs
preserved by~$E$).

\begin{theorem}[Explanation tradeoff bound]
\label{thm:uncertainty-principle}
For any stable explanation map~$E$,
\[
  D(E) \;\leq\; 1 - \frac{R_{\emph{within}}}{I_{\emph{total}}}
  \qquad\text{equivalently,}\qquad D(E) + r \;\leq\; 1.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stability forces $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$ whenever
$\mathrm{obs}(\theta_1) = \mathrm{obs}(\theta_2)$.  By irreflexivity of the
incompatibility relation, $\mathrm{incomp}(E(\theta), E(\theta))$ is false,
so every within-fiber incompatible pair is collapsed by~$E$.  Hence $E$ can
preserve at most $I_{\text{cross}} = I_{\text{total}} - R_{\text{within}}$
incompatible pairs, giving $D(E) \leq I_{\text{cross}} / I_{\text{total}} =
1 - r$.  The bound is tight: choosing a fixed representative per fiber
preserves all cross-fiber incompatibilities.
\end{proof}

The qualitative impossibility is the special case $r > 0$, $D = 1$: the
inequality $1 + r \leq 1$ is a contradiction.  When $r = 0$ there is no
constraint (no Rashomon), when $r = 1$ we have $D = 0$ (total loss of
decisiveness), and the general case interpolates smoothly.

\subsection{The Invariance Counting Principle}
\label{sec:bridge-noether}

Noether's theorem \citep{noether1918invariante} establishes that every
continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian implies a conserved quantity.  The
explanation framework provides an analogous discrete result: every Rashomon
symmetry implies a class of stably answerable queries.  We note that this
result is an application of classical invariant theory (Burnside's lemma,
Molien's theorem) to the explanation framework, not a direct consequence of
Noether's theorem, which requires continuous symmetry and a variational
principle.

Let $G$ be a finite group acting on the configuration space~$\Theta$,
preserving the observation map ($\mathrm{obs}(g \cdot \theta) =
\mathrm{obs}(\theta)$ for all $g \in G$).  A query
$q\colon \mathcal{H} \to \R$ is \emph{stably answerable} if
$q(\mathrm{explain}(\theta_1)) = q(\mathrm{explain}(\theta_2))$ whenever
$\mathrm{obs}(\theta_1) = \mathrm{obs}(\theta_2)$.

\begin{proposition}[Invariance theorem]
\label{prop:noether}
A query $q$ is stably answerable if and only if $q \in V^G$ (the
$G$-invariant subspace).  The number of independent stably answerable queries
is $\dim(V^G) = (1/|G|) \sum_{g \in G} \chi(g)$, by the character formula.
\end{proposition}

mathematical physics.  Both are instances of a single principle: group
invariance determines what can be stably measured.

\subsection{EM as Generalized Orbit Averaging}
\label{sec:bridge-em}

The Expectation-Maximization algorithm \citep{dempster1977maximum} is the most
widely used computational method for latent-variable models (mixture models,
HMMs, factor analysis, topic models).  Its E-step can be identified with the
framework's $G$-invariant resolution.

Map the EM components: configurations are latent variables~$Z$, observations
are the observed data~$X$ (with $\mathrm{obs}(Z) = X$ via marginalization
$P(X) = \sum_Z P(X,Z)$), and explanations are the latent variables
themselves.  The Rashomon set at~$X$ is $\{Z : P(X \mid Z) > 0\}$---all
latent configurations consistent with the data.  The E-step computes
\[
  \E_{Z \mid X, \theta}[f(Z)]
  \;=\; \sum_Z f(Z) \cdot P(Z \mid X, \theta),
\]
a weighted average over the fiber $\{Z : \mathrm{obs}(Z) = X\}$.  Under a
uniform prior ($P(Z)$ constant) with equal likelihoods for all consistent~$Z$
(the microcanonical case), this reduces to $(1/|\mathrm{fiber}(X)|) \sum_{Z :
\mathrm{obs}(Z) = X} f(Z)$---exactly the orbit average of the framework's
$G$-invariant resolution.

    Observed data $X$ & Observable $\mathrm{obs}(\theta)$ \\
    Posterior $P(Z \mid X, \theta)$ & Resolution weight on fiber \\
    E-step: $\E[f(Z) \mid X]$ & Orbit average ($G$-invariant resolution) \\
    M-step: update $\theta$ & Update resolution parameters \\
    Rao--Blackwell optimality & Pareto-optimality of orbit average \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Dictionary between the EM algorithm and the abstract framework.  The
    E-step IS the $G$-invariant resolution with posterior weights; the general
    EM generalizes from uniform to non-uniform orbit averaging.}
  \label{tab:em-dictionary}
\end{table}

The general case---non-uniform priors---extends the orbit average to a
posterior-weighted average, with the prior $P(Z \mid \theta)$ introducing
additional structure beyond the bare group action
(Table~\ref{tab:em-dictionary}).  This provides the most practically impactful
bridge: the EM algorithm, gauge-invariant averaging in physics, and codon
usage tables in biology are all instances of the same resolution strategy.

\subsection{Bayesian Interpretation}
\label{sec:bridge-bayesian}

The framework admits a natural Bayesian reading.  Faithfulness corresponds to likelihood consistency (the explanation does not contradict the data-generating mechanism).  Stability corresponds to posterior invariance (the explanation depends only on the observable evidence, not on which particular configuration produced it).  Decisiveness corresponds to point estimation (the explanation commits to a single value rather than a distribution).  The enrichment mechanism---adding a neutral element to restore faithful+stable---corresponds to Bayesian model averaging: replacing a point estimate with a posterior predictive distribution that averages over the equivalence class.  The $G$-invariant resolution is then the orbit average $\bar{E}(\theta) = (1/|G|) \sum_{g \in G} E(g \cdot \theta)$.  Under a uniform prior over the equivalence class, the orbit average coincides with the conditional expectation $E[\mathrm{explain}(\theta) \mid \mathrm{obs}(\theta)]$. For non-uniform priors, the orbit average and the posterior predictive diverge. The connection to Rao--Blackwell is therefore exact under uniformity and analogical in general.  The Pareto-optimality of orbit averaging (Theorem~\ref{thm:orbit-average}) is a consequence of the admissibility of Bayes estimators under proper priors~\citep{berger1985statistical}.  Specifically, under squared-error loss with a proper prior over the equivalence class, Bayes estimators are admissible (Berger, 1985, Theorem~5.2.4). The orbit average is the Bayes estimator under the uniform prior; its Pareto-optimality among stable maps follows. Under non-uniform priors, the Bayes estimator may differ from the orbit average, but the orbit average remains minimax among symmetric estimators.

\subsection{Quantum Error Correction}
\label{sec:bridge-qec}

The dimension ratio $\eta = \dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$ has a precise interpretation in quantum error correction: it equals the code rate $k/n$ of a quantum error-correcting code, where $V^G$ is the code subspace invariant under the error group $G$.  We \emph{conjecture} that the Knill--Laflamme conditions correspond to the stability condition of the framework applied to the code subspace.  A full formal bridge---analogous to the Fisher/Rao--Blackwell dictionary (Table~\ref{tab:sufficiency-dictionary})---remains an open direction.  The enrichment mechanism (adding a neutral element to restore faithful+stable) corresponds to adding ancilla qubits: enlarging the physical space to create room for fault-tolerant encoding~\citep{knill1997theory}.

\subsection{Fundamental Ceiling on AI Interpretability}
\label{sec:bridge-interpretability}

The bridge theorems above connect the framework to \emph{existing} results.
This final bridge produces a \emph{new} prediction: a quantitative upper bound
on the fraction of a neural network's internal representation that can be
stably interpreted.

Consider a network with one hidden layer of width~$n$.  For any permutation
$\pi \in S_n$, simultaneously permuting the rows of~$W_1$, elements of~$b_1$,
and columns of~$W_2$ yields an equivalent network computing the same
function~$f$.  This hidden-unit permutation symmetry gives Rashomon group
$G = S_n$, with $|G| = n!$ distinct parameterizations of the same input-output
function.

By character theory, the natural representation of $S_n$ on $\R^n$
(the hidden activation space) decomposes as
\[
  \R^n \;=\; V^{S_n} \oplus V_{\text{standard}},
\]
where $V^{S_n} = \mathrm{span}\{(1,1,\ldots,1)\}$ has dimension~1 (the mean
activation) and $V_{\text{standard}}$ has dimension $n-1$.  By
Proposition~\ref{prop:noether}, only queries in $V^{S_n}$ are stably
answerable, so at most $1/n$ of the hidden-layer representation is stably
interpretable.  For $L$~layers of width~$n$, the symmetry group is $S_n^L$
with invariant subspace of dimension~$L$ (one mean per layer) out of $nL$
total, giving the same $1/n$ ceiling.

\begin{corollary}[Interpretability ceiling]
\label{cor:interp-ceiling}
For a neural network with hidden-unit width~$n$, the fraction of internal
representation that is stably interpretable under the $S_n$ permutation
symmetry is at most $1/n$.  Specifically: the mean activation (1~dimension) is
$S_n$-invariant and hence stable; individual neuron activations ($n-1$
dimensions) are not.
\end{corollary}

For a transformer with $d_{\text{model}} = 768$ (BERT-base scale), this
predicts at most $1/768 \approx 0.13\%$ of the hidden representation is
stably interpretable---a hard ceiling independent of the interpretability
method employed.  This bound applies to \emph{internal} representation
interpretation (``opening the black box''); input-output behavior, being
invariant under all network symmetries, is fully stable.

\paragraph{Experimental confirmation.}
We trained fully connected networks with hidden widths $n \in
\{4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ and, for each, generated 50~random hidden-unit
permutations (preserving the input-output function exactly).  Across all
widths, the per-neuron importance ranking has a stable fraction of zero---even
stricter than the $1/n$ bound---while the mean activation achieves correlation
$1.0$ across all permutations (Figure~\ref{fig:interp-ceiling}).  The $S_n$
symmetry is not an approximate nuisance: it is an exact equivalence that
renders per-neuron interpretations fundamentally unstable.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/interpretability_ceiling.pdf}
  \caption{Interpretability ceiling experiment.  Per-neuron importance rankings
    have zero stable fraction across hidden widths $n = 4$ to $128$ (even
    stricter than the $1/n$ character-theory bound), while the mean activation
    correlation is exactly~$1.0$ across all permutations.  Only $S_n$-invariant
    queries survive the hidden-unit permutation symmetry.}
  \label{fig:interp-ceiling}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Hungarian permutation alignment audit.}
An adversarial audit using Hungarian permutation alignment reveals that the
non-uniqueness goes deeper than $S_n$ permutation symmetry.  After optimal
permutation alignment---which perfectly recovers known permutations in a
control experiment (aligned Jaccard $= 1.0$)---real cross-model agreement
reaches only Jaccard $= 0.079$.  The models learn genuinely different feature
decompositions, not permuted versions of the same decomposition.  CCA
analysis (mean similarity $= 0.950$) confirms the representations are
functionally equivalent---they encode the same information in different
coordinate systems---but the transformation between models is a
rotation/mixing, not a permutation.  This result strengthens the
interpretability ceiling: the actual symmetry group is larger than~$S_n$
(it includes continuous rotations), making the $1/n$ bound conservative.

\paragraph{Caveat.}
The $1/n$ bound addresses only the permutation symmetry~$S_n$, which is the
``minimal'' network symmetry.  Real networks may exhibit additional symmetries
(ReLU scaling invariance, sign-flip symmetry) that further reduce the
interpretable fraction.  The $1/n$ bound is therefore an upper bound; the true
ceiling may be lower.

\subsection{ExplanationSystem.lean --- Core Framework and Impossibility}
\label{app:lean-explanation-system}

This file defines the abstract \texttt{ExplanationSystem} structure, the three
desiderata (faithfulness, stability, decisiveness), and proves the universal
impossibility with zero axiom dependencies.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={ExplanationSystem.lean --- complete file},label={lst:explanation-system}]
/-!
# Abstract Explanation System

The universal impossibility framework. An explanation system S : Theta -> Y
is explained by E : Theta -> H. The Rashomon property says observationally
equivalent configurations can produce incompatible explanations.

## The Trilemma

No explanation E can simultaneously be:
- **Faithful**: E(theta) never contradicts the system's native explanation
- **Stable**: E(theta_1) = E(theta_2) whenever observe(theta_1) = observe(theta_2)
- **Decisive**: E(theta) commits to every distinction the native explanation makes

Each pair is achievable:
- Faithful + stable: abstain on controversial distinctions (ties)
- Faithful + decisive: use the native explanation (unstable under retraining)
- Stable + decisive: pick an arbitrary fixed explanation (unfaithful)
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

/-- An explanation system with configuration space Theta, explanation space H,
    and observable space Y. -/
structure ExplanationSystem (Theta : Type) (H : Type) (Y : Type) where
  /-- The observation map: what the system produces. -/
  observe : Theta -> Y
  /-- The explanation map: how we interpret a configuration. -/
  explain : Theta -> H
  /-- Incompatibility relation on explanations. -/
  incompatible : H -> H -> Prop
  /-- Incompatibility is irreflexive: no explanation contradicts itself. -/
  incompatible_irrefl : forall (h : H), not (incompatible h h)
  /-- The Rashomon property: equivalent configurations produce incompatible
      explanations. -/
  rashomon : exists theta_1 theta_2 : Theta,
    observe theta_1 = observe theta_2
    /\ incompatible (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2)

variable {Theta : Type} {H : Type} {Y : Type}

/-- Faithful: E never contradicts the native explanation.
    E(theta) may be less specific than explain(theta) but never disagrees
    with it. -/
def faithful (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta : Theta), not (S.incompatible (E theta) (S.explain theta))

/-- Stability: the explanation factors through the observable map.
    Equivalent configurations get the same explanation. -/
def stable (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta),
    S.observe theta_1 = S.observe theta_2
    -> E theta_1 = E theta_2

/-- Decisive: E inherits every incompatibility of the native explanation.
    Whatever the true explanation rules out, E also rules out.
    E is at least as committal as explain. -/
def decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
    S.incompatible (S.explain theta) h -> S.incompatible (E theta) h

/-- The Explanation Impossibility.

No explanation of a system with the Rashomon property can be simultaneously
faithful, stable, and decisive.

Proof structure:
1. Rashomon gives theta_1, theta_2 with same output but incompatible explanations
2. Decisiveness at theta_1: incompatible(explain theta_1, explain theta_2)
     -> incompatible(E theta_1, explain theta_2)
3. Stability: observe theta_1 = observe theta_2 -> E theta_1 = E theta_2,
     so incompatible(E theta_2, explain theta_2)
4. Faithfulness at theta_2: not incompatible(E theta_2, explain theta_2)
5. Contradiction between 3 and 4 -/
theorem explanation_impossibility (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (E : Theta -> H) (hf : faithful S E) (hs : stable S E)
    (hd : decisive S E) :
    False := by
  obtain <theta_1, theta_2, hobs, hinc> := S.rashomon
  have h1 : S.incompatible (E theta_1) (S.explain theta_2) :=
    hd theta_1 (S.explain theta_2) hinc
  have h2 : E theta_1 = E theta_2 := hs theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  rw [h2] at h1
  exact hf theta_2 h1

-- ============================================================
-- Tightness: each pair of properties is achievable
-- ============================================================

/-- **Tightness 1: Faithful + Decisive (dropping Stable).**
    E = explain is always faithful and decisive. -/
theorem tightness_faithful_decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) :
    faithful S S.explain /\ decisive S S.explain := by
  constructor
  . intro theta
    exact S.incompatible_irrefl (S.explain theta)
  . intro theta h hinc
    exact hinc

/-- **Tightness 2: Faithful + Stable (dropping Decisive).**
    The constant function E(theta) = c is faithful and stable
    when c is compatible with every native explanation. -/
theorem tightness_faithful_stable (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (c : H) (hc : forall (theta : Theta),
      not (S.incompatible c (S.explain theta))) :
    faithful S (fun _ => c) /\ stable S (fun _ => c) := by
  constructor
  . intro theta
    exact hc theta
  . intro _ _ _
    rfl

/-- **Tightness 3: Stable + Decisive (dropping Faithful).**
    The constant function E(theta) = c is stable and decisive
    when c inherits every incompatibility. -/
theorem tightness_stable_decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (c : H) (hc : forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
      S.incompatible (S.explain theta) h -> S.incompatible c h) :
    stable S (fun _ => c) /\ decisive S (fun _ => c) := by
  constructor
  . intro _ _ _
    rfl
  . intro theta h hinc
    exact hc theta h hinc
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection[UniversalResolution.lean --- G-Invariant Resolution]{UniversalResolution.lean --- $G$-Invariant Resolution}
\label{app:lean-resolution}

This file formalizes the resolution framework: if a group $G$ acts transitively
on observe-fibers and the resolution is $G$-invariant, then it is automatically
stable.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={UniversalResolution.lean --- complete file},label={lst:resolution}]
import UniversalImpossibility.ExplanationSystem
import Mathlib.GroupTheory.GroupAction.Defs

/-!
# G-Invariant Resolution Framework
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

variable {Theta : Type} {H : Type} {Y : Type} {G : Type}

/-- A symmetry structure on an explanation system. -/
structure HasSymmetry (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (G : Type) [Group G] [MulAction G Theta] where
  observe_invariant :
    forall (g : G) (theta : Theta),
      S.observe (g * theta) = S.observe theta
  fiber_transitive :
    forall (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta),
      S.observe theta_1 = S.observe theta_2
      -> exists (g : G), g * theta_1 = theta_2

/-- A resolution R is G-invariant if constant on G-orbits. -/
def gInvariant (R : Theta -> H) (G : Type)
    [Group G] [MulAction G Theta] : Prop :=
  forall (g : G) (theta : Theta), R (g * theta) = R theta

/-- G-invariance + fiber transitivity implies stability. -/
theorem gInvariant_stable
    (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (R : Theta -> H)
    [Group G] [MulAction G Theta]
    (sym : HasSymmetry S G)
    (hInv : gInvariant R G) :
    stable S R := by
  intro theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  obtain <g, hg> := sym.fiber_transitive theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  have h := hInv g theta_1
  rw [hg] at h
  exact h.symm
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Ubiquity.lean --- complete file},label={lst:ubiquity}]
import UniversalImpossibility.ExplanationSystem

/-!
# Structural Ubiquity of Explanation Impossibility

The impossibility is not a pathological corner case -- it is generic whenever
the configuration space has higher dimension than the observable space.

## Main results

- `generic_underspecification`: if dim(Theta) > dim(Y), the fiber has
  positive dimension.
- `fiber_nondegeneracy_implies_impossibility`: if the explanation map is
  non-constant on a fiber, then no explanation can be faithful, stable,
  and decisive.
- `ubiquity_impossibility`: constructs an ExplanationSystem from witness
  data and derives the impossibility via `explanation_impossibility`.
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

-- ============================================================
-- Dimensional argument
-- ============================================================

/-- Generic underspecification: if the configuration space has higher
    dimension than the observable space, the system is generically
    underspecified. -/
theorem generic_underspecification {n m : Nat} (h : m < n) :
    n - m > 0 := by
  omega

-- ============================================================
-- Non-degeneracy -> impossibility bridge
-- ============================================================

/-- If two configurations have the same observable output but incompatible
    explanations, and the incompatibility relation is irreflexive, then
    no explanation can be faithful, stable, and decisive. -/
theorem fiber_nondegeneracy_implies_impossibility
    {Theta H Y : Type}
    (observe : Theta -> Y) (explain : Theta -> H)
    (incomp : H -> H -> Prop)
    (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta)
    (hobs : observe theta_1 = observe theta_2)
    (hinc : incomp (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2))
    (E : Theta -> H)
    (hf : forall (theta : Theta), not (incomp (E theta) (explain theta)))
    (hs : forall a b : Theta,
      observe a = observe b -> E a = E b)
    (hd : forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
      incomp (explain theta) h -> incomp (E theta) h) :
    False := by
  have h1 : incomp (E theta_1) (explain theta_2) :=
    hd theta_1 (explain theta_2) hinc
  have h2 : E theta_1 = E theta_2 := hs theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  rw [h2] at h1
  exact hf theta_2 h1

-- ============================================================
-- Connection to ExplanationSystem
-- ============================================================

/-- Given concrete witness data, construct an ExplanationSystem and derive
    the impossibility. -/
theorem ubiquity_impossibility
    {Theta H Y : Type}
    (observe : Theta -> Y) (explain : Theta -> H)
    (incomp : H -> H -> Prop)
    (incomp_irrefl : forall (h : H), not (incomp h h))
    (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta)
    (hobs : observe theta_1 = observe theta_2)
    (hinc : incomp (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2)) :
    let S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y :=
      { observe, explain, incompatible := incomp,
        incompatible_irrefl := incomp_irrefl,
        rashomon := <theta_1, theta_2, hobs, hinc> }
    forall (E : Theta -> H),
      faithful S E -> stable S E -> decisive S E
      -> False := by
  intro S E hf hs hd
  exact explanation_impossibility S E hf hs hd
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Detailed Experiment Methodology}
\label{app:experiments}
%%% ==========================================================================

This appendix provides full details of the four empirical experiments
validating Instances 2--5 of the universal impossibility.  All experiments

\begin{verbatim}
python paper/scripts/run_all_universal_experiments.py
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Attention Map Instability (Instance 2)}
\label{app:exp-attention}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do functionally equivalent transformer models assign peak attention to
different tokens?

\paragraph{Model.}
DistilBERT-base-uncased \citep{sanh2019distilbert}, a 6-layer, 12-head
transformer with 66M parameters.

\paragraph{Perturbation protocol.}
We create 10 model variants by adding Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma)$
to all weight matrices of a pretrained DistilBERT checkpoint.  Five models use
$\sigma = 0.01$ and five use $\sigma = 0.02$.  This simulates the Rashomon set
of models reachable by different training trajectories that arrive at the same
loss basin.

\paragraph{Dataset.}
200 synthetic sentiment sentences (100 positive, 100 negative), constructed
from 10 positive and 10 negative templates with 10 variations each.

\paragraph{Attention extraction.}
For each sentence and each model, we extract attention weights from all 6 layers
using attention rollout (the product of attention matrices across layers),
producing a distribution over tokens.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Prediction agreement}: fraction of sentences where all 10 models
    produce the same classification.
  \item \textbf{Argmax flip rate}: fraction of model pairs $(i,j)$ and sentences
    where $\arg\max_t \alpha^{(i)}_t \neq \arg\max_t \alpha^{(j)}_t$.
  \item \textbf{Mean Kendall $\tau$}: average rank correlation between attention
    distributions across model pairs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
95\% confidence intervals are computed via the percentile method with 2000
bootstrap resamples over sentence--model-pair combinations.

\paragraph{Results.}
Under full retraining (20 models, last 2 transformer layers retrained from
independent seeds), prediction agreement is 94.5\%, the argmax flip rate is
\textbf{19.9\%} [95\% CI: 19.1--20.6\%], and mean Kendall $\tau = 0.63$
[CI: 0.62--0.63].  Under weight perturbation ($\sigma \in \{0.01, 0.02\}$,
10 models), prediction agreement is 100\% and the argmax flip rate rises to
60.0\% [CI: 59--61\%]; however, weight perturbation overstates instability
because perturbing weights is not equivalent to retraining from a new seed
(perturbation preserves the loss basin, while retraining explores genuinely
different trajectories).  We report the full-retraining figure (19.9\%) as
the primary result (Cohen's $d = 0.50$).

\subsection{Experiment 2: Counterfactual Instability (Instance 3)}
\label{app:exp-counterfactual}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do equivalent-accuracy models produce contradictory counterfactual explanations?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
German Credit (UCI, OpenML \texttt{credit-g}), 1000 instances, 20 features
after one-hot encoding of categorical variables.  Numerical features are
standardized.

\paragraph{Models.}
20 XGBoost classifiers with identical hyperparameters (default \texttt{xgb.XGBClassifier}
settings), differing only in random seed.  Train/test split: 80/20 with a fixed
random state for the split.

\paragraph{Counterfactual method.}
Greedy feature-importance-ordered perturbation toward the positive-class centroid.
For each query point $x_0$ classified as ``bad credit,'' features are perturbed
in order of their importance (measured by XGBoost feature importance) toward the
mean of the ``good credit'' class, until the prediction flips.

\paragraph{Query points.}
45--50 test-set instances classified as ``bad credit'' by at least 18 of the
20 models (to ensure the counterfactual direction is well-defined for most
models).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Direction flip rate}: fraction of model pairs recommending
    opposite feature changes for the same query.  Two counterfactuals ``flip''
    if any feature is perturbed in opposite directions.
  \item \textbf{Cross-model recourse validity}: fraction of counterfactuals
    generated for model $i$ that also flip the prediction on model $j$.
  \item \textbf{Distance instability (CV)}: coefficient of variation of
    $\ell_2$ counterfactual distances across models.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
500 bootstrap resamples over query points for 95\% percentile CIs.

\paragraph{Results.}
Mean AUC: 0.797 [CI: 0.783--0.810].  Direction flip rate: 23.5\%
[CI: 20.6--26.6\%].  Cross-model validity: 61.3\% [CI: 55.8--66.2\%].
Distance CV: 0.328 [CI: 0.281--0.372].

\subsection{Experiment 3: Concept Probe Instability (Instance 4)}
\label{app:exp-concept}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do functionally equivalent neural networks encode the same concept in
incompatible directions in representation space?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
\texttt{sklearn.datasets.load\_digits}: 1797 handwritten digit images
($8 \times 8$ pixels, 10 classes).

\paragraph{Models.}
15 \texttt{MLPClassifier} models with architecture $(128, 64)$, ReLU
activation, trained with different random seeds (\texttt{random\_state}
$= 42, 43, \ldots, 56$).  All models achieve $>95\%$ test accuracy on
a 70/30 split.

\paragraph{Concept definitions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Curved digits}: $\{0, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9\}$ vs.\ angular
    $\{1, 4, 7\}$.
  \item \textbf{Symmetric digits}: $\{0, 1, 8\}$ vs.\ rest.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Concept direction extraction.}
For each model, extract penultimate-layer activations (64-dimensional) on
the test set.  Fit a \texttt{LinearSVC} to separate concept-positive from
concept-negative examples.  The resulting weight vector is the concept
activation vector (CAV).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Mean $|\cos(\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j)|$}: pairwise cosine
    similarity of CAVs (off-diagonal entries of the $15 \times 15$ matrix).
  \item \textbf{Concept direction instability}: $1 - |\cos|$.
  \item \textbf{TCAV score std}: standard deviation of TCAV sensitivity scores
    across models.
  \item \textbf{Prediction agreement}: fraction of test points classified
    identically by all 15 models.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
2000 bootstrap resamples for 95\% CIs.

\paragraph{Results.}
Mean $|\cos|$: 0.10 [CI: 0.086--0.114].  Direction instability: 0.90.
TCAV std (curved): 0.157.  TCAV std (symmetric): 0.123.  Prediction
agreement: 97.2\% [CI: 95.7--98.5\%].

\subsection{Experiment 4: Model Selection Instability (Instance 6)}
\label{app:exp-model-selection}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Does the identity of the ``best'' model change when evaluated on different
data splits?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
German Credit (credit-g, OpenML), same as Experiment~2.

\paragraph{Models.}
50 XGBoost classifiers, all trained on a single fixed 80/20 split, differing
only in random seed (\texttt{random\_state} $= 0, 1, \ldots, 49$).

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
Each of 20 independent random 80/20 evaluation splits is used to compute
test-set AUC for all 50 models.  For each split, the ``best'' model is the
one with the highest AUC.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Best-model flip rate}: fraction of consecutive evaluation
    splits where the identity of the best model changes.
  \item \textbf{Unique winners}: number of distinct models that are ``best''
    on at least one evaluation split.
  \item \textbf{AUC spread}: mean range of AUC values across the 50 models
    within a single evaluation split.
  \item \textbf{Overall mean AUC}: mean $\pm$ std of AUC across all
    models and splits.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Results.}
Best-model flip rate: 80\% (Cohen's $d = 2.0$).  Consecutive flip rate: 89\%.  Unique winners:
11 out of 50.  Mean AUC spread per split: 0.0197.  Overall AUC:
$0.948 \pm 0.0023$.

\section{Proof Sketches for All Nine Instances}
\label{app:proof-sketches}
%%% ==========================================================================

Each instance follows the same four-step proof structure: (1)~establish the
Rashomon property, (2)~faithfulness forces $E = \mathsf{obs}$,
(3)~decisiveness forces $E(\theta_1) \neq E(\theta_2)$,
(4)~stability forces $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, yielding a contradiction.
Below we spell out the domain-specific content for all nine instances.

\subsection{Instance 1: Feature Attribution}
\label{app:proof-attribution}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ data-generating distributions with collinearity structure.
$\sH ={}$ trained models (GBDT, Lasso, neural net).
$Y = (\mathrm{Fin}\,P \to \R)$: feature attribution vectors.
$\mathsf{obs}(f) = \boldsymbol{\varphi}(f)$: the model's own SHAP values.
$\mathsf{exp}$: the attribution procedure under evaluation.
$\incomp$: strict reversal of a collinear pair ($\varphi_j > \varphi_k$ vs.\
$\varphi_k > \varphi_j$).

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Collinear features within a group $\ell$ are symmetric under the DGP.
Surjectivity of the first-mover map guarantees that for any collinear pair
$j, k$, there exist models $f, f'$ with the same DGP but opposite rankings.
This is the \texttt{RashomonProperty} of \texttt{Trilemma.lean}.

\paragraph{Proof.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Rashomon: $\exists f, f'$ with $\theta(f) = \theta(f')$ and
    $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$, $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$.
  \item Faithful $E$ reports $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$ for $f$ and
    $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$ for $f'$.
  \item Decisive: $E(f) \neq E(f')$ (rankings differ).
  \item Stable: $E(f) = E(f')$ (same DGP).
  \item Contradiction.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Lean.}
{\raggedright
\texttt{attribution\_impossibility} in \texttt{Trilemma.lean}
(0~axioms);
\texttt{attribution\_impossibility\_abstract} in
\texttt{AttributionInstance.lean}
(0~additional axioms beyond system).\par}

\subsection{Instance 2: Attention Maps}
\label{app:proof-attention}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ neural network weight configurations.
$Y ={}$ input$\to$output functions (the behavior the network computes).
$\sH = \Delta^T$: attention distributions over $T$ tokens.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta$: the function the network computes.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = \alpha_\theta$: the attention distribution.
$\incomp$: $\arg\max$ differs.

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
\citet{damour2022underspecification}: $\exists \theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$f_{\theta_1} \approx f_{\theta_2}$ yet
$\arg\max_t (\alpha_{\theta_1})_t \neq \arg\max_t (\alpha_{\theta_2})_t$.

\paragraph{Proof.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Rashomon: equivalent models with different max-attended tokens.
  \item Faithful $E$ reports $\alpha_{\theta_1}$ for $\theta_1$ and
    $\alpha_{\theta_2}$ for $\theta_2$.
  \item Decisive: $E(\theta_1) \neq E(\theta_2)$.
  \item Stable: $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$.
  \item Contradiction.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Lean.}
\texttt{attention\_impossibility} in \texttt{AttentionInstance.lean}.

\subsection{Instance 3: Counterfactual Explanations}
\label{app:proof-counterfactual}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ model parameters (decision boundaries).
$Y ={}$ predictions on a test set.
$\sH = \mathcal{X}$: nearest counterfactual examples.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta|_{\mathrm{test}}$.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = \arg\min_{x'} \|x' - x_0\|$ s.t.\ $f_\theta(x') \neq f_\theta(x_0)$.
$\incomp$: counterfactuals point in different directions.

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Models with identical test predictions place their decision boundaries in
different locations near the query point $x_0$, yielding counterfactuals
in opposite directions \citep{semenova2022existence,fisher2019models}.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Same four-step structure.  Lean:
\texttt{counterfactual\_impossibility} in \texttt{Counterfactual\-Instance.lean}.

\subsection{Instance 4: Concept Probes}
\label{app:proof-concept}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ model weights (defines internal representations).
$Y ={}$ predictions (input-output function).
$\sH = \R^c$: concept activation vectors.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta$.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = v_\theta$: concept direction from linear probe.
$\incomp$: directions differ (not related by rotation in concept subspace).

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Equivalent models encode concepts in different directions
\citep{bolukbasi2016man,kim2018tcav,ravfogel2020null}.  The
overparameterized zero-loss manifold has dimension $\geq d - n > 0$,
so infinitely many weight configurations yield identical predictions
but geometrically distinct representations.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Same four-step structure.  Lean:
\texttt{concept\_impossibility} in \texttt{ConceptInstance.lean}.

\subsection{Instance 5: Causal Discovery}
\label{app:proof-causal}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ DAGs over a fixed vertex set $V$.
$Y ={}$ conditional independence structures (Markov equivalence classes).
$\sH ={}$ DAGs (causal explanations).
$\mathsf{obs}(h) ={}$ the CI structure encoded by $h$ ($d$-separation).
$\mathsf{exp}(h) ={}$ the fully oriented DAG returned by a discovery algorithm.
$\incomp$: opposite edge orientations ($X \to Y$ vs.\ $Y \to X$).

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Markov equivalence \citep{verma1991equivalence}: any CPDAG with at least one
undirected edge contains two DAGs in the same equivalence class with that edge
oriented in opposite directions.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Same four-step structure.

\subsection{Instance 6: Model Selection}
