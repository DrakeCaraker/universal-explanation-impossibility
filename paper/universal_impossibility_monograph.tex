\begin{abstract}
%%% ==========================================================================
We prove a universal impossibility: no explanation system can simultaneously be
faithful (never contradicting the system's native explanation), stable
(consistent across observationally equivalent configurations), and decisive
(inheriting every incompatibility of the native explanation) when
the underlying inference problem is underspecified---that is, when
observationally equivalent configurations produce incompatible explanations
(the Rashomon property).
This single abstract theorem, which we call the \emph{Explanation
Impossibility}, is a meta-theorem whose reach extends far beyond machine
learning.  We derive the impossibility as a zero-axiom consequence in
eight scientific domains: the degeneracy of the genetic code (biology),
gauge freedom in electromagnetism (physics), microstate degeneracy in
statistical mechanics (physics), syntactic ambiguity in natural
language (linguistics), the phase problem in crystallography,
underdetermined linear systems (mathematics), the database view update
problem (computer science), and Markov equivalence in causal inference
(statistics).  We further instantiate it across nine ML explanation types:
feature attribution under collinearity
\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}, attention map instability, counterfactual
explanation instability, concept probe (TCAV) instability, causal discovery
ambiguity \citep{verma1991equivalence}, model selection instability under the
Rashomon effect \citep{rudin2024amazing,fisher2019models}, saliency map
(GradCAM) instability, token citation explanation instability, and
mechanistic interpretability non-uniqueness.
The proof is a tight four-step chain---decisiveness, stability, faithfulness,
contradiction---and Lean-verified tightness theorems show that each pair of
properties is achievable, confirming that the impossibility is non-trivial.
Cross-domain experiments empirically validate the impossibility in seven of
the eight scientific domains (Table~\ref{tab:cross-domain}), including a
perfect dose-response between codon degeneracy and usage entropy
($p = 10^{-17}$), significant parser disagreement on ambiguous sentences
($p = 9 \times 10^{-4}$), and causal discovery convergence only at infinite
sample size.
Empirical experiments on four ML instances confirm severe instability among
functionally equivalent models: 19.9\% attention argmax flip rate
(under full retraining; 60\% under weight perturbation), 23.5\%
counterfactual direction flip rate, 0.90 concept direction instability,
and 80\% model selection flip rate.
The resolution is uniform: $G$-invariant explanation systems that aggregate
over equivalent hypotheses restore faithfulness-in-expectation and stability at
the cost of decisiveness, and \DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation of
\textbf{SH}AP) is a concrete instance of this resolution for feature attribution.
The impossibility is ubiquitous: any learning problem with strictly more
parameters than constraints admits a Rashomon set of positive measure, and
neural networks with hidden dimension exceeding the data dimension always fall
into this regime.
The framework is mechanically verified in Lean~4 (82~files, 377~theorems,
72~axioms, 0~\texttt{sorry}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%% ==========================================================================

A data scientist trains a gradient-boosted model for credit risk, runs \SHAP{},
and finds that \emph{debt-to-income ratio} is the top feature.  She retrains
from a different random seed---same data, same hyperparameters, same test
accuracy---and the top feature flips to \emph{credit utilization}
\citep{krishna2022disagreement}.  An NLP engineer inspects attention weights in
a sentiment classifier, retrains, and the most-attended token changes from
``not'' to ``terrible'' \citep{jainwallace2019}.  A colleague runs LIME on the
same credit model and obtains yet a third ranking
\citep{molnar2022general}.  None of these are software bugs.

The explainable AI (XAI) research program promises explanations that are
\emph{faithful} (they reflect what the model actually computes),
\emph{stable} (they are reproducible across equivalent models), and
\emph{decisive} (they resolve every explanation query with a definite answer).
Regulators now demand all three: the EU AI Act requires ``meaningful
explanations'' for high-risk systems \citep{euaiact2024}, and SR~11-7 requires
``developmental evidence'' documenting model decisions
\citep{occ2011sr117}.  Yet a growing body of theoretical work shows that the
promise is mathematically impossible to keep---at least in specific domains.
\citet{bilodeau2024impossibility} prove that no feature attribution satisfying
the Shapley axioms is stable under collinearity.
\citet{huang2024failings} show that SHAP values can be arbitrarily
misleading.  \citet{chouldechova2017fair} and \citet{kleinberg2017inherent}
independently prove that common fairness metrics cannot simultaneously be
satisfied.  \citet{verma1991equivalence} establish that observationally
equivalent causal structures are indistinguishable from data alone.

Each of these impossibilities was proved independently, in domain-specific
language, under domain-specific assumptions.  The attribution impossibility
assumes collinearity among features; Chouldechova's impossibility assumes
unequal base rates across groups; Markov equivalence assumes faithfulness of
the directed acyclic graph.  No one has asked: is there \emph{one} structural
cause?  Is the impossibility intrinsic to underspecification itself, rather
than to any particular domain?

In this paper we answer affirmatively.  We prove a \emph{Universal Explanation
Impossibility}: no explanation of an underspecified system can simultaneously
be faithful, stable, and decisive.  The theorem requires only the
\emph{Rashomon property}---the existence of equivalent hypotheses that
honestly report incompatible explanations---and the proof is four lines.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{A universal impossibility theorem}
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) that unifies impossibility results
    across eight sciences and nine ML explanation types under a single
    proof requiring only the Rashomon property.
  \item \textbf{Eight derived instances across eight sciences}
    (Section~\ref{sec:derived-instances}): underdetermined linear systems
    (mathematics), genetic code degeneracy (biology), gauge freedom
    (physics), microstate degeneracy in statistical mechanics (physics),
    syntactic ambiguity (linguistics), the phase problem
    (crystallography), the view update problem (computer science), and
    Markov equivalence (statistics)---each derived with zero axioms from
    domain-specific first principles.
  \item \textbf{Nine ML instances}
    (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}): feature attribution
    \citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}, attention maps
    \citep{jainwallace2019}, counterfactual explanations, concept probes,
    causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, model selection
    \citep{rudin2024amazing}, saliency maps (GradCAM), token citation explanations,
    and mechanistic interpretability (circuit
    discovery)---each shown to be a special case of the abstract framework.
  \item \textbf{Empirical illustration} for seven of the nine ML instances
    (attribution, attention, counterfactual, concept probes, model selection, saliency maps, and token citation),
    confirming explanation instability on standard benchmarks.
  \item \textbf{A uniform resolution}: $G$-invariant aggregation restores
    faithfulness and stability at the cost of decisiveness.  \DASH{} for
    feature attribution, CPDAGs for causal discovery, and ensemble probes
    for concepts are all instances of this resolution.
  \item \textbf{Ubiquity}: the impossibility is generic whenever the
    parameter dimension exceeds the observable dimension, covering
    virtually all modern overparameterized models.
  \item \textbf{Mechanical verification} in Lean~4
    \citep{demoura2021lean4}: 82~files, 377~theorems, 72~axioms,
    0~\texttt{sorry}.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent In short: faithful, stable, decisive---pick two.

This paper generalizes the attribution impossibility of our companion work
\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility} from feature attribution to arbitrary
explanation systems.  The companion paper provides quantitative depth
(divergence rates, ensemble size bounds, \DASH{} optimality) for the
attribution instance; this paper supplies the abstract framework, eight
additional instances, and the ubiquity result.  The two papers share a
Lean codebase but are self-contained.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.

Section~\ref{sec:derived-instances} derives the impossibility in eight

Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and open questions.

\section{The Abstract Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%%% ==========================================================================

We introduce a minimal abstract framework sufficient to state and prove the
Explanation Impossibility.  All instances in
Sections~\ref{sec:derived-instances} and~\ref{sec:ml-instances} are special cases.
The framework aligns exactly with the Lean~4 formalization in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}: every definition and theorem below has a
machine-checked counterpart.

\subsection{Explanation Systems}

\begin{definition}[Explanation System]
\label{def:explanation-system}
An \emph{explanation system} is a tuple
\[
  \mathcal{S} = (\Params, \sH, Y, \mathsf{observe}, \mathsf{explain}, \incomp)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Params$ is a \emph{configuration space} (the set of possible
    configurations, e.g., random seeds, hyperparameter settings);
  \item $\sH$ is an \emph{explanation space} (the set of possible explanations);
  \item $Y$ is an \emph{observable space} (e.g., input--output functions,
    test-set predictions);
  \item $\mathsf{observe}: \Params \to Y$ is the \emph{observation map}, assigning
    to each configuration its observable behavior;
  \item $\mathsf{explain}: \Params \to \sH$ is the system's \emph{native
    explanation map}, assigning to each configuration the explanation that the
    system itself ``honestly reports''; and
  \item $\incomp\;\subseteq \sH \times \sH$ is an \emph{incompatibility relation}
    on explanations satisfying \emph{irreflexivity}:
    $\neg\,\incomp(h, h)$ for every $h \in \sH$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Irreflexivity of~$\incomp$ is a structural requirement: no explanation
contradicts itself.  This is the \texttt{incompatible\_irrefl} field of the
Lean structure and is used in the tightness proof
(Section~\ref{sec:tightness}).

An \emph{explanation map} $E : \Params \to \sH$ is a procedure that a
practitioner \emph{chooses} (e.g., SHAP, LIME, a fixed causal orientation
rule).  The native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ records what the
configuration \emph{actually implies}; $E(\theta)$ is what the chosen method
reports.

\subsection{The Three Desiderata}

\begin{definition}[Faithful]
\label{def:faithful}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{faithful} if $E(\theta)$ never contradicts the
system's native explanation $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\quad
  \neg\,\incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta)\bigr).
\]
That is, $E(\theta)$ may be less specific than $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ but
never disagrees with it.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Stable]
\label{def:stable}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{stable} if equivalent configurations receive
the same explanation:
\[
  \forall\,\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params,\quad
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Decisive]
\label{def:decisive}
An explanation map $E$ is \emph{decisive} if $E(\theta)$ inherits every
incompatibility of the native explanation: whatever
$\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$ rules out, $E(\theta)$ also rules out.  Formally,
\[
  \forall\,\theta \in \Params,\;\forall\,h \in \sH,\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta),\, h\bigr)
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  \incomp\bigl(E(\theta),\, h\bigr).
\]
$E$ is at least as committal as $\mathsf{explain}$.
\end{definition}

In the feature attribution instance (Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}),
faithfulness means the chosen method never contradicts the model's own SHAP
values, stability means consistency across retraining seeds, and decisiveness
means that every distinction made by the native attribution is preserved.

\subsection{The Rashomon Property}

The key structural condition linking underspecification to explanation
impossibility is the following.

\begin{definition}[Rashomon Property]
\label{def:rashomon}
An explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the \emph{Rashomon property} if there
exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params$ with
\[
  \mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \incomp\bigl(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1),\;\mathsf{explain}(\theta_2)\bigr).
\]
That is, two configurations that are observationally equivalent honestly report
incompatible explanations.
\end{definition}

The Rashomon property is the formal statement of underspecification at the
explanation level: the data do not uniquely determine the explanation, even in
principle.  It generalizes the Rashomon effect of
\citet{rudin2024amazing,fisher2019models} from model selection to arbitrary
explanation targets.

\subsection{Main Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Explanation Impossibility]
\label{thm:universal}
If an explanation system $\mathcal{S}$ has the Rashomon property, then no
explanation map $E$ can simultaneously be faithful, stable, and decisive.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By the Rashomon property, there exist configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ and
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
Suppose for contradiction that $E$ is faithful, stable, and decisive.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness} at $\theta_1$:
    $\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$
    implies
    $\incomp(E(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Stability}:
    $\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$
    implies $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, so
    $\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item \textbf{Faithfulness} at $\theta_2$:
    $\neg\,\incomp(E(\theta_2), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$.
  \item Steps 2 and 3 contradict.  \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof uses all three properties in a tight chain:
decisive $\to$ stable $\to$ faithful $\to$ contradiction.
This mirrors the Lean proof exactly (\texttt{explanation\_impossibility} in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The simplicity of the proof---four steps from the Rashomon
property to contradiction---is a feature of the framework, not a
limitation of the result.  The intellectual content lies in identifying the
right definitions: faithful as non-contradiction, stable as factoring
through observations, decisive as inheriting incompatibilities.  The
contribution is the axiomatic framework and its unification of nine
domains, not the proof technique.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The theorem is deliberately parameter-free: it requires no regularity
conditions on $\sH$, no metric structure on $Y$, and no probabilistic
assumptions.  All quantitative depth (divergence rates, sample complexity,
ensemble sizes) appears in the instance-specific sections below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Tightness}
\label{sec:tightness}

The impossibility is tight: every pair of properties is simultaneously
achievable.  Three Lean-verified witnesses establish this
(\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_decisive},
\texttt{tightness\_faithful\_stable},
\texttt{tightness\_stable\_decisive} in \texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}).

\begin{proposition}[Tightness of the Universal Impossibility]
\label{prop:tightness}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Decisive (drop Stable).}
    Set $E = \mathsf{explain}$.  Faithfulness holds by irreflexivity of
    $\incomp$: $\neg\,\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta), \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$.
    Decisiveness holds by identity.  Stability fails because the Rashomon
    property places incompatible native explanations on the same observe-fiber.

  \item \textbf{Faithful $+$ Stable (drop Decisive).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``neutral'' element with
    $\neg\,\incomp(c, \mathsf{explain}(\theta))$ for all~$\theta$, and set
    $E(\theta) = c$.  Faithfulness and stability hold, but $E$ abstains on
    every distinction the native explanation makes.

  \item \textbf{Stable $+$ Decisive (drop Faithful).}
    Let $c \in \sH$ be a ``maximally committal'' element inheriting every
    incompatibility of every native explanation, and set $E(\theta) = c$.
    Stability and decisiveness hold, but $c$ may contradict some
    $\mathsf{explain}(\theta)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Decidable Underspecification Implies Impossible Explanation]
\label{cor:decidable}
If the data-generating process is not identifiable from the
observations---i.e., if there exist $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$\mathsf{observe}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{observe}(\theta_2)$ yet
$\incomp(\mathsf{explain}(\theta_1), \mathsf{explain}(\theta_2))$---then
the Rashomon property holds and the impossibility applies.
\end{corollary}

The converse also holds for fully specified systems: when each observation
uniquely determines its configuration, $E = \mathsf{explain}$ satisfies all
three properties simultaneously (Lean: \texttt{fully\_specified\_possibility}
in \texttt{Necessity.lean}).

\paragraph{Instance overlap and independence.}
We note that instances 2 (attention), 7 (saliency), and 8 (token citation)
share a common Rashomon mechanism---weight-space multiplicity affecting
internal representations.  They differ in modality and metric but are not
structurally independent.  A conservative count identifies six genuinely
distinct Rashomon mechanisms across the nine instances.

\section{The Universal Resolution}
\label{sec:resolution}
%%% ==========================================================================

The impossibility of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} is tight: one of the three
desiderata must be sacrificed.  In this section we show that a \emph{uniform}
resolution exists whenever the Rashomon property arises from a symmetry group
acting on the hypothesis space.  The resolution sacrifices decisiveness and
achieves faithfulness in expectation together with exact stability.

\subsection[G-Invariant Explanation Systems]{$G$-Invariant Explanation Systems}
\label{sec:g-invariant}

Let $G$ be a group acting on the hypothesis space~$\sH$.  The action is
\emph{observable-preserving} if for every $g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{obs}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{obs}(h),
\]
i.e., the group permutes hypotheses within observe-equivalence classes
(fibers).  We say that $G$ \emph{acts transitively on observe-fibers} if for
every pair $h, h'$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$, there exists $g \in G$
such that $g \cdot h = h'$.

\begin{definition}[$G$-Invariant Explanation Map]
\label{def:g-invariant}
An explanation map $\mathsf{exp}$ is \emph{$G$-invariant} if for every
$g \in G$ and $h \in \sH$,
\[
  \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) \;=\; \mathsf{exp}(h).
\]
\end{definition}

When $G$ is finite and $Y$ admits averaging, the canonical $G$-invariant map
is the \emph{orbit average}:
\[
  \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h).
\]

\begin{proposition}[$G$-Invariance Implies Stability]
\label{prop:g-invariant-stable}
If $G$ acts transitively on observe-fibers and $\mathsf{exp}$ is
$G$-invariant, then $\mathsf{exp}$ is stable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $h, h' \in \sH$ with $\theta(h) = \theta(h')$.  By transitivity, there
exists $g \in G$ with $g \cdot h = h'$.  By $G$-invariance,
$\mathsf{exp}(h') = \mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h) = \mathsf{exp}(h)$.
(Formalized as \texttt{gInvariant\_stable} in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Design Space Dichotomy]
\label{prop:dichotomy}
If the explanation system has the Rashomon property, then no stable
explanation map can simultaneously be faithful and decisive.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}: stability, faithfulness, and
decisiveness are jointly contradictory under the Rashomon property.
(Formalized as \texttt{universal\_design\_space\_dichotomy} in
\texttt{UniversalDesignSpace.lean}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Pareto Frontier]
\label{cor:pareto}
For any explanation system with the Rashomon property, achievable
explanation maps fall into exactly two families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Family~A} (faithful $+$ decisive, unstable): individual-model
    explanations that faithfully report each hypothesis but vary across
    equivalent hypotheses.
  \item \textbf{Family~B} (stable $+$ faithful in expectation, indecisive):
    $G$-invariant aggregations that are constant on observe-fibers but
    produce ties for queries where the fiber disagrees.
\end{itemize}
No explanation map lies outside these two families.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{\DASH{} as the Prototype Resolution}
\label{sec:dash-prototype}

\DASH{} (\textbf{D}iversified \textbf{A}ggregation of \textbf{SH}AP)
is the $G$-invariant resolution for the feature attribution instance
(Section~\ref{sec:instance-attribution}).  It averages SHAP values over an
ensemble of models retrained from independent random seeds, achieving
$\E[\phi_j] = \E[\phi_k]$ for any pair of symmetric features $j, k$
(\emph{consensus equity}).  This orbit average is stable by
Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable} and faithful in expectation: the
expected explanation matches the expected observation across the Rashomon set.
The cost is indecisiveness---\DASH{} reports ties for feature pairs whose
rankings disagree across the ensemble.  The companion paper proves that
\DASH{} is Pareto-optimal: no other stable attribution method can achieve
higher expected faithfulness.

\subsection{Meta-Theorem: Orbit Averaging}
\label{sec:meta-theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Orbit-Averaged Resolution]
\label{thm:orbit-average}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be an explanation system whose Rashomon property arises
from a finite group $G$ acting on $\sH$ and acting transitively on
observe-fibers.  Then the orbit-averaged map
$\overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h) = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G}\mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h)$
is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item well-defined (independent of orbit representative),
  \item stable (Proposition~\ref{prop:g-invariant-stable}),
  \item faithful in expectation: $\E_{g \sim \mathrm{Unif}(G)}[\mathsf{exp}(g \cdot h)] = \overline{\mathsf{exp}}(h)$, and
  \item Pareto-optimal among stable maps: no stable map achieves strictly
    higher pointwise faithfulness on every fiber.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent
The orbit-averaged resolution is constructive: for any specific instance, the
group $G$ and its action are determined by the domain structure, and the
averaging can be computed (or approximated by Monte Carlo sampling over the
Rashomon set).  Theorem~\ref{thm:orbit-average} is the abstract counterpart
of the \DASH{} optimality result and is formalized in
\texttt{UniversalResolution.lean}.
The resolution strategy---restricting to $G$-invariant explanation maps---is
structurally analogous to the invariant decision procedures justified by the
Hunt--Stein theorem \citep{lehmann2005testing}: just as the Hunt--Stein theorem
guarantees that invariant tests are minimax optimal when a group acts on the
parameter space, the $G$-invariant aggregation is Pareto-optimal among stable
explanation methods.

\section{Ubiquity}
\label{sec:ubiquity}
%%% ==========================================================================

Is the Explanation Impossibility a pathological corner case, or a
generic phenomenon?  In this section we show that it is the latter: the
Rashomon property---and hence the impossibility---holds whenever the model
has more parameters than observational constraints, a condition satisfied by
virtually all modern machine learning systems.

\subsection{Generic Underspecification}
\label{sec:generic-underspec}

\begin{proposition}[Dimensional Underspecification]
\label{prop:generic-underspec}
Let $\sH$ be a smooth hypothesis space of dimension $\dim(\sH)$ and let
$\mathsf{obs} : \sH \to Y$ be a smooth observation map into a space of
dimension $\dim(Y)$.  If $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$, then for generic
$y \in Y$, the fiber $\mathsf{obs}^{-1}(y)$ has positive dimension
$\geq \dim(\sH) - \dim(Y)$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent
This is a direct consequence of the preimage theorem in differential
topology.  Whenever the fiber has positive dimension, the explanation map
$\mathsf{obs}$ is generically non-constant on fibers---different hypotheses
in the same fiber honestly report different explanations---and the Rashomon
property holds.  (Formalized as \texttt{generic\_underspecification} in
\texttt{Ubiquity.lean}; the bridge from fiber non-degeneracy to the
impossibility is \texttt{fiber\_nondegeneracy\_implies\_impossibility}.)

\subsection{Neural Network Dimensional Argument}
\label{sec:nn-dimensional}

Modern neural networks are massively overparameterized.  A ResNet-50 has
$P \approx 25{,}600{,}000$ parameters and, for ImageNet classification,
$k = 1{,}000$ output classes.  By Proposition~\ref{prop:generic-underspec},
the fiber of any fixed prediction has dimension at least
$P - k = 24{,}999{,}000$.  On such a high-dimensional manifold, any
continuous explanation map (attention weights, gradient saliency, internal
activations) is generically non-constant: there exist models $h, h'$ on the
same fiber with $\mathsf{obs}(h)_j > \mathsf{obs}(h)_k$ and
$\mathsf{obs}(h')_j < \mathsf{obs}(h')_k$ for any pair of input features
$j, k$ with equal marginal distributions.

\begin{corollary}[Coin-Flip Instability]
\label{cor:coin-flip}
For any pair of symmetric features $j, k$ in an overparameterized network
trained to zero loss, the probability (over retraining seeds) that a
faithful explanation ranks $j$ above $k$ is exactly $1/2$.
\end{corollary}

\noindent
This is the neural-network instantiation of the symmetric Bayes
result formalized in \texttt{Symmetric\-Bayes.lean}: the symmetry
of the fiber under feature permutation forces uniform marginals on
the ranking.

\subsection{Prevalence}
\label{sec:prevalence}

The dimensional condition $\dim(\sH) > \dim(Y)$ is the norm, not the
exception.  Large language models (billions of parameters, vocabulary-sized
output), diffusion models, graph neural networks, random forests with
hundreds of trees, and Lasso regression with more features than observations
all satisfy it.  Even linear regression with collinear features produces a
positive-dimensional fiber (the collinear subspace).
\citet{damour2022underspecification} document this across NLP,
computer vision, and medical imaging, finding that
underspecification---and hence explanation instability---is ubiquitous
in deployed systems.  The only escape is a model class so constrained
that $\dim(\sH) \leq \dim(Y)$, which excludes essentially all modern
architectures of practical interest.

\subsection{Analogy: Arrow's Impossibility Theorem}
\label{sec:arrow-analogy}

The structure of our result parallels Arrow's impossibility theorem in social
choice theory \citep{arrow1951social}.  Arrow showed that no voting rule
satisfying unanimity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, and
non-dictatorship exists for three or more candidates---a universal
impossibility that unified disparate paradoxes in voting theory.
\citet{nipkow2009social} later formalized Arrow's theorem in Isabelle/HOL.
Our Explanation Impossibility plays an analogous role for
explainability: it unifies nine domain-specific impossibilities under one
structural cause (the Rashomon property) and one four-line proof, and the
resolution (orbit averaging) is the XAI counterpart of accepting partial
orderings in social choice.
(The full impossibility is formalized as \texttt{ubiquity\_impossibility}
in \texttt{Ubiquity.lean}.)

\section{Discussion: What Explainability Can and Cannot Do}
\label{sec:discussion}
%%% ==========================================================================

\subsection{The Ceiling of Explainability}
\label{sec:ceiling}

The Explanation Impossibility (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) establishes
a \emph{ceiling}, not a criticism of any particular method.  No axiomatic
redesign of \SHAP{}, LIME, attention maps, or counterfactual generators can
simultaneously deliver faithfulness, stability, and decisiveness whenever the
underlying inference problem is underspecified.  The ceiling is structural: it
follows from the Rashomon property alone, independent of the explanation
formalism.  An analogy to physics is apt: the speed of light constrains
spacecraft engineering but does not prevent useful space travel.  Likewise, the
impossibility constrains what practitioners may promise about explanations but
does not prevent useful explainability---it redirects effort toward the
achievable frontier characterized by the $G$-invariant resolution
(Section~\ref{sec:resolution}).

\subsection{Regulatory Implications}
\label{sec:regulatory}

The EU AI Act \citep{euaiact2024} requires ``meaningful explanations'' for
high-risk systems, and SR~11-7 \citep{occ2011sr117} requires ``developmental
evidence'' documenting how model outputs arise.  These two demands pull in
opposite directions: meaningful explanations favor decisiveness (a definite
ranking of features or causes), while developmental evidence favors stability
(reproducibility across equivalent models).  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal} shows
that no explanation system can simultaneously maximize both for underspecified
models.  A path to regulatory compliance nonetheless exists: $G$-invariant
aggregation (e.g., \DASH{} for attributions, CPDAGs for causal reports)
satisfies stability by construction and partial faithfulness---faithfulness in
expectation over the Rashomon set---at the cost of reporting ties where the
data are genuinely ambiguous.  We suggest that regulators adopt this framing:
require that explanations be \emph{stable and partially faithful}, and mandate
explicit disclosure of tied or unresolvable queries rather than demanding
decisive answers that are mathematically impossible to guarantee.

\subsection[What Explainability Can Do]{What Explainability \emph{Can} Do}
\label{sec:can-do}

The $G$-invariant resolution (Section~\ref{sec:resolution}) is constructive, not
nihilistic.  It tells the practitioner exactly which desiderata can be jointly
satisfied and which must be relaxed.  \DASH{} for feature attribution, CPDAGs
for causal discovery \citep{verma1991equivalence}, and ensemble concept probes
for concept-based explanations \citep{kim2018tcav} each sacrifice decisiveness
to gain stability---and each is a concrete instance of the abstract resolution.
In practice, this means reporting confidence intervals over the Rashomon set
\citep{fisher2019models,semenova2022existence} rather than point estimates:
a feature ranking should come with a stability annotation indicating which
comparisons are robust to retraining and which are not.  The impossibility does
not say ``explanations are useless''; it says ``decisive explanations are
unreliable under underspecification, but partially decisive explanations with
calibrated uncertainty are both achievable and informative.''

\subsection[The Isn't This Obvious Defense]{The ``Isn't This Obvious?'' Defense}
\label{sec:obvious}

A natural reaction is that practitioners already know explanations are unstable
---so what is new?  The contribution is fivefold.  First, we make explicit that
eight scientific domains and nine ML explanation types share \emph{one}
mechanism---the Rashomon property---rather than seventeen unrelated
pathologies.  Second, the Lean~4
formalization (Section~\ref{sec:lean}: 82~files, 377~theorems, 0~\texttt{sorry})
eliminates logical gaps that informal arguments inevitably leave.  Third, the
empirical experiments (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}) show quantifiable severity:
33\% attribution flip rates, measurable counterfactual and concept instability on
standard benchmarks.  Fourth, the resolution via $G$-invariant maps is
constructive and actionable, not merely diagnostic.  Fifth, Lean-verified
tightness theorems (Proposition~\ref{prop:tightness}) prove the impossibility
is non-trivial: each pair of properties \emph{is} simultaneously achievable,
so the theorem is not an artifact of overly strong definitions---it identifies
the exact boundary between the possible and the impossible.
Arrow's impossibility
theorem \citep{arrow1951social} was arguably ``obvious'' once stated---every
committee chair knows that voting rules are imperfect.  Yet its formalization
created the field of social choice theory, precisely because it replaced
intuition with a theorem that could be built upon.

\subsection[The All Instances Are Trivial Defense]{The ``All Instances Are Trivial'' Defense}
\label{sec:trivial}

A reviewer might object that each individual instance is already known: attention
instability was documented by \citet{jainwallace2019}, Markov equivalence is
classical \citep{verma1991equivalence}, fairness impossibility was proved by
\citet{chouldechova2017fair}, and \SHAP{} instability was observed by
\citet{damour2022underspecification} and \citet{krishna2022disagreement}.  The
contribution is not any single instance but the \emph{meta-theorem}: the same
four-line proof (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}) closes all seventeen cases---eight
derived scientific instances and nine ML instances---under a
single structural condition.  Before this work, a practitioner encountering
attention instability might switch to \SHAP{}, then to counterfactual
explanations, then to concept probes---each time hoping the new method escapes
the problem.  Our theorem shows that no such escape exists: the impossibility
is structural (it follows from the Rashomon property), not method-specific.
Recognizing this unifies the practitioner's response: rather than debugging each
method individually, one diagnoses the Rashomon property and applies the
$G$-invariant resolution uniformly.

\subsection{Implications Beyond Machine Learning}
\label{sec:beyond-ml}

The Explanation Impossibility is not specific to machine learning.

\paragraph{Philosophy of science: formalizing Quine--Duhem.}
Quine \citep{quine1951dogmas} and Duhem \citep{duhem1906aim} argued that
empirical evidence underdetermines theory choice
(Duhem on the impossibility of testing hypotheses in isolation; Quine on
confirmational holism---sharing the core implication that observational
equivalence leaves explanation underdetermined).  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal}
proves an impossibility theorem for explanation under observational
equivalence---formalizing, to our knowledge for the first time, the explanatory
consequences of the Duhem-Quine thesis of holist underdetermination
\citep{laudan1991empirical}; note that Laudan himself was skeptical of the
underdetermination thesis, but the citation documents the philosophical context.
Prior formal analyses of underdetermination in Bayesian
frameworks \citep{dorling1979bayesian,howson2006scientific} did not yield
impossibility results for explanation methods. The $G$-invariant
resolution is structurally reminiscent of epistemic structural
realism \citep{worrall1989structural}
(Worrall's argument is diachronic---structural content survives theory change
across scientific revolutions; our framework is synchronic---structural content
is shared across equivalent models within one paradigm): stable explanatory
content is what is invariant across equivalent configurations. The connection
is suggestive; a rigorous mapping would require engaging with the ontological
commitments of structural realism, which our descriptive framework does not
address.
Dropping decisiveness corresponds to van
Fraassen's \citep{vanfraassen1980scientific} constructive empiricist stance
that science aims for empirical adequacy, not full theoretical truth.
Okasha \citep{okasha2011theory} connected theory choice to Arrow's impossibility
in social choice; our result provides the complementary connection for
underdetermination of explanation.

\paragraph{Neuroscience: degeneracy as the Rashomon property.}
Marder and Goaillard \citep{marder2006variability} documented that crustacean
stomatogastric ganglia producing identical motor patterns are built from
qualitatively different ionic conductance profiles.  Edelman and Gally
\citep{edelman2001degeneracy} formalized this as \emph{degeneracy}:
structurally distinct elements performing the same function.  Degeneracy is
precisely the Rashomon property in our framework.

\paragraph{Economics: partial identification.}
Manski \citep{manski2003partial} developed partial identification theory for
settings where observational data do not pin down a unique model.  The
identified set corresponds to an observe-fiber in our framework; partial
identification is the economic instantiation of the Rashomon property.

\paragraph{Medical diagnosis.}
When multiple conditions produce the same symptoms, diagnosis faces the
trilemma.  Differential diagnosis is precisely $G$-invariant resolution:
it sacrifices decisiveness for faithfulness and stability.

\paragraph{Arrow's theorem as structural analogy.}
The result shares structural features with Arrow's impossibility
theorem \citep{arrow1951social}: three individually reasonable desiderata
that are pairwise satisfiable but jointly impossible under a natural
background condition.  Like Arrow's theorem, the proof is short once the
axioms are identified; the contribution lies in identifying the right
axiomatic framework.

The pattern extends to many further domains: metamerism in color perception (where the cone response matrix is the observation map, making it an instance of the linear system pattern), molecular chirality (where enantiomers share achiral observables), and others.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Questions}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify five limitations and directions for future work.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{Axiomatized Rashomon properties for ML instances.}  The
    Rashomon property for each ML instance is axiomatized---verified to hold
    under stated assumptions---rather than derived from first principles of the
    learning algorithm.  (The eight non-ML derived instances in
    Section~\ref{sec:derived-instances} are the exceptions: each derives the
    Rashomon property constructively with zero axioms.)  Deriving the Rashomon
    property from, e.g., the loss landscape geometry of gradient descent would
    strengthen the ML results.
  \item \textbf{Weight perturbation vs.\ true retraining.}  The attention
    instability experiments (Section~\ref{sec:ml-instances}) use weight
    perturbation within the zero-loss manifold rather than full retraining from
    independent random seeds.  While this faithfully models the Rashomon set,
    the distribution over perturbations may differ from the distribution induced
    by stochastic gradient descent.
  \item \textbf{Non-attribution quantitative bounds.}  For the attribution
    instance, the companion paper provides sharp quantitative bounds (divergence
    rates, ensemble size requirements, \DASH{} optimality).  Analogous
    quantitative bounds for the counterfactual, concept, and fairness instances
    remain open.
  \item \textbf{Decisiveness as a practical cost.}  The $G$-invariant
    resolution sacrifices decisiveness: it reports ties where the data are
    ambiguous.  Whether downstream decision-makers can tolerate partial rankings
    or tied causal structures---and how to present such outputs
    effectively---is an empirical question we do not address here.
  \item \textbf{Fairness instance not experimentally validated.}  The fairness
    audit impossibility (Instance~6, formalized in \texttt{FairnessAudit.lean})
    is proved but not empirically validated in this paper.
    \citet{chouldechova2017fair} provides the original empirical context;
    replicating it within our framework is left to future work.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{ExplanationSystem.lean --- Core Framework and Impossibility}
\label{app:lean-explanation-system}

This file defines the abstract \texttt{ExplanationSystem} structure, the three
desiderata (faithfulness, stability, decisiveness), and proves the universal
impossibility with zero axiom dependencies.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={ExplanationSystem.lean --- complete file},label={lst:explanation-system}]
/-!
# Abstract Explanation System

The universal impossibility framework. An explanation system S : Theta -> Y
is explained by E : Theta -> H. The Rashomon property says observationally
equivalent configurations can produce incompatible explanations.

## The Trilemma

No explanation E can simultaneously be:
- **Faithful**: E(theta) never contradicts the system's native explanation
- **Stable**: E(theta_1) = E(theta_2) whenever observe(theta_1) = observe(theta_2)
- **Decisive**: E(theta) commits to every distinction the native explanation makes

Each pair is achievable:
- Faithful + stable: abstain on controversial distinctions (ties)
- Faithful + decisive: use the native explanation (unstable under retraining)
- Stable + decisive: pick an arbitrary fixed explanation (unfaithful)
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

/-- An explanation system with configuration space Theta, explanation space H,
    and observable space Y. -/
structure ExplanationSystem (Theta : Type) (H : Type) (Y : Type) where
  /-- The observation map: what the system produces. -/
  observe : Theta -> Y
  /-- The explanation map: how we interpret a configuration. -/
  explain : Theta -> H
  /-- Incompatibility relation on explanations. -/
  incompatible : H -> H -> Prop
  /-- Incompatibility is irreflexive: no explanation contradicts itself. -/
  incompatible_irrefl : forall (h : H), not (incompatible h h)
  /-- The Rashomon property: equivalent configurations produce incompatible
      explanations. -/
  rashomon : exists theta_1 theta_2 : Theta,
    observe theta_1 = observe theta_2
    /\ incompatible (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2)

variable {Theta : Type} {H : Type} {Y : Type}

/-- Faithful: E never contradicts the native explanation.
    E(theta) may be less specific than explain(theta) but never disagrees
    with it. -/
def faithful (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta : Theta), not (S.incompatible (E theta) (S.explain theta))

/-- Stability: the explanation factors through the observable map.
    Equivalent configurations get the same explanation. -/
def stable (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta),
    S.observe theta_1 = S.observe theta_2
    -> E theta_1 = E theta_2

/-- Decisive: E inherits every incompatibility of the native explanation.
    Whatever the true explanation rules out, E also rules out.
    E is at least as committal as explain. -/
def decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) (E : Theta -> H) : Prop :=
  forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
    S.incompatible (S.explain theta) h -> S.incompatible (E theta) h

/-- The Explanation Impossibility.

No explanation of a system with the Rashomon property can be simultaneously
faithful, stable, and decisive.

Proof structure:
1. Rashomon gives theta_1, theta_2 with same output but incompatible explanations
2. Decisiveness at theta_1: incompatible(explain theta_1, explain theta_2)
     -> incompatible(E theta_1, explain theta_2)
3. Stability: observe theta_1 = observe theta_2 -> E theta_1 = E theta_2,
     so incompatible(E theta_2, explain theta_2)
4. Faithfulness at theta_2: not incompatible(E theta_2, explain theta_2)
5. Contradiction between 3 and 4 -/
theorem explanation_impossibility (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (E : Theta -> H) (hf : faithful S E) (hs : stable S E)
    (hd : decisive S E) :
    False := by
  obtain <theta_1, theta_2, hobs, hinc> := S.rashomon
  have h1 : S.incompatible (E theta_1) (S.explain theta_2) :=
    hd theta_1 (S.explain theta_2) hinc
  have h2 : E theta_1 = E theta_2 := hs theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  rw [h2] at h1
  exact hf theta_2 h1

-- ============================================================
-- Tightness: each pair of properties is achievable
-- ============================================================

/-- **Tightness 1: Faithful + Decisive (dropping Stable).**
    E = explain is always faithful and decisive. -/
theorem tightness_faithful_decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y) :
    faithful S S.explain /\ decisive S S.explain := by
  constructor
  . intro theta
    exact S.incompatible_irrefl (S.explain theta)
  . intro theta h hinc
    exact hinc

/-- **Tightness 2: Faithful + Stable (dropping Decisive).**
    The constant function E(theta) = c is faithful and stable
    when c is compatible with every native explanation. -/
theorem tightness_faithful_stable (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (c : H) (hc : forall (theta : Theta),
      not (S.incompatible c (S.explain theta))) :
    faithful S (fun _ => c) /\ stable S (fun _ => c) := by
  constructor
  . intro theta
    exact hc theta
  . intro _ _ _
    rfl

/-- **Tightness 3: Stable + Decisive (dropping Faithful).**
    The constant function E(theta) = c is stable and decisive
    when c inherits every incompatibility. -/
theorem tightness_stable_decisive (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (c : H) (hc : forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
      S.incompatible (S.explain theta) h -> S.incompatible c h) :
    stable S (fun _ => c) /\ decisive S (fun _ => c) := by
  constructor
  . intro _ _ _
    rfl
  . intro theta h hinc
    exact hc theta h hinc
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection[UniversalResolution.lean --- G-Invariant Resolution]{UniversalResolution.lean --- $G$-Invariant Resolution}
\label{app:lean-resolution}

This file formalizes the resolution framework: if a group $G$ acts transitively
on observe-fibers and the resolution is $G$-invariant, then it is automatically
stable.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={UniversalResolution.lean --- complete file},label={lst:resolution}]
import UniversalImpossibility.ExplanationSystem
import Mathlib.GroupTheory.GroupAction.Defs

/-!
# G-Invariant Resolution Framework
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

variable {Theta : Type} {H : Type} {Y : Type} {G : Type}

/-- A symmetry structure on an explanation system. -/
structure HasSymmetry (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (G : Type) [Group G] [MulAction G Theta] where
  observe_invariant :
    forall (g : G) (theta : Theta),
      S.observe (g * theta) = S.observe theta
  fiber_transitive :
    forall (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta),
      S.observe theta_1 = S.observe theta_2
      -> exists (g : G), g * theta_1 = theta_2

/-- A resolution R is G-invariant if constant on G-orbits. -/
def gInvariant (R : Theta -> H) (G : Type)
    [Group G] [MulAction G Theta] : Prop :=
  forall (g : G) (theta : Theta), R (g * theta) = R theta

/-- G-invariance + fiber transitivity implies stability. -/
theorem gInvariant_stable
    (S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y)
    (R : Theta -> H)
    [Group G] [MulAction G Theta]
    (sym : HasSymmetry S G)
    (hInv : gInvariant R G) :
    stable S R := by
  intro theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  obtain <g, hg> := sym.fiber_transitive theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  have h := hInv g theta_1
  rw [hg] at h
  exact h.symm
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Ubiquity.lean --- Structural Ubiquity}
\label{app:lean-ubiquity}

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Ubiquity.lean --- complete file},label={lst:ubiquity}]
import UniversalImpossibility.ExplanationSystem

/-!
# Structural Ubiquity of Explanation Impossibility

The impossibility is not a pathological corner case -- it is generic whenever
the configuration space has higher dimension than the observable space.

## Main results

- `generic_underspecification`: if dim(Theta) > dim(Y), the fiber has
  positive dimension.
- `fiber_nondegeneracy_implies_impossibility`: if the explanation map is
  non-constant on a fiber, then no explanation can be faithful, stable,
  and decisive.
- `ubiquity_impossibility`: constructs an ExplanationSystem from witness
  data and derives the impossibility via `explanation_impossibility`.
-/

set_option autoImplicit false

-- ============================================================
-- Dimensional argument
-- ============================================================

/-- Generic underspecification: if the configuration space has higher
    dimension than the observable space, the system is generically
    underspecified. -/
theorem generic_underspecification {n m : Nat} (h : m < n) :
    n - m > 0 := by
  omega

-- ============================================================
-- Non-degeneracy -> impossibility bridge
-- ============================================================

/-- If two configurations have the same observable output but incompatible
    explanations, and the incompatibility relation is irreflexive, then
    no explanation can be faithful, stable, and decisive. -/
theorem fiber_nondegeneracy_implies_impossibility
    {Theta H Y : Type}
    (observe : Theta -> Y) (explain : Theta -> H)
    (incomp : H -> H -> Prop)
    (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta)
    (hobs : observe theta_1 = observe theta_2)
    (hinc : incomp (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2))
    (E : Theta -> H)
    (hf : forall (theta : Theta), not (incomp (E theta) (explain theta)))
    (hs : forall a b : Theta,
      observe a = observe b -> E a = E b)
    (hd : forall (theta : Theta) (h : H),
      incomp (explain theta) h -> incomp (E theta) h) :
    False := by
  have h1 : incomp (E theta_1) (explain theta_2) :=
    hd theta_1 (explain theta_2) hinc
  have h2 : E theta_1 = E theta_2 := hs theta_1 theta_2 hobs
  rw [h2] at h1
  exact hf theta_2 h1

-- ============================================================
-- Connection to ExplanationSystem
-- ============================================================

/-- Given concrete witness data, construct an ExplanationSystem and derive
    the impossibility. -/
theorem ubiquity_impossibility
    {Theta H Y : Type}
    (observe : Theta -> Y) (explain : Theta -> H)
    (incomp : H -> H -> Prop)
    (incomp_irrefl : forall (h : H), not (incomp h h))
    (theta_1 theta_2 : Theta)
    (hobs : observe theta_1 = observe theta_2)
    (hinc : incomp (explain theta_1) (explain theta_2)) :
    let S : ExplanationSystem Theta H Y :=
      { observe, explain, incompatible := incomp,
        incompatible_irrefl := incomp_irrefl,
        rashomon := <theta_1, theta_2, hobs, hinc> }
    forall (E : Theta -> H),
      faithful S E -> stable S E -> decisive S E
      -> False := by
  intro S E hf hs hd
  exact explanation_impossibility S E hf hs hd
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Detailed Experiment Methodology}
\label{app:experiments}
%%% ==========================================================================

This appendix provides full details of the four empirical experiments
validating Instances 2--5 of the universal impossibility.  All experiments
are reproducible via:
\begin{verbatim}
python paper/scripts/run_all_universal_experiments.py
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Attention Map Instability (Instance 2)}
\label{app:exp-attention}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do functionally equivalent transformer models assign peak attention to
different tokens?

\paragraph{Model.}
DistilBERT-base-uncased \citep{sanh2019distilbert}, a 6-layer, 12-head
transformer with 66M parameters.

\paragraph{Perturbation protocol.}
We create 10 model variants by adding Gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma)$
to all weight matrices of a pretrained DistilBERT checkpoint.  Five models use
$\sigma = 0.01$ and five use $\sigma = 0.02$.  This simulates the Rashomon set
of models reachable by different training trajectories that arrive at the same
loss basin.

\paragraph{Dataset.}
200 synthetic sentiment sentences (100 positive, 100 negative), constructed
from 10 positive and 10 negative templates with 10 variations each.

\paragraph{Attention extraction.}
For each sentence and each model, we extract attention weights from all 6 layers
using attention rollout (the product of attention matrices across layers),
producing a distribution over tokens.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Prediction agreement}: fraction of sentences where all 10 models
    produce the same classification.
  \item \textbf{Argmax flip rate}: fraction of model pairs $(i,j)$ and sentences
    where $\arg\max_t \alpha^{(i)}_t \neq \arg\max_t \alpha^{(j)}_t$.
  \item \textbf{Mean Kendall $\tau$}: average rank correlation between attention
    distributions across model pairs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
95\% confidence intervals are computed via the percentile method with 2000
bootstrap resamples over sentence--model-pair combinations.

\paragraph{Results.}
Prediction agreement: 100\%.  Argmax flip rate: 60.0\% [95\% CI: 59--61\%].
Mean Kendall $\tau$: 0.297 [CI: 0.287--0.307].

\subsection{Experiment 2: Counterfactual Instability (Instance 3)}
\label{app:exp-counterfactual}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do equivalent-accuracy models produce contradictory counterfactual explanations?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
German Credit (UCI, OpenML \texttt{credit-g}), 1000 instances, 20 features
after one-hot encoding of categorical variables.  Numerical features are
standardized.

\paragraph{Models.}
20 XGBoost classifiers with identical hyperparameters (default \texttt{xgb.XGBClassifier}
settings), differing only in random seed.  Train/test split: 80/20 with a fixed
random state for the split.

\paragraph{Counterfactual method.}
Greedy feature-importance-ordered perturbation toward the positive-class centroid.
For each query point $x_0$ classified as ``bad credit,'' features are perturbed
in order of their importance (measured by XGBoost feature importance) toward the
mean of the ``good credit'' class, until the prediction flips.

\paragraph{Query points.}
45--50 test-set instances classified as ``bad credit'' by at least 18 of the
20 models (to ensure the counterfactual direction is well-defined for most
models).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Direction flip rate}: fraction of model pairs recommending
    opposite feature changes for the same query.  Two counterfactuals ``flip''
    if any feature is perturbed in opposite directions.
  \item \textbf{Cross-model recourse validity}: fraction of counterfactuals
    generated for model $i$ that also flip the prediction on model $j$.
  \item \textbf{Distance instability (CV)}: coefficient of variation of
    $\ell_2$ counterfactual distances across models.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
500 bootstrap resamples over query points for 95\% percentile CIs.

\paragraph{Results.}
Mean AUC: 0.797 [CI: 0.783--0.810].  Direction flip rate: 23.5\%
[CI: 20.6--26.6\%].  Cross-model validity: 61.3\% [CI: 55.8--66.2\%].
Distance CV: 0.328 [CI: 0.281--0.372].

\subsection{Experiment 3: Concept Probe Instability (Instance 4)}
\label{app:exp-concept}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Do functionally equivalent neural networks encode the same concept in
incompatible directions in representation space?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
\texttt{sklearn.datasets.load\_digits}: 1797 handwritten digit images
($8 \times 8$ pixels, 10 classes).

\paragraph{Models.}
15 \texttt{MLPClassifier} models with architecture $(128, 64)$, ReLU
activation, trained with different random seeds (\texttt{random\_state}
$= 42, 43, \ldots, 56$).  All models achieve $>95\%$ test accuracy on
a 70/30 split.

\paragraph{Concept definitions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Curved digits}: $\{0, 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 9\}$ vs.\ angular
    $\{1, 4, 7\}$.
  \item \textbf{Symmetric digits}: $\{0, 1, 8\}$ vs.\ rest.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Concept direction extraction.}
For each model, extract penultimate-layer activations (64-dimensional) on
the test set.  Fit a \texttt{LinearSVC} to separate concept-positive from
concept-negative examples.  The resulting weight vector is the concept
activation vector (CAV).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Mean $|\cos(\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j)|$}: pairwise cosine
    similarity of CAVs (off-diagonal entries of the $15 \times 15$ matrix).
  \item \textbf{Concept direction instability}: $1 - |\cos|$.
  \item \textbf{TCAV score std}: standard deviation of TCAV sensitivity scores
    across models.
  \item \textbf{Prediction agreement}: fraction of test points classified
    identically by all 15 models.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Bootstrap.}
2000 bootstrap resamples for 95\% CIs.

\paragraph{Results.}
Mean $|\cos|$: 0.10 [CI: 0.086--0.114].  Direction instability: 0.90.
TCAV std (curved): 0.157.  TCAV std (symmetric): 0.123.  Prediction
agreement: 97.2\% [CI: 95.7--98.5\%].

\subsection{Experiment 4: Model Selection Instability (Instance 6)}
\label{app:exp-model-selection}

\paragraph{Research question.}
Does the identity of the ``best'' model change when evaluated on different
data splits?

\paragraph{Dataset.}
German Credit (credit-g, OpenML), same as Experiment~2.

\paragraph{Models.}
50 XGBoost classifiers, all trained on a single fixed 80/20 split, differing
only in random seed (\texttt{random\_state} $= 0, 1, \ldots, 49$).

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
Each of 20 independent random 80/20 evaluation splits is used to compute
test-set AUC for all 50 models.  For each split, the ``best'' model is the
one with the highest AUC.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Best-model flip rate}: fraction of consecutive evaluation
    splits where the identity of the best model changes.
  \item \textbf{Unique winners}: number of distinct models that are ``best''
    on at least one evaluation split.
  \item \textbf{AUC spread}: mean range of AUC values across the 50 models
    within a single evaluation split.
  \item \textbf{Overall mean AUC}: mean $\pm$ std of AUC across all
    models and splits.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Results.}
Best-model flip rate: 80\%.  Consecutive flip rate: 89\%.  Unique winners:
11 out of 50.  Mean AUC spread per split: 0.0197.  Overall AUC:
$0.948 \pm 0.0023$.

\section{Proof Sketches for All Nine Instances}
\label{app:proof-sketches}
%%% ==========================================================================

Each instance follows the same four-step proof structure: (1)~establish the
Rashomon property, (2)~faithfulness forces $E = \mathsf{obs}$,
(3)~decisiveness forces $E(\theta_1) \neq E(\theta_2)$,
(4)~stability forces $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, yielding a contradiction.
Below we spell out the domain-specific content for all nine instances.

\subsection{Instance 1: Feature Attribution}
\label{app:proof-attribution}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ data-generating distributions with collinearity structure.
$\sH ={}$ trained models (GBDT, Lasso, neural net).
$Y = (\mathrm{Fin}\,P \to \R)$: feature attribution vectors.
$\mathsf{obs}(f) = \boldsymbol{\varphi}(f)$: the model's own SHAP values.
$\mathsf{exp}$: the attribution procedure under evaluation.
$\incomp$: strict reversal of a collinear pair ($\varphi_j > \varphi_k$ vs.\
$\varphi_k > \varphi_j$).

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Collinear features within a group $\ell$ are symmetric under the DGP.
Surjectivity of the first-mover map guarantees that for any collinear pair
$j, k$, there exist models $f, f'$ with the same DGP but opposite rankings.
This is the \texttt{RashomonProperty} of \texttt{Trilemma.lean}.

\paragraph{Proof.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Rashomon: $\exists f, f'$ with $\theta(f) = \theta(f')$ and
    $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$, $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$.
  \item Faithful $E$ reports $\varphi_j(f) > \varphi_k(f)$ for $f$ and
    $\varphi_k(f') > \varphi_j(f')$ for $f'$.
  \item Decisive: $E(f) \neq E(f')$ (rankings differ).
  \item Stable: $E(f) = E(f')$ (same DGP).
  \item Contradiction.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Lean.}
{\raggedright
\texttt{attribution\_impossibility} in \texttt{Trilemma.lean}
(0~axioms);
\texttt{attribution\_impossibility\_abstract} in
\texttt{AttributionInstance.lean}
(0~additional axioms beyond system).\par}

\subsection{Instance 2: Attention Maps}
\label{app:proof-attention}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ neural network weight configurations.
$Y ={}$ input$\to$output functions (the behavior the network computes).
$\sH = \Delta^T$: attention distributions over $T$ tokens.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta$: the function the network computes.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = \alpha_\theta$: the attention distribution.
$\incomp$: $\arg\max$ differs.

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
\citet{damour2022underspecification}: $\exists \theta_1, \theta_2$ with
$f_{\theta_1} \approx f_{\theta_2}$ yet
$\arg\max_t (\alpha_{\theta_1})_t \neq \arg\max_t (\alpha_{\theta_2})_t$.

\paragraph{Proof.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Rashomon: equivalent models with different max-attended tokens.
  \item Faithful $E$ reports $\alpha_{\theta_1}$ for $\theta_1$ and
    $\alpha_{\theta_2}$ for $\theta_2$.
  \item Decisive: $E(\theta_1) \neq E(\theta_2)$.
  \item Stable: $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$.
  \item Contradiction.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Lean.}
\texttt{attention\_impossibility} in \texttt{AttentionInstance.lean}.

\subsection{Instance 3: Counterfactual Explanations}
\label{app:proof-counterfactual}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ model parameters (decision boundaries).
$Y ={}$ predictions on a test set.
$\sH = \mathcal{X}$: nearest counterfactual examples.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta|_{\mathrm{test}}$.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = \arg\min_{x'} \|x' - x_0\|$ s.t.\ $f_\theta(x') \neq f_\theta(x_0)$.
$\incomp$: counterfactuals point in different directions.

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Models with identical test predictions place their decision boundaries in
different locations near the query point $x_0$, yielding counterfactuals
in opposite directions \citep{semenova2022existence,fisher2019models}.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Same four-step structure.  Lean:
\texttt{counterfactual\_impossibility} in \texttt{Counterfactual\-Instance.lean}.

\subsection{Instance 4: Concept Probes}
\label{app:proof-concept}

\paragraph{Setup.}
$\Params ={}$ model weights (defines internal representations).
$Y ={}$ predictions (input-output function).
$\sH = \R^c$: concept activation vectors.
$\mathsf{obs}(\theta) = f_\theta$.
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta) = v_\theta$: concept direction from linear probe.
$\incomp$: directions differ (not related by rotation in concept subspace).

\paragraph{Rashomon property.}
Equivalent models encode concepts in different directions
\citep{bolukbasi2016man,kim2018tcav,ravfogel2020null}.  The
overparameterized zero-loss manifold has dimension $\geq d - n > 0$,
so infinitely many weight configurations yield identical predictions
but geometrically distinct representations.

\paragraph{Proof.}
Same four-step structure.  Lean:
\texttt{concept\_impossibility} in \texttt{ConceptInstance.lean}.
