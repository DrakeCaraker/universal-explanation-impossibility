The proof immediately yields a quantitative version.
For each Rashomon pair~$(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, the four-step chain
forces at least one failure: unfaithfulness at~$\theta_2$,
instability on~$(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, or indecisiveness at~$\theta_1$.
Summing over the Rashomon set under a probability measure:
$\varepsilon + \delta + (1-d) \geq \mu(\text{Rashomon})$,
where $\varepsilon$~is the unfaithfulness rate, $\delta$~the
instability rate, $d$~the decisiveness rate, and
$\mu$~the Rashomon set measure.
This tradeoff surface---which includes the binary impossibility
as the special case $\varepsilon = \delta = 0$---is developed
with domain-specific bounds in forthcoming work.
