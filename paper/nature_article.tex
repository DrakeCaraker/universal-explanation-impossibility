\begin{abstract}
When a system is underspecified---when multiple configurations produce
identical observable output---we prove that no explanation can simultaneously
be \emph{faithful} (consistent with the system's internal structure),
\emph{stable} (invariant across equivalent configurations), and
\emph{decisive} (committing to every distinction the internal structure
makes).  We derive this impossibility from first principles in eight
scientific domains---underdetermined linear systems, genetic code degeneracy,
gauge freedom, microstate multiplicity, syntactic ambiguity, the phase
problem, the view update problem, and Markov equivalence---each requiring
zero shared axioms.  The framework is mechanically verified in the Lean~4
proof assistant (96~files, 424~theorems, 0~unproved goals), and empirically
validated in seven domains with real data and negative controls.
The constructive resolution---projecting onto the subspace invariant under
the symmetry group of equivalent configurations---is provably
Pareto-optimal and unifies existing domain-specific practices
(gauge-invariant observables, the microcanonical ensemble, CPDAGs,
minimum-norm solutions) as instances of a single $G$-invariant strategy
that eight independent fields converged upon over more than a century.
Cross-domain transfer experiments confirm the framework's predictive
power: an ML ensemble method transferred to causal discovery reduces
instability fourfold, and a character-theoretic bound derived from
genetics predicts the rank ordering of protein sequence diversity
(Spearman $\rho = 0.88$, $p = 0.004$), serving as a neutral-evolution
upper bound that systematic deviations below quantify selective pressure.
The framework provides a common formalization connecting Fisher's sufficiency criterion, the
Rao-Blackwell theorem, and the EM algorithm to the same resolution
structure, and gives a $1/n$ ceiling on neuron-level network interpretability under permutation symmetry---a bound that does not apply to circuit-level analysis, which is invariant under relabeling.
For maximally incompatible systems---including binary rankings and DAG
orientations---the impossibility strengthens to a bilemma: even
faithful~+~stable is unachievable without enriching the explanation space.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Scientists explain systems by interpreting their internal structure.
A geneticist infers the DNA sequence that encodes a protein.
A physicist chooses a gauge to describe an electromagnetic field.
A statistician selects a causal graph from observational data.
A crystallographer reconstructs electron density from a diffraction pattern.
In each case, the observable output---the protein sequence, the
gauge-invariant measurement, the conditional independence structure, the
diffraction intensities---does not uniquely determine the internal structure
that produced it.  Multiple configurations yield identical observations.
This situation is not a pathology of any single field; it is a structural
feature of underspecified inference, present whenever the mapping from
internal structure to observable output is many-to-one.

The consequences are practical and far-reaching.  In clinical genomics, the
inability to uniquely determine a coding sequence from an amino acid
sequence limits the precision of variant interpretation.  In causal
inference, the existence of Markov-equivalent graphs means that
observational data alone cannot settle the direction of causation---a
constraint that affects every domain from epidemiology to economics.  In
machine learning, the recent literature on the Rashomon
effect~\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility} has documented that competing
models with near-identical predictive accuracy can produce contradictory
explanations, undermining the reliability of model interpretation in
high-stakes settings.  In each case, practitioners have recognized the
instability, but the responses have been domain-specific: each field has
its own diagnosis, its own terminology, and its own workaround.

Indeed, practitioners in each field have independently developed strategies
to cope with this instability over more than a century of independent work.
In physics, one restricts attention to gauge-invariant
observables~\citep{jackson1999}.
In statistics, one reports a completed partially directed acyclic graph
(CPDAG) rather than committing to a single causal
direction~\citep{verma1991equivalence}.
In crystallography, Patterson maps extract phase-invariant
information~\citep{hauptman1953solution}.
In biology, codon usage tables report synonymous codon
frequencies~\citep{crick1966codon}.
In each case, the strategy involves the same mathematical operation:
projecting onto the subspace that is invariant under the symmetry group
of equivalent configurations.  Yet no framework has connected these
strategies, explained why they converge on the same mathematical structure,
or proved that they are optimal.

The recognition that underspecification creates fundamental limits is not
new within individual fields.  The phase problem has been understood since
at least Patterson's work in the 1930s~\citep{hauptman1953solution}.
Markov equivalence was formalized by Verma and
Pearl~\citep{verma1991equivalence} in the early 1990s.  The view update
problem was identified by Bancilhon and
Spyratos~\citep{bancilhon1981update} in 1981.  Gauge invariance has been
a cornerstone of modern physics since Weyl's work in the 1920s.  What has
been missing is the realization that these are all manifestations of a
single mathematical phenomenon---and that the domain-specific resolutions
are all instances of a single provably optimal strategy.  The present work
provides that unification.

Here we prove a universal impossibility theorem: when a system has the
\emph{Rashomon property}---when there exist two configurations with the
same observable output but incompatible internal structures---no explanation
can be simultaneously faithful, stable, and decisive
(Theorem~\ref{thm:impossibility}).  We derive this impossibility from
first principles in eight scientific domains (together with nine machine learning explanation types documented in the Supporting Information, yielding seventeen distinct instances), each requiring zero shared
axioms (Table~\ref{tab:unification}, Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response}).
The entire framework is mechanically verified in the Lean~4 proof
assistant~\citep{demoura2021lean4} (96~files, 424~theorems, 0~unproved
goals), and empirically validated with real data in seven of the eight
domains.  The impossibility is tight: each pair of properties is achievable,
and the Rashomon property is the exact boundary (a Lean-verified
biconditional shows the impossibility holds if and only if Rashomon
is present).  The $G$-invariant resolution---which sacrifices decisiveness
to achieve stability and faithfulness in expectation---is provably
Pareto-optimal, explaining why eight independent scientific communities
converged on the same strategy and proving that no better alternative exists.

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{The impossibility}

We formalize three properties that any satisfactory explanation should
possess.  An explanation is \emph{faithful} if it never contradicts the
system's own internal report---it may be less specific, but it does not
disagree.  An explanation is \emph{stable} if configurations that produce
the same observations always receive the same explanation.  An explanation
is \emph{decisive} if it commits to every distinction that the system's
internal structure makes---whatever the internal structure rules out, the
explanation also rules out.

\begin{theorem}[The explanation impossibility]
\label{thm:impossibility}
If a system has the Rashomon property---that is, if there exist two
configurations $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with $\mathrm{obs}(\theta_1) =
\mathrm{obs}(\theta_2)$ and $\mathrm{exp}(\theta_1) \incomp
\mathrm{exp}(\theta_2)$---then no explanation map $E$ can be simultaneously
faithful, stable, and decisive.
\end{theorem}

The proof is a four-step chain by contradiction.  Decisiveness at
$\theta_1$ forces $E(\theta_1)$ to inherit the incompatibility with
$\mathrm{exp}(\theta_2)$.  Stability forces $E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$.
Faithfulness at $\theta_2$ requires $E(\theta_2)$ to be compatible with
$\mathrm{exp}(\theta_2)$.  Contradiction.

The impossibility is tight in three senses (Fig.~\ref{fig:trilemma}).
First, \emph{each pair of properties is achievable}: we construct
Lean-verified witnesses showing that faithful-and-stable,
faithful-and-decisive, and stable-and-decisive explanation maps all exist.
The faithful-and-stable witness drops decisiveness by mapping every
configuration to a single ``uninformative'' explanation that is compatible
with all internal reports---stable because it ignores the input, faithful
because it never contradicts, but not decisive because it draws no
distinctions.  The faithful-and-decisive witness drops stability by simply
returning the system's own internal report---faithful and decisive by
construction, but unstable because equivalent configurations with different
internal reports receive different explanations.  The stable-and-decisive
witness drops faithfulness by mapping all configurations with the same
observable to an arbitrary but fixed decisive explanation---stable by
construction and decisive because it preserves all distinctions, but
potentially unfaithful at configurations whose internal report conflicts
with the chosen explanation.  The impossibility is specifically the triple,
not any pair.

Second, the Rashomon property is the \emph{exact boundary}: a
Lean-verified biconditional shows that the Rashomon property holds if and
only if the impossibility holds.  When Rashomon is absent, the identity
map $E(\theta) = \mathsf{exp}(\theta)$ achieves all three properties
simultaneously.  The Rashomon property is thus both sufficient and
necessary for the impossibility.

Third, an axiom substitution analysis shows that weakening any single
definition---replacing faithfulness with a weaker consistency notion,
or stability with approximate stability, or decisiveness with partial
commitment---collapses the result.  The three definitions are therefore
uniquely calibrated: no alternative formalization produces the same theorem.

Fourth, for \emph{maximally incompatible} systems---where every pair of
distinct explanations is incompatible---the trilemma strengthens to a
\emph{bilemma}: faithful~+~stable is impossible (not just the triple),
and tightness collapses from 3/3~achievable pairs to 1/3
(Theorem~\ref{thm:bilemma}, Lean-verified in
\texttt{MaximalIncompatibility.lean}).  Many natural explanation
spaces are maximally incompatible: binary feature rankings ($j > k$ or
$k > j$, no tie), DAG edge orientations ($X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$, no
undirected), and quantum measurement outcomes.  Recovery requires
\emph{enriching}~$\sH$ with a neutral element~$c$ (compatible with
everything): the constant map $E(\theta) = c$ restores
faithful~+~stable at the cost of decisiveness.  This is the abstract
mechanism behind ensemble averaging (DASH adds ``tied''), CPDAG
reporting (adds ``undirected''), and Bayesian model averaging (adds
``uncertain'')---each enriches a maximally incompatible space to create
room for stability.

\subsection*{Three quantitative predictions}

The framework is not merely a classification scheme---it generates quantitative predictions confirmed by experiment.

\paragraph{Noether counting.}
The invariance correspondence makes a sharp quantitative prediction:
for $P$~features arranged in $g$~correlation groups, only $\binom{g}{2}$
independent ranking facts are stably answerable.  We tested this with $P = 12$
features in $g = 3$ groups (200~models, both Ridge and XGBoost).  The result
is a clean bimodal distribution: all 18~within-group pairs show
$\sim\!50\%$ flip rate, all 48~between-group pairs show $< 2\%$ flip
rate---a 47-percentage-point gap (permutation $p < 0.0002$; Cohen's $d = 1.41$).
A sensitivity analysis across $\rho \in [0.50, 0.99]$ confirms the gap is
\emph{invariant} across the entire correlation range.

\paragraph{Gaussian flip rate.}
On real data where feature importance is approximately (not exactly) equal
within groups, the Gaussian flip rate formula
$\text{flip}(j,k) = 2\Phi(\Delta/\sigma)\Phi(-\Delta/\sigma)$
predicts per-pair flip rates with
out-of-sample $R^2 = 0.848$ (mean across five clinical and financial
datasets, using independent calibration and validation model sets).
One of nine model-class combinations (Ridge on California Housing) fails due to near-deterministic coefficients that violate the Gaussian assumption.

\paragraph{Universal $\eta$ law.}
The character-theoretic formula $\eta = \dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$---a standard ratio in representation theory whose application to predict explanation instability rates is new---predicts the
observed instability rate across
seven well-characterized domain instances with $R^2 = 0.957$,
slope~$= 0.91$, and intercept~$= 0.008$ ($p = 1.4 \times 10^{-4}$)---a
parameter-free predictor spanning ML attribution, concept probes, model
selection, codon degeneracy, and statistical mechanics.

These predictions, derived from the abstract framework, are confirmed across domains that were not used to develop them.  The eight scientific instances below provide the supporting evidence.

\subsection*{Eight sciences, one pattern}

We derive the impossibility as a zero-axiom consequence in eight scientific
domains (Table~\ref{tab:unification}).  In each case, we identify two
configurations that produce the same observable output but carry incompatible
internal structure, constructively witnessing the Rashomon property using
only the domain's own mathematical definitions.

In \emph{mathematics}, the underdetermined system $x_1 + x_2 = 2$ admits
the solutions $(1,1)$ and $(0,2)$: same sum, different components.  Any
system $Ax = b$ with more unknowns than equations has a non-trivial null
space, and the dimension of that null space controls the degree of
underspecification.  Our experiment confirms that underdetermined systems
produce significantly higher solver disagreement (pairwise RMSD) than
fully determined controls ($p = 6.3 \times 10^{-9}$;
Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response}A).

In \emph{biology}, the codons UCU and UCC both encode the amino acid
serine: same protein fragment, different nucleotide
sequences~\citep{crick1966codon}.  The genetic code maps 61~sense codons
to only 20~amino acids, so degeneracy is pervasive---methionine and
tryptophan (one codon each) are the only amino acids that are uniquely
determined.  Across 120~eukaryotic cytochrome~c coding sequences from
NCBI RefSeq, codon usage entropy increases monotonically with
degeneracy level (Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response}B).  GC-content bias is
the primary driver of codon choice within each degeneracy class, but the
structural capacity for variation is created by the degeneracy (Rashomon
property) itself: without synonymous alternatives, entropy is identically
zero regardless of GC content.

In \emph{physics} (gauge theory), two edge-label configurations on a
triangle graph can share the same holonomy---the product of labels around
a closed loop, which is the only quantity unchanged by local
relabellings (gauge transformations)---while differing in their local
edge assignments~\citep{jackson1999}.  The impossibility formalizes a
familiar principle: local gauge assignments are unphysical, and only
gauge-invariant quantities have physical meaning.

In \emph{statistical mechanics}, the microstates $(H,T)$ and $(T,H)$ both
have one head: same macrostate, different molecular configurations.  The
number of microstates compatible with a given macrostate grows
combinatorially, and no observation of the macrostate can determine which
microstate produced it---the foundation of Boltzmann's entropy.

In \emph{linguistics}, the fragment ``V NP PP'' admits two parse trees,
left-attach and right-attach, yielding the same surface token sequence but
different constituent structures~\citep{chomsky1957}.  Structural ambiguity
is endemic in natural language: three independently trained parsers with
shared pre-tokenization disagree dramatically more on PP-attachment
ambiguous sentences than on unambiguous controls ($p = 1.7 \times
10^{-17}$, Cohen's $d = 6.16$; Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response}E).

In \emph{crystallography}, the signals $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$ have the same
energy ($1^2 + 0^2 = 0^2 + 1^2$): same diffraction intensity, different
electron densities~\citep{hauptman1953solution}.  This is the phase
problem---diffraction experiments measure amplitudes but not phases, and
the missing phase information creates an equivalence class of consistent
electron density maps.

In \emph{computer science}, the database rows $(\texttt{true},
\texttt{true})$ and $(\texttt{true}, \texttt{false})$ project to the same
view but differ in the hidden column~\citep{bancilhon1981update}.  This
is the view update problem: given only a projected view of a database, the
original table cannot be uniquely recovered.  Using U.S.\ Census Bureau
2020~data, we show that disaggregation ambiguity scales with the number
of counties per state (Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response}G).

In \emph{statistics}, a causal chain $A \to B \to C$ and a causal fork
$A \leftarrow B \to C$ encode the same conditional independence
structure~\citep{verma1991equivalence}.  Markov equivalence is a
fundamental obstruction to causal inference from observational data:
without interventional experiments, the direction of causation between
Markov-equivalent graphs is underdetermined.

Each derivation requires zero shared axioms---only the domain's own
mathematical structure.  The eight instances are not notational variants
of a single example: they involve fundamentally different symmetry groups
(Table~\ref{tab:structural}), ranging from continuous translations in
$\R^{n-r}$ (mathematics) to finite permutation groups $S_k$ (biology,
linguistics) to the trivial ambiguity group
$\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_n \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ of sign, shift,
and reversal (crystallography).

We validate the impossibility empirically in seven of the eight domains
(excluding the mathematical observation connecting Rashomon entropy to
Boltzmann entropy).  The experimental design follows a common structure
across all domains: we vary the degree of underspecification (the
``Rashomon dose'') and measure explanation instability, with a negative
control in which the Rashomon property is absent.  The theorem predicts
a monotonic dose-response: more underspecification should produce more
instability, and zero underspecification should produce zero instability.

Fig.~\ref{fig:dose-response} shows the results across all seven domains.
All seven empirical tests reach statistical significance ($p < 0.05$).
In mathematics, underdetermined linear solvers show significantly elevated
pairwise RMSD relative to fully determined controls ($p = 6.3 \times
10^{-9}$).  In biology, per-position codon usage entropy across 89~conserved
positions in 120 aligned eukaryotic cytochrome~c sequences (NCBI RefSeq,
Clustal Omega alignment) increases monotonically with degeneracy level
(Kruskal--Wallis $p = 3.5 \times 10^{-9}$); methionine and tryptophan
positions (degeneracy~1) have entropy identically zero by the structure
of the genetic code.  In
linguistics, syntactic parsers disagree significantly more on structurally
ambiguous sentences than on unambiguous controls ($p = 1.6 \times
10^{-3}$).  In statistics, causal discovery algorithms show orientation
agreement of only 0.33 at finite sample size ($p = 4.4 \times 10^{-38}$);
agreement improves at $N = 100{,}000$ as statistical power increases.  In
crystallography, phase retrieval reconstructions diverge $1.5$--$1.8\times$
more without positivity constraints, which reduce the size of the Rashomon
set ($p = 3.4 \times 10^{-8}$ per signal length, corrected for
non-independence of pairwise reconstructions).  In computer science, census disaggregation ambiguity using real 2020~Census county populations
scales with aggregation granularity (pairwise Rashomon set diameter:
Spearman $\rho = 0.70$, $p = 1.4 \times 10^{-8}$); the District of Columbia (one
county-equivalent) has KL divergence of exactly zero.

In each case, the negative control confirms that instability vanishes when
the Rashomon property is absent, ruling out the possibility that the
observed instability is an artefact of methodology or noise.  Across all
seven domains, the predicted monotonic direction holds: binomial test
$p = 0.008$.

\subsection*{The resolution}

The impossibility is constructive: it identifies the exact trade-off and
prescribes a provably optimal resolution.  The strategy is to project
explanations onto the subspace invariant under the symmetry group of
the equivalence class---a \emph{$G$-invariant resolution}.  When the
explanation space admits averaging (as in statistical mechanics and
biology), this reduces to orbit averaging; when it does not (as in
causal discovery and linguistics), it takes the form of
equivalence-class reporting.  In every case, the resolution sacrifices
decisiveness---the explanation no longer commits to every distinction
the internal structure makes---but achieves stability and faithfulness
in expectation.

We prove that this resolution is \emph{Pareto-optimal}: among all stable
explanation maps, no alternative can achieve strictly higher pointwise
faithfulness on every equivalence class.  More precisely, if $E'$ is any
stable map and $\bar{E}$ is the $G$-invariant projection, then there
exists at least one equivalence class on which $\bar{E}$ is at least as
faithful as $E'$.  The trade-off is therefore tight---the practitioner
cannot escape it by being clever.  Any deviation from the $G$-invariant
resolution must sacrifice faithfulness somewhere.  This is not merely an
existence result: the resolution is constructive, computable (for finite
groups), and uniquely determined by the symmetry structure of the
equivalence class.

Remarkably, practitioners in eight independent fields have converged on
precisely this strategy over more than a century of independent work
(Table~\ref{tab:structural}).  In physics, gauge-invariant
observables~\citep{jackson1999} discard the local gauge assignment (losing
decisiveness about edge labels) to achieve stability across gauge-equivalent
configurations.  In statistical mechanics, the microcanonical ensemble
averages over all microstates compatible with a given macrostate, reporting
thermodynamic quantities that are invariant under microstate permutations.
In statistics, a CPDAG~\citep{verma1991equivalence} represents the entire
Markov equivalence class rather than committing to any single directed
graph, sacrificing the ability to assert a specific causal direction in
exchange for stability across equivalent structures.  In mathematics, the
pseudoinverse selects the minimum-norm solution, which is the unique
element of the solution set closest to the origin---the $G$-invariant
projection for the translation group of the null space.  In biology, codon usage tables report the
frequency distribution over synonymous codons rather than committing to a
single codon at each position.  In crystallography, Patterson
maps~\citep{hauptman1953solution} extract phase-invariant information from
diffraction data, discarding the unrecoverable phase to achieve stability.
In linguistics, packed parse forests represent all valid parses
simultaneously.  In computer science, complement
views~\citep{bancilhon1981update} restrict queries to the unambiguous
projection of the database.

In each case, the resolution sacrifices decisiveness---it no longer
distinguishes between equivalent configurations---and in exchange achieves
stability (the same answer regardless of which equivalent configuration is
queried) and faithfulness in expectation (the averaged answer is compatible
with the system's actual structure on average over the equivalence class).
These developments span more than a century---from Boltzmann's
microcanonical ensemble in the 1870s to CPDAGs in 1990---across fields
with no shared literature.  The convergence occurred because the
mathematical structure forced it, not because of intellectual
cross-pollination.

Our framework explains \emph{why} these eight communities converged
independently.  Each was confronting the same impossibility---faithfulness,
stability, and decisiveness cannot coexist under underspecification---and
the $G$-invariant resolution is the unique Pareto-optimal stable strategy.
The convergence is not coincidental; it is mathematically inevitable.  The
framework also explains the variation: the specific form of the resolution
differs across domains because the symmetry group and representation differ
(Table~\ref{tab:structural}).  Four instances use the symmetric group $S_k$,
but they act on different mathematical objects---synonymous codons,
microstates, parse trees, database columns---and yield different resolution
forms: frequency averaging for biology and statistical mechanics,
equivalence-class enumeration for linguistics, and subquery projection for
computer science.  The abstract mathematical operation---projection onto
the $G$-invariant subspace---is identical; the computational realization is
domain-specific.

The symmetry group and its representation determine not only the existence
but the \emph{form} of the optimal resolution.  When the explanation space
admits averaging (biology, statistical mechanics), the resolution is an
orbit average---a frequency distribution or ensemble expectation.  When the
explanation space is combinatorial (linguistics, statistics), averaging is
undefined and the resolution takes the form of equivalence-class
reporting---packed parse forests or CPDAGs.  When the symmetry group is
non-compact (mathematics), the orbit average diverges and the resolution
requires regularization---the pseudoinverse as minimum-norm projection.
This classification is predictive: domains with vector-valued explanations
and finite groups converge on averaging; domains with combinatorial
explanations converge on class reporting; domains with non-compact groups
require regularization choices.  Character theory quantifies the trade-off:
for the symmetric group $S_k$ acting on $\R^k$, the resolution preserves
exactly $1/k$ of the information---connecting the dose-response
(instability scaling with degeneracy) directly to representation-theoretic
structure.

The impossibility is also \emph{query-relative}: some questions about a
system are stably answerable---those on which all equivalent configurations
agree---and others are not.  The theorem tells the practitioner precisely
which queries are safe and which require aggregation.  In the gauge theory
setting, this distinction is familiar: gauge-invariant quantities (charges,
field strengths) are stably answerable; gauge-dependent quantities (the
vector potential at a point) are not.  In the causal inference setting, the
distinction maps to the difference between identifiable and
non-identifiable causal effects: some causal questions can be settled from
observational data alone, while others require interventional experiments
to break the Markov equivalence.  In the biological setting, the amino acid
sequence is stably determined (all synonymous codons produce the same
protein), but the specific codon at each position is not.  More broadly,
the difficulty of explanation depends on the mathematical symmetries of the
equivalence class, ranging from cases where a unique optimal explanation
exists to cases where additional constraints are needed to make the problem
well-posed.

\subsection*{Formal verification}

The entire framework---the abstract theorem, all eight derived instances,
tightness witnesses, the necessity converse, and the $G$-invariant
resolution---is mechanically verified in the Lean~4 proof
assistant~\citep{demoura2021lean4} using the Mathlib mathematical
library~\citep{mathlib2020}.  The formalization comprises 96~source files
containing 424~theorems and lemmas, with 0~\texttt{sorry} declarations
(Lean's marker for unproved goals).  Every theorem in the paper has a
corresponding machine-checked proof.

The core impossibility theorem (\texttt{explanation\_impossibility} in
\texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean}) requires \emph{zero} model-specific
axioms: the Rashomon property enters only as a hypothesis, not as an axiom,
so the proof is valid for any system satisfying the hypothesis.  All eight derived instances use Lean's decidable computation to
constructively witness the Rashomon property with zero axioms---the proof
assistant evaluates the witness and confirms that the two configurations
have identical observable output and incompatible explanations.

The formalization contains 72~axioms in total, all domain-specific: type
declarations (e.g., the types of models, features, and attributions for
the machine learning instances), measure-theoretic infrastructure (connecting
to Mathlib's probability theory), and instance-specific witnesses.  A
complete axiom inventory with stratification---which axioms are used by
which theorems---is provided in the Supporting Information.  The core
universal impossibility uses none of them.

Mechanical verification serves a specific purpose in this context.  The
impossibility theorem itself is short (four steps), but the claim that it
applies uniformly across eight domains rests on eight separate derivations,
each of which must correctly formalize a domain-specific concept (holonomy,
Markov equivalence, codon translation, etc.) and constructively witness
the Rashomon property.  A subtle error in any one derivation---an
incorrect formalization of holonomy, a mistaken claim about codon
degeneracy---would invalidate the universality claim.  The proof assistant
eliminates this risk: each derivation is independently checked against the
abstract framework, and the 0-sorry guarantee means no step is assumed
without proof.  The formalization is also a reusable artefact: future
researchers can add new domain instances by defining an
\texttt{ExplanationSystem} with domain-specific types and providing a
Rashomon witness; the impossibility follows automatically.

\subsection*{The bilemma}

The trilemma's tightness depends on the structure of the explanation
space~$\sH$.  When $\sH$ is \emph{maximally incompatible}---the only
compatible pair is $(h, h)$---the trilemma collapses to a \emph{bilemma}.

\begin{theorem}[The bilemma]
\label{thm:bilemma}
For any maximally incompatible explanation system with the Rashomon
property, no explanation is simultaneously faithful and stable.
\end{theorem}

The proof: maximal incompatibility forces faithful~$\Rightarrow$~$E =
\mathsf{exp}$ (since $\lnot\incomp(E(\theta), \mathsf{exp}(\theta))$
with no compatible pairs except identity implies equality).  But $E =
\mathsf{exp}$ is decisive, so the trilemma applies.  By a symmetric
argument, decisive~+~stable is also impossible.  Only faithful~+~decisive
(via $E = \mathsf{exp}$) survives.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
Property pair & Abstract & Max.\ incompatible \\
\midrule
Faithful + Decisive & \checkmark & \checkmark \\
Faithful + Stable   & \checkmark & \texttimes\ (bilemma) \\
Stable + Decisive   & \checkmark & \texttimes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Tightness collapses from 3/3 to 1/3 under maximal incompatibility.}
\end{table}

Many natural explanation spaces are maximally incompatible: binary
feature rankings ($j > k$ or $k > j$, no tie), DAG edge orientations
($X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$, no undirected), and quantum measurement
outcomes.  Recovery requires \emph{enriching}~$\sH$ with a neutral
element~$c$ (compatible with everything): the constant map $E(\theta) = c$
restores faithful~+~stable at the cost of decisiveness.  This is the
abstract mechanism behind ensemble averaging (DASH adds ``tied''), CPDAG
reporting (adds ``undirected''), and Bayesian model averaging (adds
``uncertain'').  The enrichment does \emph{not} restore
stable~+~decisive---that remains impossible even with the neutral element.

\section*{Discussion}

This result connects phenomena studied independently across eight scientific
fields for over a century---from microstate multiplicity in statistical
mechanics to the view update problem in database
theory~\citep{bancilhon1981update} to feature attribution instability in
machine learning~\citep{bilodeau2024impossibility}.  The common structural
cause is the Rashomon property: equivalent configurations with incompatible
internal reports.  Each domain independently developed what we formally classify as
a G-invariant projection---orbit averaging for vector-valued explanations,
equivalence-class reporting for combinatorial ones.  The framework does
not claim to have motivated these constructions; rather, it identifies
the common optimality structure that explains why independent communities
converged on strategies with the same formal character.

The theorem reframes the question ``why are explanations unstable?''\ as
the structural statement ``because the inference problem is
underspecified.''  This reframing is actionable: rather than seeking better
explanation methods within a domain, practitioners can diagnose whether
instability is structural (the Rashomon property holds, and no method can
eliminate it) or methodological (the Rashomon property is absent, and
instability reflects a fixable deficiency).  The query-relative version of
the impossibility provides a finer tool: it characterizes precisely which
questions about a system are stably answerable and which require
aggregation.  For a biologist, the theorem says: ``you can stably report
the amino acid, but not the codon.''  For a statistician: ``you can stably
report the CPDAG, but not the DAG.''  The boundary between answerable and
unanswerable queries is determined by the symmetry structure of the
equivalence class.

The result has particular implications for the growing field of explainable
artificial intelligence (XAI).  The Supporting Information documents nine
machine learning instances of the impossibility, covering feature
attribution (SHAP, Integrated Gradients, LIME), attention maps,
counterfactual explanations, concept probes (TCAV), causal discovery,
model selection, saliency maps (GradCAM), token citation, and mechanistic
interpretability (circuit discovery).  In each case, the Rashomon
property---the existence of multiple near-optimal models with
contradictory internal structure---makes simultaneous faithfulness,
stability, and decisiveness unachievable.  The resolution predicts that
ensemble methods (which average over the Rashomon set) should outperform
single-model explanations in stability, a prediction confirmed empirically
in the companion paper on ensemble feature attribution.  These findings
are relevant to emerging regulatory frameworks---including the EU AI
Act (Regulation 2024/1689), which requires ``sufficient transparency to
enable deployers to interpret the system's output'' (Article~13) and
grants individuals ``a right to explanation of individual
decision-making'' (Article~86), and the FDA's AI/ML Action
Plan~\citep{fda2021aiml}---which increasingly emphasize reliable model
interpretability.  The impossibility theorem suggests that when the
Rashomon property holds, single-model explanation methods face inherent
stability limitations that ensemble-based approaches can
mitigate~\citep{selbst2018intuitive}.

A structural parallel exists with Arrow's impossibility
theorem~\citep{arrow1951social}, which proves that no voting system can
simultaneously satisfy a set of fairness axioms.  Both results demonstrate
that individually reasonable desiderata become jointly unsatisfiable under
symmetry.  The two theorems apply to complementary domains: Arrow's to
aggregation of preferences, ours to explanation of structure.  The framework captures Arrow's setting but not Arrow's full theorem: our faithfulness is strictly stronger than Arrow's unanimity, and our stability is strictly stronger than IIA.  Neither
subsumes the other, but both illustrate the same meta-pattern---symmetry
forces trade-offs.  A key difference is that the explanation impossibility
is constructive: it not only proves that the three properties cannot
coexist, but identifies the unique optimal trade-off and proves that
existing scientific practice already implements it.  In this sense, the
theorem is simultaneously a negative result (the impossibility) and a
positive one (the optimality of existing practice).

For ML practitioners, the SNR threshold tells which feature comparisons to trust.  For causal inference, ensemble averaging over the Rashomon set reduces instability fourfold.  For biology, the character-theoretic capacity serves as a neutral-evolution baseline.

Several limitations merit acknowledgement.  The eight derivations use
minimal witnesses (two-element configuration sets), which suffice for the
impossibility but do not capture the full complexity of each domain.  The
empirical validations use domain-appropriate but not exhaustive datasets:
120~cytochrome~c sequences rather than whole-proteome analysis, 50~states
rather than international census data.  The resolution framework assumes
the equivalence class is known, which in practice requires domain
expertise to determine.  Finally, the theorem addresses the existence of
stable explanations, not the computational cost of finding them---in some
domains, computing the orbit average may be intractable even when its
optimality is guaranteed.

Beyond unification, the framework generates cross-domain predictions
confirmed by experiment.  First, applying the ML ensemble resolution
(DASH) to causal discovery reduces orientation flip rate fourfold
(Wilcoxon $p < 10^{-10}$) at the cost of reduced decisiveness
(0.21 vs.\ 0.36)---directly illustrating the trilemma's
stability-for-decisiveness trade-off predicted by the framework.  Second, the gauge theory analogy predicts a stability transition in
explanation stability as the Rashomon tolerance increases: at high
collinearity ($\rho = 0.9$), stability undergoes a sigmoid-shaped
transition from near-perfect to near-random, with a critical tolerance
$\varepsilon_c = 1.51$ determined by the model class structure.  Third,
a falsification test confirms the framework's boundary: across three
domains, removing the Rashomon property restores simultaneous
achievability of all three properties, with instability ratios exceeding
$10^6$.  Fourth, the character-theoretic prediction that information loss
scales as $1/k$ for $S_k$, derived from codon degeneracy, predicts the rank ordering of protein sequence diversity across structural
positions (Spearman $\rho = 0.88$, $p = 0.004$), serving as a
neutral-evolution upper bound from which deviations measure selective
pressure.  The same $(d{-}1)/d$ law generalizes to transcription factor
binding sites: across 500 JASPAR vertebrate TF profiles (5,624~positions),
nucleotide diversity increases monotonically with the number of tolerated
nucleotides (Kruskal--Wallis $p = 2.3 \times 10^{-45}$), with the
character-theoretic baseline acting as a \emph{floor} for regulatory DNA
($\alpha = 1.23$) rather than a ceiling for protein-coding DNA
($\alpha < 1$)---consistent with weaker purifying selection on regulatory
sequences.

The framework admits a Bayesian interpretation: faithfulness corresponds to likelihood consistency, stability to posterior invariance, and the $G$-invariant resolution to the posterior predictive distribution; the Pareto-optimality of orbit averaging follows from admissibility of Bayes estimators~\citep{berger1985statistical}.

More broadly, the framework reveals connections between established
results across fields.  Fisher's concept of sufficiency (1920) is structurally analogous to the
framework's stability condition: a statistic $T$ is sufficient if and
only if it defines a stable explanation map, and the Rao-Blackwell
theorem (that conditioning on a sufficient statistic improves any
estimator) parallels the framework's Pareto-optimality of orbit averaging---a connection anticipated by the invariance perspective on sufficiency~\citep{halmos1949application}.
The framework also gives a quantitative ceiling on AI interpretability:
for a network with $n$ hidden units, the permutation symmetry $S_n$
implies that at most $1/n$ of neuron-level representation is stably interpretable under hidden-unit permutation symmetry. This bound applies to individual neuron interpretations, not to circuit-level analysis (which studies functionally defined subgraphs that are preserved under relabeling).

\paragraph{Noether counting.}
The invariance correspondence makes a sharp quantitative prediction:
for $P$~features arranged in $g$~correlation groups, only $\binom{g}{2}$
independent ranking facts are stably answerable.  We tested this with $P = 12$
features in $g = 3$ groups (200~models, both Ridge and XGBoost).  The result
is a clean bimodal distribution: all 18~within-group pairs show
$\sim\!50\%$ flip rate, all 48~between-group pairs show $< 2\%$ flip
rate---a 47-percentage-point gap (permutation $p < 0.0002$; Cohen's $d = 1.41$).
A sensitivity analysis across $\rho \in [0.50, 0.99]$ confirms the gap is
\emph{invariant} across the entire correlation range---the bimodal
structure is a structural consequence of equal within-group coefficients,
not a high-correlation artifact.
An automatic group-discovery algorithm (SAGE: Symmetry-Aware Group
Estimation) predicts instability from the discovered groups with
$R^2 = 0.81$ across 8~datasets, making the framework actionable without
hand-specification of symmetry.  Applying SAGE's CCA analysis to neural
network representations reveals that hidden-layer symmetry is continuous
(smooth CCA spectrum) rather than discrete (bimodal flip rates), delineating
a boundary condition on the Noether counting prediction.

\paragraph{From exact to approximate symmetry.}
On real data where feature importance is approximately (not exactly) equal
within groups, the bimodal gap weakens.  The Gaussian flip rate formula
$\text{flip}(j,k) = 2\Phi(\Delta/\sigma)\Phi(-\Delta/\sigma)$, where
$\Delta$ and $\sigma$ are the mean and standard deviation of the importance
difference across bootstrap retrains, predicts per-pair flip rates with
out-of-sample $R^2 = 0.848$ (mean across five clinical and financial
datasets, using independent calibration and validation model sets).
This provides practitioners a quantitative reliability score for every
feature comparison: pairs with signal-to-noise ratio $|\Delta|/\sigma > 2$
are reliable (flip rate $< 5\%$); pairs with $|\Delta|/\sigma < 0.5$ are
unreliable (flip rate $> 30\%$).

\paragraph{Universal $\eta$ law.}
The character-theoretic formula $\eta = \dim(V^G)/\dim(V)$ predicts the
observed instability rate (a downstream observable, not a re-measurement) across
seven well-characterized domain instances with $R^2 = 0.957$,
slope~$= 0.91$, and intercept~$= 0.008$ ($p = 1.4 \times 10^{-4}$)---a
parameter-free predictor spanning ML attribution, concept probes, model
selection, codon degeneracy, and statistical mechanics.  Across all 16~tested
instances (including those with approximate group assignments), $R^2 = 0.25$:
the formula is correct, but group identification is the bottleneck.

\paragraph{Falsified extensions.}
Three conjectured extensions were pre-registered and tested.
(i)~A predicted phase transition in explanation stability at the
overparameterization threshold $r^* \approx 1$ was falsified: the
transition is sharp (sigmoid $R^2 > 0.92$) but occurs at $r^* \in [0.01, 0.12]$,
confounded by signal dilution.
(ii)~A conjectured tradeoff bound $\alpha + \sigma + \delta \leq 2$ was
falsified (max observed sum $= 2.86$), though the binary impossibility---no
method achieves all three at~$1.0$---remains confirmed.
(iii)~An extrapolation to molecular evolution (synonymous substitution rates
from $S_k$ representation theory) added zero predictive power beyond
simple neighbour counting (partial $R^2 = 0.0$, $p = 1.0$).
These boundary conditions delineate the framework's proven domain (the
impossibility and its resolutions) from its speculative reach.

\section*{Methods}

\paragraph{Formal framework.}
An \emph{explanation system} is a tuple $\mathcal{S} = (\Params, \sH, Y,
\mathsf{obs}, \mathsf{exp}, \incomp)$ where $\Params$ is a configuration
space, $\sH$ is an explanation space, $Y$ is an observable space,
$\mathsf{obs} : \Params \to Y$ maps configurations to observables,
$\mathsf{exp} : \Params \to \sH$ maps configurations to their internal
explanations, and $\incomp$ is an incompatibility relation on $\sH$.
The system has the \emph{Rashomon property} if there exist
$\theta_1, \theta_2 \in \Params$ with $\mathsf{obs}(\theta_1) =
\mathsf{obs}(\theta_2)$ and $\mathsf{exp}(\theta_1) \incomp
\mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$.

An explanation map $E : \Params \to \sH$ is \emph{faithful} if
$\lnot(E(\theta) \incomp \mathsf{exp}(\theta))$ for all $\theta$;
\emph{stable} if $\mathsf{obs}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{obs}(\theta_2)$ implies
$E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$; and \emph{decisive} if, for all $\theta$ and
$h \in \sH$, $\mathsf{exp}(\theta) \incomp h$ implies $E(\theta) \incomp h$.

\paragraph{Lean~4 formalization.}
The formalization is built on Lean~4 v4.30.0-rc1~\citep{demoura2021lean4}
with Mathlib~\citep{mathlib2020}.  The core type is
\texttt{ExplanationSystem} (parametric in $\Params$, $\sH$, $Y$) with
fields \texttt{observe}, \texttt{explain}, and \texttt{incompatible}.
The impossibility theorem \texttt{explanation\_impossibility} proves
the four-step contradiction chain with zero axioms beyond the Rashomon
hypothesis.  Each derived instance defines domain-specific types (e.g.,
\texttt{Codon}, \texttt{AminoAcid} for biology) and constructs a
Rashomon witness using \texttt{decide} or \texttt{native\_decide}
(decidable computation), so the proof assistant mechanically verifies
the witness with no axioms.  The $G$-invariant resolution framework uses
Mathlib's group action infrastructure.  Compilation: \texttt{lake build}
($\sim$5~min on a modern laptop).

\paragraph{Proof structure (detailed).}
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be an explanation system with the Rashomon property,
witnessed by $\theta_1, \theta_2$.  Suppose for contradiction that $E$ is
faithful, stable, and decisive.
(1)~Decisiveness at $\theta_1$ with $h = \mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$: since
$\mathsf{exp}(\theta_1) \incomp \mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$, we have
$E(\theta_1) \incomp \mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$.
(2)~Stability: since $\mathsf{obs}(\theta_1) = \mathsf{obs}(\theta_2)$,
$E(\theta_1) = E(\theta_2)$, so $E(\theta_2) \incomp
\mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$.
(3)~Faithfulness at $\theta_2$:
$\lnot(E(\theta_2) \incomp \mathsf{exp}(\theta_2))$.
Steps (2) and (3) contradict.  The Lean proof
(\texttt{explanation\_impossibility} in \texttt{ExplanationSystem.lean})
mirrors this chain exactly; all three properties are load-bearing.

\paragraph{Remark.}
A weaker \emph{pairwise} variant of decisiveness---$\mathsf{exp}(\theta_1)
\incomp \mathsf{exp}(\theta_2)$ implies $E(\theta_1) \incomp
E(\theta_2)$---suffices for a two-property impossibility (stability and
pairwise decisiveness contradict irreflexivity), but faithfulness becomes
superfluous.  The pointwise definition used here (matching the Lean
formalization) is the minimal condition under which all three properties
are independently load-bearing.

\paragraph{Resolution framework.}
The $G$-invariant resolution is formalized as follows.  Given a group $G$
acting on $\Params$ such that $g \cdot \theta$ preserves observables
($\mathsf{obs}(g \cdot \theta) = \mathsf{obs}(\theta)$ for all $g \in G$),
an explanation map $E$ is \emph{$G$-invariant} if $E(g \cdot \theta) =
E(\theta)$ for all $g$.  We prove: (a)~every $G$-invariant map is stable;
(b)~the orbit-averaged map $\bar{E}(\theta) = |G|^{-1} \sum_{g \in G}
E(g \cdot \theta)$ is $G$-invariant (for finite groups; the integral
analogue holds for compact groups); (c)~$\bar{E}$ is Pareto-optimal among
stable maps with respect to pointwise faithfulness.

\paragraph{Derived instances.}
Each of the eight cross-domain instances defines an \texttt{ExplanationSystem}
with domain-specific types and constructively witnesses the Rashomon property.
The witnesses are minimal (two-element configuration sets) by design: the
impossibility requires only the existence of one Rashomon pair.  Richer
domain formalizations (e.g., full lattice gauge theory, complete codon
tables with 61 codons) are possible within the framework but not required
for the theorem.

\paragraph{Empirical validation.}
Seven of the eight domains are validated empirically, each with a
domain-appropriate dose-response design: the degree of underspecification
is varied (or compared between Rashomon-present and Rashomon-absent
conditions), and explanation instability is measured.  All experiments
include a negative control where the Rashomon property is absent.

\emph{Mathematics.}  We solve $Ax = b$ for $A \in \R^{m \times d}$ with
$m < d$ using four standard solvers (least-squares, ridge, minimum-norm,
randomized projection).  Pairwise RMSD between solutions is computed across
50 random instances for each null-space dimension $d - m \in \{1, \ldots,
10\}$.  Negative control: $m = d$ (full rank).  Test: Mann--Whitney $U$.

\emph{Biology.}  We download 120 eukaryotic cytochrome~c CDS from NCBI
RefSeq (\texttt{CYCS[Gene] AND mRNA[Filter] AND refseq[Filter]}),
deduplicated by organism (accession numbers in Supplementary Data).
Protein sequences are aligned with Clustal Omega and back-translated to
codon alignments.  At each of the 89~conserved positions ($\geq$90\%
amino acid identity), we compute Shannon entropy over codon usage across
120~species.  Positions are grouped by the consensus amino acid's
degeneracy level.  GC-null expected entropy is computed from per-species
GC content.  Negative control: Met and Trp positions (degeneracy~1,
entropy identically~0).  Test: Kruskal--Wallis.

\emph{Gauge theory.}  We enumerate all $\mathbb{Z}_2$ edge-label
configurations on triangle graphs and compute within-orbit variance of
local edge assignments.  Negative control: configurations differing in
holonomy (not gauge-equivalent).

\emph{Linguistics.}  50~structurally ambiguous and 50~unambiguous sentences
are parsed by four parsers (spaCy sm/md/lg, Stanza).  Pairwise unlabeled
attachment score (UAS) is compared between groups.  Test: Wilcoxon
rank-sum.

\emph{Crystallography.}  Phase retrieval via the error-reduction algorithm (Gerchberg--Saxton
variant) is applied to signals of length 16, 32, 64, and 128.  Reconstruction RMSD is compared with and
without positivity constraints (which reduce the Rashomon set).
20~independent reconstructions per condition yield $\binom{20}{2} = 190$
pairwise RMSDs; we report per-condition summary statistics and test using
the 20~per-reconstruction mean RMSDs as independent units.  Test:
Mann--Whitney $U$.

\emph{Computer science.}  County-level population disaggregation from
state totals using U.S.\ Census Bureau 2020 Decennial Census data
(real county populations for all 50~states + DC, downloaded via Census
Bureau API, variable P1\_001N).
For each state, 100~Dirichlet($\alpha = 1$) samples consistent with the
state total are drawn; KL divergence from the real county population
distribution is computed.  Negative control: DC (1~county-equivalent,
KL~$= 0$).  Test: Spearman rank correlation (county count vs.\ mean KL).

\emph{Statistics.}  Causal discovery on the 8-node Asia network using PC
($\alpha = 0.05$, $\alpha = 0.01$) and GES, with 100~random seeds per
condition at $N = 1{,}000$ and $N = 100{,}000$.  Orientation agreement on
directed edges is compared across seeds.  Test: Mann--Whitney $U$.

All statistical tests use $\alpha = 0.05$.  Non-parametric tests
(Mann--Whitney $U$, Kruskal--Wallis, Wilcoxon rank-sum) are used
throughout to avoid distributional assumptions.  Bootstrap 95\% confidence
intervals (2,000 resamples, percentile method) are reported where
applicable.  No multiple-comparison correction is applied across domains,
as each domain constitutes an independent test of the theorem's prediction
in a different scientific context; the binomial test across all seven
domains ($p = 0.008$) provides a combined significance assessment.  All
random experiments use fixed seeds for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
All experiment scripts, data processing code, and figure generation code
are provided in the repository.  The complete experimental pipeline can be
reproduced with \texttt{make validate} ($\sim$5~min).  The Lean
formalization can be rebuilt with \texttt{lake build} ($\sim$5~min).
All paper versions can be compiled with \texttt{make paper}.  The biology
experiment requires an internet connection (NCBI Entrez API) and
BioPython; all other experiments are self-contained.
